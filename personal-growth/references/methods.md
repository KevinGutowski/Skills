## Personal operating profile

Meg Lewis's public Full Time You framework contributes a fit-oriented intake: values, personality, energizing abilities, draining abilities, definition of success, work conditions, money needs, communication, boundaries, support, and sustainability. Use it to build a provisional profile from lived evidence.

Do not reproduce the paid curriculum or promise that the profile makes a career direction obvious. Convert the profile into two or three hypotheses.

Evidence prompts:

- Which tasks leave more usable energy than they consume?
- Which tasks are performed well but carry an unsustainable cost?
- What has the person protected when it was inconvenient?
- Under which conditions did good work become easier?
- What level of stability, time, money, health, and connection counts as enough?
- Which success markers were inherited from family, peers, an industry, or a past identity?

## Motivation audit

Self-Determination Theory frames strong autonomous motivation around three basic psychological needs:

- **Autonomy:** ownership and meaningful choice.
- **Competence:** a credible path to effectiveness and growth.
- **Relatedness:** connection, care, and belonging.

Use the audit before treating follow-through as discipline failure. The goal may be controlled by status or obligation, the difficulty may offer no usable feedback, or the environment may isolate the person.

## Possible futures

The Stanford Life Design approach treats direction as a design problem: generate alternatives, prototype assumptions, gather feedback, and iterate. Do not wait for a single passion.

Make futures differ structurally:

1. **Adjust:** keep the current base; change duties, boundaries, team, or schedule.
2. **Transfer:** move the same value/capability into a different role or environment.
3. **Portfolio:** combine stable work with a small creative, service, learning, or business experiment.

For each future, name the riskiest assumption and the cheapest humane test.

## WOOP and behavior design

WOOP combines mental contrasting with an implementation intention:

1. Wish
2. Best outcome
3. Main internal obstacle
4. If-then plan

Positive fantasy alone is not the plan. The obstacle should be an internal response the person can notice and answer; do not use WOOP to erase external barriers.

Then diagnose the action with Fogg's Behavior Model:

`Behavior happens when motivation, ability, and prompt converge.`

If the action does not happen, inspect all three. Common repair order: make it easier → make the prompt visible → revisit whether the person genuinely wants it.

## Practice and feedback

Ludwin's public class contributes a useful practice sequence: define observable goals; protect preparation; study three exemplars at mechanism level; isolate the weak link; keep most repetitions reliable while testing a smaller novel portion.

Chris Do's strongest public example is graduated exposure: lower the first step, repeat, solicit specific feedback, prepare differently, and notice whether discomfort becomes more workable. Do not generalize this into clinical exposure advice.

Macnamara et al.'s meta-analysis is the guardrail: deliberate practice explains different amounts of performance variance in different domains. Never convert “practice matters” into “practice guarantees the outcome.”

## Recovery and compassionate review

Sonnentag and Fritz separate recovery into detachment, relaxation, mastery, and control. Ask which experience is missing rather than prescribing generic rest.

Neff's self-compassion model combines:

- self-kindness rather than self-judgment;
- common humanity rather than isolation;
- mindfulness rather than over-identification.

Use it to keep the review accurate enough to learn. Compassion does not mean declaring every choice good; it prevents a setback from becoming an identity sentence.

## Trajectory decision

Mark Manson's conversation with Chris Do adds a practical persist/quit distinction: passion often develops after enough investment to experience skill and reward, but sustained focused effort should eventually produce some learning, progress, feedback, or acceleration.

Review the trajectory, not one mood or one result:

| Signal | Question |
|---|---|
| Learning | Do we understand the work or ourselves better? |
| Capability | Is a relevant part becoming easier or stronger? |
| Feedback | Are informed people or the environment returning useful signal? |
| Energy | Is the cost becoming more sustainable, or only accumulating? |
| Fit | Do the real daily activities match the desired identity and life? |
| Momentum | Does another focused period plausibly move the person somewhere new? |

## Common failure modes

- **Purpose theater:** forcing a grand sentence before enough evidence exists. Use a broad, revisable direction.
- **Authenticity essentialism:** treating one profile as the true fixed self. People change across context and life stage.
- **Goal inflation:** responding to uncertainty with a five-year plan. Run one revealing test.
- **Mindset blame:** ignoring money, access, health, discrimination, caregiving, or unsafe conditions.
- **Productivity recovery:** optimizing rest until it becomes another performance demand.
- **Sunk-cost persistence:** continuing to defend identity, status, or past effort.
- **Premature quitting:** judging a field after novelty, not real practice.
- **Motivational immunity:** using belief to reject feedback. Confidence starts action; evidence steers it.
