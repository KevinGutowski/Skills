## Solicit before you give (Scott's protocol)

If you don't take criticism well, you'll never hear the truth — so the order is solicit first, give second.

1. **Have a go-to question** (from Fred Kofman): "Is there anything I could do or stop doing that would make it easier to work with me?" If those words don't fall easily off your tongue, find words that do.
2. **Embrace the discomfort.** They'll say "everything is fine." Don't rescue them with a nod. "One technique is to count to six before saying anything else, forcing them to endure the silence." Ask again. (Sheryl Sandberg sweated a banker into real feedback after a flawless IPO meeting by simply refusing to accept "nothing.") Also call out body language that contradicts words.
3. **Listen with the intent to understand, not to respond.** Don't criticize the criticism; repeat it back ("So what I hear you saying is…").
4. **Reward candor visibly.** If you agree, change something fast and visibly. Scott's "radical bander": an inveterate interrupter, she wore a rubber band and asked her staff to snap it whenever she interrupted — the visible mechanism mattered as much as the behavior change. If you *disagree*: find the part you can agree with, check understanding, schedule a return conversation, and explain exactly why — a thoughtful explanation of why not is itself a reward for candor. Toyota's red square: managers painted a square on the assembly-line floor and made new employees stand in it and *criticize the line* — engineering candor against a politeness culture.
5. **Gauge it.** Tally how often reports criticize vs praise you. All praise = smoke being blown; work harder.

## The boss is the exception to "criticize in private"

Praise in public, criticize in private — except *upward*. "You are the exception to the 'criticize in private' rule of thumb." Ask your most comfortable critic to do it in front of others at a staff meeting; reacting well in public teaches the whole team it's safe (Michelle Peluso shared her own 360 results with the company: "Here is where I want your help"). Bonus at scale: you hear things people would never book calendar time to say, and you hear them once instead of N times.

## Hogan's feedback equation

**Observation + impact + open question.** Write it out first; gut-check it won't hijack the amygdala.

- **Observation = facts only**, who/what/when/where — not feelings, judgments, or motive-guesses. Not "your emails seem angry" but "Over the last few weeks, I've noticed that your emails to me contain fewer than five words on average."
- **Iterate the impact.** First impacts are surface ("I had to ask for more info"). Keep asking *"But what's the impact of that?"* until you hit something measurable and shared (decisions delayed; trust eroding). When coaching others to give feedback: "Ask them 'What's the impact of that?' repeatedly until you get to the nugget of measurable, clear impact." If you can't find an impact, it probably isn't feedback — it's a personal preference; coach the would-be giver to introspect instead.
- **End with an open question, not a request.** Hogan's eye-contact story: she emailed a request ("practice maintaining eye contact") to a man who looked away when answering her — his reply revealed he'd been *taught* that scanning the room was respectful engagement. A request forecloses the explanation; an authentically curious question ("What's your approach when answering questions in a group?") surfaces it.
- Third-party feedback you must deliver yourself: find an aspect *you* can own (she reframed "yelling at teammates I never witnessed" around team trust and psychological safety, which she was responsible for).

## Coach reports to deliver their own feedback

Don't become the team's feedback courier. When a report brings you "Jay interrupts my focus time": coach (open questions — what outcome do they want?), then mentor them through the equation, helping strip judgment from the observation and iterate the impact. Zhuo knows a manager who, whenever someone hands him feedback about a report, asks: "Would you be comfortable sharing that feedback directly with X?" — removing the middleman removes the distortion.

## Zhuo: expectations, openers, decisions, and resonance

- **Every major disappointment is a failure to set expectations.** The Aronowitz exchange — "So is he meeting expectations?" … "And have you told him that *explicitly*?" — if the review is the first time a report hears "not meeting expectations," there are only three explanations, all bad: (1) the review is unfair/a mistake; (2) the manager was negligent and didn't notice; (3) the manager knew and withheld it. Tell people the moment performance is trending down; same for promotions that aren't coming ("here are the gaps I'm seeing") and project check-in cadence (announce involvement up front, don't swoop and poop).
- **Ranked bad-news openers** (worst→best framing): #1 "You're such a screwup" (personal attack); #2 "Your work is terrible" (judge-and-scold); #3 "I'm concerned about the quality of work…"; #4 "Your last few deliverables weren't comprehensive enough to hit the mark…" (best: specific + dispassionate); #5 "I have a few questions about your latest work—do you have a moment to walk me through it?" — looks gentle but is **"the scared manager's choice"**: concerns dressed as questions are disingenuous at best, and at worst the report never hears that you're concerned, so nothing changes.
- **Template:** "When I [heard/observed/reflected on] your [action/behavior/output], I felt concerned because…" then "I'd like to understand your perspective and talk about how we can resolve this."
- **Decision-first script.** Delivering a decision (someone else got the role, you're pulling them off the project): "the decision should be the first thing out of your mouth when you both sit down." Don't stage a fake consultation when nothing they say can change your mind. Disagreement is fine: "I recognize that you may not agree with my decision, but I'm asking for your cooperation in moving forward."
- **Check resonance:** state the point directly, then "Does this feedback resonate with you? Why or why not?" Yes → acknowledged and reflected, more likely to stick. No → now you can discuss why.
- **≥50% positive** ("Strive for at least 50 percent positive feedback") — and not only task-level; reports' #2 ask is feedback on skills and career trajectory. **No compliment sandwich** — superficial praise around a hard message reads insincere and buries the message ("as if the only way veggies can be palatable is if they're surrounded by a bunch of marshmallows").

## Clarity beats volume

- Zhuo's George story: feedback "your presentation was complicated" was heard and acted on — wrongly — because he couldn't see what *complicated* meant. Specific examples + **clarify what success looks like**: Chris Cox on a "heavy" form — "Think of the lines at Disneyland… you're going from one small room to another" → break one long form into a series of small ones. A vivid success image does what ten adjectives can't.
- Scott's mantra from the stranger who got her dog to sit: "It's not mean, it's clear!" Clarity is kindness; vagueness to spare feelings is ruinous empathy.

## SBI — situation, behavior, impact

The Center for Creative Leadership's technique: state (1) the situation you saw, (2) the behavior, (3) the impact you observed — "to help leaders be more precise and therefore less arrogant when giving feedback." Applies to praise too: "You're a genius" begs "who are you to judge my intelligence?" — describe the situation/behavior/impact instead.

## The abrasive trap

Kieran Snyder's linguistic analysis of performance reviews: when women challenge directly — which they must do to be successful — they get penalized as "abrasive" ("That word actually comes up verbatim a lot"), by women reviewers as well as men. Modeled effect: gender bias worth just 5% of rating difference turns a 58%-female entry level into 29%-female leadership.

Before giving a woman "too aggressive" feedback:
- **Switch genders**: "If the woman were a man who did the exact same thing, would the criticism 'you're too aggressive' turn into 'you really know how to get things done'?"
- **Be specific** — abstract "too aggressive" is where bias hides; concrete examples reveal whether there's a real problem.
- **Ban gendered words**: "abrasive," "shrill," "screechy," "bossy" — words rarely applied to men (Dick Costolo challenged them in reviews on sight).
- **Never just say "be more likeable."** Give specific behavior changes — and fix the bias in the environment rather than coaching women to navigate around it. (Scott's own boss suggested a *demotion* to make her more "likeable"; she quit within three weeks.)

Four rules for the recipient told "you're abrasive": (1) never stop challenging directly; (2) care personally but kill the angel in the office — no compensatory cookie-baking; (3) stay open to the possibility you *were* out of line — the research doesn't acquit you automatically; (4) don't be radically candid down but obnoxiously aggressive up.

## Systems that keep candor alive

- **Gauge stickers**: print the 2×2, leave praise- and criticism-colored stickers by your desk, ask the team to place them on your recent interactions per quadrant — daily exposure plus a running read on where you actually land.
- **Orange box** (Michael Dearing, eBay): a slotted box in a high-traffic area; he answered the contents off the cuff at all-hands, respectfully, every time — and over time "the orange box emptied out" because people just challenged him directly.
- **Management fix-it weeks** (Google): a public bug tracker for *management* problems (slow expense approvals, badly-timed reviews), voted on, deduplicated, then a week where managers cancel regular work and fix the top ones. Proof that criticism gets acted on, at the org level.
