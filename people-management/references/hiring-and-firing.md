## Hiring is your job, and it's a funnel (Zhuo)

- The priority reframe — Chris Cox to Zhuo, who never let a day pass without working on recruiting while projects stalled anyway: "If I told you that hiring well was the only thing that mattered, would you do anything differently?" Of course yes — dozens of new moves appeared (referral asks, candidate coffees, pitch-honing); the four months that followed filled every open leadership role. When the team is growing, run that thought experiment for real.
- **Funnel math kills hiring anxiety.** Individual candidates are unpredictable; aggregates aren't. If one accept = two offers = eight interviews = twenty initial meetings = forty emails, then twenty hires ≈ "you'll need to send about 800 emails over the course of the year." Divide by managers (8 managers → ~4 emails/week each), set weekly interview targets, train enough interviewers, and optimize funnel stages (better first email, events, sharper questions). "Your success depends on how well you operate."
- Hiring is not delegable to recruiting: "No recruiter can possibly know what an ideal candidate looks like for *your* team." Recruiter brings network + process; you bring role understanding + personal connection with candidates.
- Plan the team annually: future org chart, gap analysis, open-roles list — so hunger doesn't make pickles-and-ketchup look like dinner.
- Leadership hires: disproportionate blast radius; research the bar by interviewing people who *hold* the role even if they'd never take yours.
- At scale, make hiring everyone's job and culture-set it: introduce every new hire at team meetings; Zhuo's proudest moment was scanning a room of great new faces and not having hired a single one of them.

## Decision rules: weak hires, advocates, pre-debrief write-ups

- **Pre-debrief independent write-ups**: "each person should independently record their rationale and their final 'hire' or 'no hire' decision before hearing other interviewers' thoughts to ensure that the discussion doesn't lead to groupthink."
- **Reject weak hires.** The unanimous-but-tepid "hire" ("I could see him being valuable on someone *else's* team," "I don't see a reason why we *shouldn't*…") is a trap: "Since every hire is already a gamble, reject any weak hires."
- **Bet on passionate advocates over consensus**: mixed reviews where the yes-votes are *adamant* about wanting to work with her usually signal something highly valued. Polarized-with-champions beats unanimous-and-lukewarm.

## Declined offers are pipeline

Top people decline; months or years later the email arrives out of the blue: the situation has changed — "Are there any opportunities on my team?" "Jobs may be short, but careers are long." Many of Zhuo's current leaders said no once or twice first. So: tell decliners you hope your paths cross again, track rising stars, build the team's external reputation — recruiting top talent is a relationships business, not a transactions business.

## Scott's committee mechanics

- Hiring manager (not a recruiter) writes the job description, defining team **fit** as rigorously as skills — three or four culture words ("detail-oriented, quirky, blunt") interviewed for deliberately, which crowds out gut-call bias. Define the growth trajectory the role needs (rock star vs superstar) too.
- Blind skills assessments prescreen: work-sample tasks, identity stripped where possible (the orchestra blind-audition effect).
- **Committee of ~four, consistent across candidates** (different panels per candidate make results incomparable), diverse, **with at least one interviewer from another team — this prevents "desperation hiring"**: someone who isn't feeling the pain of the hole on the team is the one who'll point out the warning signs everyone else is ignoring.
- Manufacture **casual moments**: lunch, and "An executive I know who's a great hirer always walks candidates to the car" — unguarded minutes that surfaced a drug habit, a my-women-in-line braggart, and a gossip. Ask the receptionist and scheduler for reactions (Scott no-hired a strong candidate who made the scheduler cry; hired the one who picked up napkins he hadn't dropped).
- Write feedback immediately — "schedule an hour, interview for forty-five minutes, and write for fifteen." Writing is where Scott caught the charming candidate whose "scale to infinity" was a few million rows.
- **In-person debrief with study hall**: one-hour meeting, first fifteen minutes silent reading of everyone's feedback, then discuss; ends early when run well.
- The bar: "if you're not dying to hire somebody, don't make an offer" — and even if you are dying to, let any strong objector overrule you. "A bias toward no is useful when hiring."
- Promotions, same fairness machinery: Google engineering's twice-yearly off-site committees debated *other* managers' reports from objective packets — the fix for favoritism and backroom my-person/your-person bartering.

## Firing: the 3-questions test

Is it time to fire "Peggy"? Three questions (Scott):
1. **Have you given Radically Candid guidance** — caring personally, challenging directly, specifically, repeatedly over time — and seen no real improvement?
2. **Do you understand the impact on her colleagues?** By the time poor performance reaches you, it's usually been driving peers nuts for a long time.
3. **Have you sought a second opinion** — someone trusted, plus HR/legal reality (have an experienced person *edit* your PIP and emails, not just advise: managers hear "fair" and ignore "not too easy").

Identify underperformance *early* — in Scott's classes nearly everyone says the company has underperformers and almost no one has put a name in a poor-performance box. Early identification is fairness four ways: to the person (time to improve, less shock), to the company (legal risk), to you (a good rating one quarter and a firing the next destroys everyone's trust), and above all to the people performing well.

## The four lies managers tell themselves

1. **"It will get better."** It won't by itself. What exactly will you do differently? What will they do differently? No precise answer → it won't get better.
2. **"Somebody is better than nobody."** Poor performers create as much extra work as they accomplish. Steve Jobs, succinctly if harshly: "It's better to have a hole than an asshole."
3. **"A transfer is the answer."** Passing them to an unsuspecting colleague feels "nicer" and is a mistake for everyone, including the person.
4. **"It's bad for morale."** Keeping someone who can't do the job is far worse for morale — yours, theirs, and every strong performer carrying the slack. (Scott: morale has always *improved* after removing a poor performer; she's lost the people she most wanted to keep by hanging on too long. Retained bad *bosses* are the most damaging case.)

## How to fire with humanity

- Reframe before the meeting: "it's not the person who sucks, it's the job that sucks—at least for this person." Imagine specifically what job *would* be great for them; offer introductions if you can.
- Don't distance yourself; do it in the way easiest on *them* even if harder on you — Scott overruled her lawyer's security-guard advice and let the man say goodbye on his own terms; he thanked her later.
- **Follow up about a month later.** Scott emails every fired person ~a month out, keeps an ear open for fitting jobs, and doesn't push if they don't reply. One man (and his wife) thanked her profusely — leaving was good for his career, his marriage, and his kids.
- Companies where firing is too *easy* breed fear and risk-aversion; too *hard* breeds foisted transfers and "the gravitational pull of organizational mediocrity." Calibrate against both.
