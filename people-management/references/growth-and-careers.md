## Laraway's Career Conversations (3 × 45 minutes)

Russ Laraway's procedure (developed rescuing a demoralized acquired team at Google; cascaded to ~700 people, with an unprecedented jump in measured optimism). Three forty-five-minute conversations per direct report over three to six weeks, in normal 1:1 slots. Scott calls them "your single biggest opportunity to move up on the 'care personally' axis" and the *first* thing to roll out.

**Conversation 1 — life story.** Open with: "Starting with kindergarten, tell me about your life." Focus on *transitions* — "Values often get revealed in moments of change." Why did they drop grad school for Wall Street? Quit cheerleading for swimming? From each change, write down a motivation in their terms ("financial independence," "see tangible results of work," "being part of a team"). Then **check your words against theirs**: Russ wrote "leadership" for Sarah's open-space volunteer work; "she didn't like that word, preferring 'stewardship.'" The reason to extract motivations from stories rather than discuss them abstractly: abstractions get misread ("financial independence" ≠ materialism once you've heard the Bourbon Street story). If someone signals discomfort about a life chapter, drop it — respect the boundary. Practice these conversations manager-to-manager before running them.

**Conversation 2 — dreams.** "What do you want the pinnacle of your career to look like?" Ask for **three to five dreams** — plural on purpose, so they can include the one they think you want to hear *and* the real ones. Russ's framing: "A CAD—crazy-ass dream" — which is how Sarah surfaced the spirulina farm. Why "dreams," not "career goals"/"five-year plan": those phrases elicit professional, boss-pleasing answers, exclude other employers/careers, and collapse into promotion talk (which satisfies no one). Then build the **skills-by-dreams matrix**: dreams as columns, skills as rows; mark each skill's importance to each dream and the person's current competence. The needed skill usually becomes obvious — for spirulina-rancher Sarah it was *management experience*, not the presentation skills she'd assumed (and hated). Also check dreams against the Conversation-1 values: "If ‘hard work’ is a core value, why is one of your dreams to retire early?" — that probe surfaced a father planning around a special-needs child. Most dreams are humbler than spirulina: one man's was a 30-minute daylight walk with his wife while their baby was ill; Scott treated getting him out the door on time as the career plan.
- Retention is the *by-product*: Sarah stayed years longer once the job visibly served the ranch.

**Conversation 3 — the eighteen-month plan** (seeded by Sheryl Sandberg's advice that everyone needs a long-term vision and "an eighteen-month plan"). Have them ask: What do I need to learn to move toward my dreams? How do I prioritize? Whom can I learn from? How can my role change? Then list role changes, people to learn from, classes/books — and "next to each item, note who does what by when—and make sure you have some action items." Current work translated into future dreams beats "here's the next rung."

## Growth trajectory replaces "potential"

Scott + Scott Forstall, debating the McKinsey 9-box: "potential" can't positively describe someone great at the job who wants to *keep doing it*. Forstall: "Words matter… Let's wrestle with it" — and proposed **growth** instead. The questions change from the judgmental "high or low potential?" to: What growth trajectory does each person want to be on *right now*? Have I given opportunities in line with what they actually want? What trajectory do they believe they're on — do I agree? Trajectories change; never make the labels permanent.

## Managing rock stars and superstars

(2×2 of performance × growth trajectory assumed known: rock stars = excellent + gradual trajectory, the team's bedrock; superstars = excellent + steep.)

**Rock stars — recognize, don't promote.** Promotion-as-recognition pushes them into roles they don't want or aren't suited for. Instead:
- **Fair top ratings**: "all of your top performers should get top ratings" — many companies reserve top ratings for the about-to-be-promoted, silently taxing rock stars' compensation.
- **Guru / go-to-expert roles**: put them in charge of teaching newcomers — the WWII Air Force sent its *best* pilots home to train recruits (the Luftwaffe flew its aces until they were shot down, and lost air superiority). Honor, not requirement — some experts hate being interrupted all day.
- **Tenure awards**: "Consider, carefully, tenure awards." Apple's worked because design leadership invested in them personally (Ive's vapor-honed glass plaque at ten years), presenters knew the recipient's work, and presentation was public. Apple's no-general-managers, deep-functional-expertise org design is the systemic version: being in a role for years was revered, not a badge of shame.
- Other coin: bonuses/raises, public speaking slots if they like it, private thanks if they're shy.

**Superstars — keep them challenged** and accept you're not their last job; managing them is a long-term investment in people who will eventually outgrow the team (or the company — that's success, not betrayal). Don't force everyone steep ("That's like saying, to have a good marriage, marry…" — the choose-one advice is crazy); people in a superstar phase are bad at rock star roles and vice versa, and the right mix shifts over time.

## The annual growth-management pass

Once a year per report, Scott's discipline:
1. **Put names in boxes — twenty minutes, maximum.** "Don't obsess… Think fast." Rock stars, superstars, good-not-exceptional (the majority), poor-but-can-improve, poor-and-not-improving.
2. **De-bias with an outside perspective** (boss, peer, HR person familiar with the work but less attached). If they'd place people differently, understand why — *especially* if you disagree.
3. **Three-to-five-bullet growth plan each** (5–15 min/person): stretch projects for superstars, productivity protection for rock stars, pushes for the good, role/expectation/training checks for the salvageable, and a started process for the rest (→ [hiring-and-firing.md](hiring-and-firing.md)).
4. **Calibrate across managers** — half your team in the rock-star box when no peer has anyone there means you're out of step, not blessed.
5. **Fairness-by-level check**: cascading my-directs-are-the-best bias means "a greater percentage of senior rather than junior people get put in the superstar box" — expect the *reverse* (more seniors on gradual trajectories, more juniors on steep); demand identifiable justification when seniors dominate the top box.

## Hogan's four hats: mentor, coach, sponsor, feedback

Four distinct support modes — most managers "have their mentor hats on ninety percent of the time," because dispensing advice feels good and fast. Name the modes to your reports and gut-check which they need.

- **Mentoring** = advice from your own experience. Right when someone is new to role/org or genuinely stuck after several attempts. Failure mode: it centers *you* and robs them of connecting their own dots. Responsible mentoring: advice that works for the majority ("Be louder in meetings!", "Ask your boss for a raise!") can backfire on members of underrepresented groups (the competence/likeability double bind).
- **Coaching** = open questions + reflections. "The best open questions are about the problem, not the solution"; *what* questions stay curious ("What's most important to you about it?" "What's holding you back?" "What does success look like?") while *why* feels judgy and *how* slides into problem-solving. Reflections mirror back ("What I'm hearing you say is… Is that right?") and force introspection. This is the default to build toward — it's genuinely hard and takes practice.
- **Sponsoring** = spending your capital when they're *not in the room*: putting their name up for the stretch project, the All-Hands talk, the promotion. Center for Talent Innovation research: sponsored people get far more access to career-launching work. Critical correction: "members of underrepresented groups are typically over-mentored, but under-sponsored" — lots of unsolicited advice and coffee, little actual opportunity. Audit your sponsorship distribution.
- **Feedback** → [feedback-and-guidance.md](feedback-and-guidance.md).

Balance the four *per teammate per phase*: before each 1:1, decide how much time to spend in each mode for that person; rebalance as they grow.

## Structural clarity: RACI, responsibility Venn, VMSO

Growth needs scaffolding people can climb without falling:

- **RACI as delegation safety net**: on a stretch project, name your report **Responsible** and yourself **Consulted + Accountable** — they own decisions and day-to-day; they won't be penalized if it fails; you stay out of the way. Also use RACI to kill design-by-committee (most people are Informed, not Consulted), and deputize a sponsee as Accountable for a visible leadership shot.
- **Responsibility Venn diagram** (Hogan at Meetup): when peer leads' elbows bump daily, draw a circle per role (PM / EM / tech lead, or any trio), map every needs-doing item into a circle or an overlap. Surfaces work nobody owns, double-claimed work, and misalignments (a PM thought scoping was shared with the eng lead; the eng lead thought it was solely their responsibility). Revisit every ~6 months.
- **VMSO / team charter** (Jeff Weiner via Hogan): Vision (north star) → Mission (the team's role toward it) → Strategy (what this team is *uniquely* set up to do) → Objectives (measurable benchmarks). Hogan's Prod Infra org had tools and objectives but no vision/mission — so it couldn't defend prioritization, hiring, or its own existence against feature teams hungry for engineers. The published "team charter" wiki fixed all three. Pair with Zhuo's repeat-test (would five random people recite the same vision?) — see SKILL.md.
