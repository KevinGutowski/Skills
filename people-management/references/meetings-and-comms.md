## Zhuo's five-outcome meeting taxonomy

"All meetings should have a purpose" isn't enough — her status meeting had a purpose and was still lousy because she never asked "What does a great outcome look like?" Every meeting is one of five types; don't make one meeting do two jobs.

1. **Decision**: framed options + a designated decision-maker. Success = a decision *and* trust in the process (affected people present, options presented objectively, dissent given equal airtime — consensus not required). Failures: sides feel misrepresented; slow decisions on easily-reversible calls; flip-flopping; consensus-chasing instead of escalating; rehashing the same argument twenty ways. Her cautionary tale: she approved a deadline slip after hearing only the designer's side; the eng manager with seven blocked engineers demanded a re-decision — "I've just lost myself some major credibility."
2. **Informational**: success = group learned something valuable, key messages memorable, attention held (storytelling, pacing, interactivity), intended emotion evoked. Most pure information transfer should be email/chat instead.
3. **Feedback (review)**: success ≠ the boss likes it — "Gunning for approval creates incentives to focus more on the smoke and mirrors of the presentation than on getting the most helpful feedback." Success = shared definition of project success, honest status, crisply framed open questions, agreed next steps + next check-in.
4. **Generative**: group blurting isn't innovation; the best generation alternates *alone* thinking (quiet write-down time) with group build-on. Consider all ideas, not the loudest; end with idea→action steps.
5. **Relationship-strengthening**: success = better understanding and trust, openness, people feeling cared for — not hours logged or event lavishness.

Hygiene: invite everyone necessary and nobody extraneous; last minutes = "let's make sure we agree on next steps"; send a recap with owners and the next check-in.

**The attendance-cut story**: her design review grew with hiring until it "felt like a lecture hall" — a handful presenting, everyone else spectating. They shrank the list and replaced passive attendance with detailed write-ups and leadership Q&As: "the review meeting went back to being lower pressure, more honest, and more effective at doing what it was meant to do." Cutting attendees is an upgrade, not an insult — backfill the legitimate needs (education, visibility) through other channels.

## Attendance and the calendar audit

Zhuo's husband: "Do you need to be in all of them?" For one week, note every meeting and how you felt after: Did I participate? Was I critical to the outcome? Did I get something meaningful from being there? Then purge — ask organizers for pre/post notes where you only need the loop. Also hunt meetings valuable to *no one* (her dreaded Wednesday arms-crossed meeting) — cancel or revamp; bad meetings measurably drive exhaustion and burnout (Lehmann-Willenbrock).

## Scott: debate vs decision, and who decides

- **Separate "big debate" meetings from "big decision" meetings.** Half of every mixed meeting expects a decision, half wants to keep arguing — splitting them removes the tension. Debate meetings end with *no decision* by design: a summary of facts/issues, sharper choices, and a recommendation to keep debating or move to decide. They also let you deliberately *slow down* big contested calls, and they build the org's debate muscle.
- **Decide-by dates** on every debated item — so people know when the arguing ends, and so nobody endlessly lobbies the owner. (Scott's desk-move fiasco: she "grabbed" a near-finished decision out of frustration and triggered near-mutiny — the fix would have been a decide-by date, not a Sunday-night unilateral move.)
- **Obligation to dissent** (McKinsey): if everyone around the table agrees, that's the alarm, not the all-clear. Mid-debate role-switch (argue the other side — warn people in advance) keeps egos off positions and people actually listening.
- **Staff meeting agenda** (the only three jobs: review last week, share updates, pick this week's debates/decisions — never *hold* the debate there): **20 min metrics** (a simple dashboard spreadsheet, updated the night before) / **15 min study hall** (5–7 min everyone silently writes their 3–5 snippets in a shared doc, 5–7 min everyone reads everyone else's; no side conversations — study hall fixes the universal failure of async snippets, which die when people won't write them) / **30 min identify the week's key decisions and debates**, assign owners, publish the agendas so anyone may attend. Delegating debates and decisions "pushes them into the facts."
- **You're not the decider (usually).** Hierarchy-favoring "garbage can decision-making" — deciders are whoever's around the table rather than whoever has the best information — drives mediocrity; push decisions to the people closest to the facts.

## Nurture ideas; deciders get facts (and the IC/manager inversion)

- **Don't demand "three solutions and a recommendation."** Scott ran her Google team that way until Russ Laraway corrected her: "But then you're not helping people innovate… You're asking them to make decisions before they've had time to think things through. When do they get to just talk, brainstorm with you?" New ideas are fragile; 1:1s are the nursery (clarify, plus-don't-kill). Wojcicki's pre-EMG meeting existed because Google's exec debates were "the place where new ideas went to die."
- **Deciders get "facts, not recommendations"** (an Apple executive to Scott): people put their egos into recommendations, which breeds politics and worse decisions; facts are perspective-inflected but don't become lines in the sand.
- **Go spelunking**: "I call this 'spelunking' in your organization, and it is a good way to find out what's really going on" — dive into a detail's source directly (Jobs waiting in a young engineer's cube), never accept "facts" filtered through layers; it also flattens the hierarchy's feel.
- **The deliberate tension with `design-org-influence`:** Steiner's IC rule there is "always have a pitch for your complaints" — never bring your *boss* a problem without a proposed move. Scott/Laraway invert it for the manager *receiving*: demanding pitches from your reports kills innovation. Same person, two roles, opposite defaults — pitch upward, nurture downward.

## Quiet vs loud listening

Jony Ive: a manager's most important role is to "give the quiet ones a voice"; Eric Schmidt's opposite route ("Be loud!") serves the same goal. Pick the style that's natural to you, then patch its failure mode:
- **Quiet listening** (Tim Cook's silences; the Apple manager who spent ten expressionless minutes per 1:1 and "heard the things I didn't want to hear"): risks reading as a trap and lets people ventriloquize you ("Well, what the boss wants to do is X"). Patch: say what you think sometimes; don't be pointlessly inscrutable.
- **Loud listening** (Jobs): assert a strong view *to provoke a reaction* — it demands people challenge you; risks silencing the timid. Patch: meeting management — go around the table, physically block over-talkers, pre-brief some to pipe up and others to pipe down.

## The skip-level protocol

(Scott, via Roxane Wales.) Meet your direct reports' reports, without the manager, **once a year per manager** — to help your managers get better and to keep criticism flowing upward. The rules exist because skip-levels misfire easily (gripe sessions, undermined managers, proliferation):

1. **Never without the manager's prior consent** — and the manager explains it to their team first; the meeting supports the boss, it doesn't prosecute them.
2. **Never selectively.** Run them for *every* manager or they become a punishment signal.
3. **Not for attribution**: everything of import goes to the manager; who said it doesn't.
4. **Take live notes yourself and project them**; correct on the spot.
5. Kick-start: "What is your manager doing well?" → "What could your manager be doing better?" → "What really sucks?" Lots of complaints = it's working; all sweetness and light = you're failing.
6. Push the room to prioritize — more will be raised than fixed.
7. **Final eight minutes: review the notes together**, reminding everyone they go to the manager momentarily — focuses the conversation, makes it feel less behind-the-back, and kills the follow-up-email failure mode.
8. Afterward: manager picks one or two *tangible* changes ("I will disagree in person, not over email," not "improve my relationships"), emails their team what they heard and will change (cc you), follows up at staff meeting. No visible change = treat very seriously.

FAQ data points: whole-team loss of faith is rare (three times in Scott's years of these — each time the manager truly shouldn't have been managing); demo it first by having someone skip-level *you*; budget ≈ 7–8 hours/year for five reports. Beware proliferation when they go well.

## Hogan: comms plans, tick tock docs, recap emails, All Hands

- **Never improvise big news.** Julia Grace: "One thing I've learned: Never YOLO the communications plan. Never." Her template: header (author/date/status) · background (the What, the Why) · people (who knows; who's directly impacted) · timeline (what will be said, in which channel, when) · talking points. Worked example in the book: a manager's departure, 1:1s first, team meeting next day, who says what.
- **Tick tock doc** (Deepa Subramaniam): the template as a table — date/time, communicator, medium, evolving talking points per step — "a centralized place to game plan the who/what/when for a new, important message," shared with everyone doing the communicating.
- Execute sensitive plans *fast* (days, not weeks); pre-empt amygdala hijack by mapping which BICEPS needs the news threatens, drafting the first three questions people will ask, giving the hardest-hit a heads-up *minutes or hours* (not days) early, and remembering their reactions can hijack *your* amygdala — puzzle-solving which core need is threatened keeps your prefrontal cortex engaged.
- **All-Hands script** for big changes — walk through, slowly: (1) high-level what's changing; (2) what to do with questions ("We'll have plenty of time for questions at the end" + office hours); (3) the why, tied to goals; (4) specific changes **plus what's *not* changing and what's still undecided**; (5) pre-answered obvious questions (who does my performance review if my manager changes?). Bigger/scarier news → less live Q&A, more small-setting processing after. Never demean a question-asker; "I don't know, I'll follow up" is a fine answer if you follow up. Hold All Hands routinely even with no news — otherwise gathering everyone *itself* signals catastrophe, and you lose the repetition channel.
- **Recap email — four messages** for big nuanced changes: (1) the stated company line; (2) facts about what it means in practice (fact-based only); (3) **your personal take** — the vulnerability-and-honesty slot, *the* most important message when you're disagreeing-and-committing; (4) an invitation ("come to me with comments and questions" + where to find you). "Dump buckets of context and clarity in there," include when more clarity is coming, duplicate to a doc/wiki for questions.
- **Repeat, repeat, repeat**: "People rarely remember information the first time they hear it" — same message, multiple channels, rephrased per audience response.

## Confidentiality and disagree-and-commit

- **Confidentiality gut-check** before looping a trusted lead into closed-door news: Is sharing fair to them and to the others? How burdened will they feel? What if they leak — or share an *erroneous* version? Are they comfortable swimming in ambiguity? If yes, they can hone messaging and pre-empt questions — but it costs them; acknowledge that. When someone does spill, clean up misinformation first, feedback later.
- **Disagree-and-commit at the line level** (you, deputized to deliver a decision you argued against): first push back transparently upward — questions, foreseen impact, feedback; sometimes you change the message or the decision. If not: commit. "Disagreeing and committing is the most mature and transparent move you can make." Visible distancing — shrugging *don't ask me, leadership decided* — reads as undermining and fissures the team. Vent *outside* the team; use recap-email message #3 to be honest without deepening distrust.

## Culture and incentives (Zhuo)

- "an organization's culture is best understood not from reading what's written on its corporate website but from seeing what it's willing to give up for its values." Full-ownership cultures *pay* in tolerated chaos and challenged decisions — the Facebook intern who took down the site wasn't fired; his manager apologized for the setup and the team ran a blameless postmortem.
- **Incentive traps**: simple incentive rules promise much and leave collateral damage — lines-of-code rewards → bloated copy-paste; pay-by-the-word → padding; "nothing matters more than your personal quota" → undercutting colleagues; six-month feature-count bonuses → low-impact features over the year-long most-requested one. Usually better: a frank discussion of what we value and *why*, then let people apply it. Watch what your team celebrates and where it draws lines — that's the actual incentive structure.
