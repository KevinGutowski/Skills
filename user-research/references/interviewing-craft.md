# Interviewing Craft — Portigal in Depth

Source: Steve Portigal, *Interviewing Users: How to Uncover Compelling Insights* (Rosenfeld Media, 2013). Quotes verified against the text. Merged question palette adds Tomer Sharon's catalog from *Validating Product Ideas* (Rosenfeld Media, 2016), attributed inline.

## Worldview principles

Portigal's operating framework (his summary, Ch. 2):
- **"Check your worldview at the door."** Cultivate "bland curiosity" — "don't fixate on what you expect to learn, but rather cultivate your own general, non-specific curiosity."
- **Embrace how other people see the world.** Field in *their* environment, not yours. No company-logo clothing. Ask questions you think you know the answer to — "don't be so confident with your own presumptions" (the taxes-due example: "I always complete everything by March 1").
- **Rapport is your job**, not the participant's. "Some of my best interviews have been with people who are visibly uncomfortable or disinterested at the outset."
- **Listening goes beyond not interrupting.** Construct each question as a follow-up; signal loop-backs ("Earlier, you told us that…"); listening body language works "from the outside in."

**The Brain Dump** (do before fieldwork): "Get what's in everyone's heads out on the table. … talk through assumptions, expectations, closely-held beliefs, perspectives, and hypotheses. … The point is not establishing consensus; it's to surface what's implicit." Anonymize input; don't go back to validate the dump against data — it's "a transitional ritual of unburdening."

## Rapport rules

- **Accept the drink.** Portigal declined a proffered drink once and "met an ongoing undercurrent of hostility… The drink offer was made again, so I accepted, and suddenly everything thawed." The issue: "guests should act like guests." "Err on the side of taking the offered glass of water." In Japan and elsewhere, these rituals are stricter.
- **Self-disclosure rule:** be selective about talking about yourself; "me too!" shifts the dynamic. But "You should definitely talk about yourself if doing so gives the other person permission to share something" (his "My family is Jewish as well" example unlocked an interview).
- Small talk: a lubricant, not content. Don't let it drift into questioning before consent is signed and recording is on.
- Brian's-family story: a grunting recruit, hostile mother, cramped kitchen — salvaged by working "in small pieces, bit by bit," compartmentalizing discomfort, and listening until "a near-failure turned into a triumph" (two hours of great material, plus fried bread).

## The seven stages

1. **Crossing the threshold** — script your first words; arrange seating for eye contact; get consent forms signed "before we get started," then let the forms do their work (no defensive nattering).
2. **Restating objectives** — in *their* terminology; thumbnail the process; surface their constraints ("I have to stop at 2:15").
3. **Kick-off question** — broad and easy: "Maybe introduce yourself and tell us about what your job is here?"
4. **Accept the awkwardness** — discomfort presents as stiff posture, clipped answers, "you know, just regular." Ask easy, factual questions; be patient.
5. **The tipping point** — "there's often a point when the participant shifts from giving short answers to telling stories." From Ch. 2: "Stories are where the richest insights lie, and your objective is to get to this point in every interview." You can't predict when; trust the process.
6. **Reflection and projection** — sweeping statements about goals, dreams, the future. "Just because people are speaking about a future… doesn't mean it's an accurate prediction. That's not the point of the question; it's what these predictions and reflections reveal."
7. **The soft close** — the **doorknob phenomenon**: "crucial information is revealed just as the patient is about to depart. So consider keeping your recording device on, even if it's packed up."

## The question palette (Portigal × Sharon, merged)

**Portigal — context and details:** sequence ("Describe a typical workday… What do you do next?"); quantity ("How many files would you delete…?"); specific examples — "Ask for specific examples. 'What was the last movie you streamed?' Compare that question to 'What movies do you stream?' The specific is easier to answer than the general"; exceptions; the complete list ("What are all the different apps…?" + "What else?"); relationships; organizational structure.

**Portigal — probe the unsaid:** clarification; code words/native language ("Why do you call it the *bat cave*?"); emotional cues ("Why do you laugh when you mention Best Buy?"); why; probe delicately; probe without presuming ("Some people have very negative feelings about… while others don't. What is your take?"); **explain to an outsider** ("Let's say that I've just arrived here from another decade…"); teach another ("If you had to ask your daughter to operate your system…").

**Portigal — contrasts (frameworks and mental models):** compare processes (fax vs mail vs email); compare to others ("Do the other coaches also do it that way?"); compare across time (five years back / five years forward — not for prediction accuracy but "to break free from what exists now").

**Sharon's catalog (attributed additions):** guided tour ("Can we take a look at your email account together?"); **reenactment** ("Please demonstrate exactly how you did that") — "This is one of the best questions to ask during an interview"; **outsider perspective** — "a great way to catch your interviewees off guard when it comes to rationalization because they now think about others, not themselves"; identification ("Whom do you think would use something like that?"); successes and failures ("What would be the worst-case scenario?"); fill in the blank ("So in that situation, you… [pregnant pause]?"); **three wishes** — "A great question for understanding current pain points. What really matters here are not the wishes but the reasons for expressing them"; reflecting back ("So, what I hear you saying is ___. Is that right?"); peer comparison; changes over time; projection. Sharon also flags leading-question repairs ("new, improved one," "What's wrong with this?") and bans would/will questions.

**Projection/dream questions** (Portigal, near the end of the guide): "If we came back in five years to have this conversation again, what would be different?" and "If you could build your ideal experience, what would it be like?"

## Silence

"After you ask a question, be silent." Novices pad questions with suggested answers ("What did you have for breakfast yesterday…was it toast or juice?") — don't. Mantra: "allow silence." And after the answer: "continue to be silent. People speak in paragraphs, and they want your permission to go on to the next paragraph."

**Lynn Shade's cross-cultural caveat** (sidebar, "Silence Abroad"): American participants reliably break a held silence and "responding to the silence as the encouragement it was." Japanese conversational silence divides into setting-the-stage, effort, and failure silence — failure silence (not feeling qualified; confusion) "must be broken by interjections from the interviewer. … This is why the waiting-without-help technique used so successfully in New York wouldn't work in Japan, at least not without considerable modification."

## Lane changes and the question tree

"The guiding principle here is to signal your lane changes": "Okay, this is great. I'm just going to shift direction here. Maybe you can tell us, where do you keep your used oil drums?" If a question isn't obviously a follow-up, preface it with transitional words.

The field guide is linear; the live interview is a tree. Coping techniques: (1) wait — threads often resurface organically; (2) jot notes on the guide; (3) triage by research objectives; (4) triage by what makes the best follow-up for rapport. Return with continuity markers: "Earlier you mentioned using PayPal. I wanted to ask a bit more about that." The guide: "It's not a script; it's only for reference purposes" — keep it physically put away.

## Anti-pattern stories

- **TiVo / "Tye-vo"** — participant mispronounced the brand; Portigal mirrored him; the client pronounced it correctly and "this proud man was revealed to be, well, stupid, in front of his family… now we were the experts, and he was just some dude." Lesson: "it was right to be wrong. It wasn't our role to be right." Always use their language.
- **Old Keith/New Keith** — a client imposed his on-the-fly framework ("Old Keith" vs "New Keith"); Keith agreed — then, asked directly, said "I don't really see it." "Just because a framework isn't rejected by the participant doesn't mean it is accurate!"
- **USB pass/fail** — "Don't Make Your Questions Pass/Fail." A client asked a participant if she knew what a USB cable was "phrasing it as a challenge rather than as something she was curious about" — the participant shut down. Check language alignment without quizzing.
- **The Interviewer Sidestep** — when participants ask about the concept ("Will this be backward-compatible?" "How much will it cost?"): "Do not answer those questions. Do the Interviewer Sidestep and turn the question back to them: 'Is that important to you?' 'What would you expect it to be?'"
- **Concept reveals** — "There's a difference between what you want to know and what you ask." Present concepts neutrally ("Here's a whole bunch of early ideas that I was asked to show you"), never "Here's something I've been working on…". Lower fidelity earlier, higher fidelity later.

## Troubleshooting

- **Reticent participant:** first check it isn't just rhythm mismatch (the "Skype effect"). Then: accept the awkwardness; hand the lead to a colleague they connect with; reduce interviewer count; give more verbal space; fall back to direct, factual questions; as a last resort ask outright what would make it better.
- **Wrong participant:** "don't be hasty to dismiss the participant… complete the interview" — then fix the screener (the "late-model car," "sharing," and device-not-at-home stories). Surprise itself is "a crucial insight."
- **Over-talker:** first ask whether it's actually a problem (control vs information). Redirect via bridges ("So how does that experience as a child impact the decisions you make now…?"); interrupt only as a last resort, with apology and the time framing.
- **Safety:** "Unless you are going to a public or familiar corporate location, don't conduct interviews on your own." "Pay attention to the difference between unsafe and uncomfortable… If you feel unsafe, don't go in. If you feel uncomfortable, try to set that feeling aside and proceed." Women face more boundary-pushing; pause, restate objectives, and "be prepared to leave if the situation deteriorates."
- **Variations:** phone (ask for environment photos in advance; extra beat of silence); facility interviews are *your* house — be the host; very short interviews — prime participants with topics beforehand; multiple participants — throw questions open, let them riff on each other; professionals frame interviews as *meetings*, consumers as *visits*.

## Second-chair guide

Ideal field team: **two people** — lead plus second. "Even three interviewers will shift the power dynamic." Portigal's handout for colleagues, condensed:
- Lead runs the interview and coordinates your participation — like "second chair" on *Law and Order*: actively observing and strategizing without conducting the questioning.
- Stay engaged: eye contact, nods, notes. "You aren't just a 'fly on the wall'; you are participating."
- "Write down and hold your questions for the appropriate time." Interviews unfold like chapters; the lead opens windows at chapter ends.
- "We aren't the experts. The people we are interviewing are the experts. … If they refer to a product, brand, or feature inaccurately, don't correct them explicitly or implicitly."
- Open-ended only: not "What are three things you liked about using the bus?" but "Can you tell me about your experience using the bus?" — "We don't know that they liked anything."

Managing techniques: sticky notes for deferred questions; per-topic question windows ("Is there anything that we've talked about so far that you'd like to know more about?"); topic suggestions routed through the lead ("Steve, I'd love to learn about how Jacob sends the documents to accounting"). With an experienced peer, expect "gigging with a very tight jam band."

Improving: practice on everyday encounters; review your recordings like game film; get interviewed yourself; study broadcast interviewers (as interviewer, not audience); take an improv class; collect and swap fieldwork war stories.
