# Research Fundamentals — Hall in Depth

Source: Erika Hall, *Just Enough Research*, 2nd edition (A Book Apart, 2019). All quotes verified against the text.

## What research is — and just-enough scoping

"Research is simply systematic inquiry." And: "Research is just another name for critical thinking." Design research is "inquiries that are part of designing, not about design," whose job is to "peel away our assumptions like an extraterrestrial shedding its encounter suit."

What research is **not**: asking people what they like ("'Like' is not a part of the critical thinker's vocabulary. … Quash all talk about liking. Hating, too."); looking smart; being proven right ("Commit instead to continuous learning, and embrace being proven wrong as quickly as possible. An ego is a beautiful thing to incinerate."); better just because you have more data.

How much is enough? Scope to risk (below), then "listen for the satisfying click. That's the sound of the pieces falling into place when you have a clear idea of the problem you need to solve and enough information to start working on the solution." Final answer: "How much research is just enough? You'll need to do just enough to find out."

## Research types

By question (Ch. 2):
- **Generative/exploratory — "What's up with…?"** Research before you know what you're doing: interviews, field observation, literature review → unmet needs → problems worth solving.
- **Descriptive and explanatory — "What and how?"** You have the problem; do the homework "to ensure that you design for the audience instead of yourself."
- **Evaluative — "Are we getting close?"** Test candidate solutions, "ongoing and iterative"; usability testing is the most common form.
- **Causal — "Why is this happening?"** After launch: analytics, user paths, multivariate testing, plus looking beyond the site ("Maybe unusual weather patterns are affecting your customers or John Oliver mentioned you").

By topic (her chapter structure): **organizational research** (stakeholders, politics, requirements, workflow — "Hearing the same issues considered by people in different roles… will give you great insights"), **user/customer research** (ethnography: context, mental models, habits, relationships; "definitely not focus groups" — "focus groups are simply wildly expensive research theater"), **competitive research** (the real competitor list includes "inertia, insecurity, fear… the sofa"; SWOT, competitive audit, brand audit, usability-testing the competition), **evaluative research** (heuristic analysis, usability testing — "usability is necessary, but not sufficient"; "you either care about usability or you're a jerk").

Mixed methods rule: "If you have a qualitative question, you need a qualitative method… If you have a quantitative question, you need a quantitative method. … You want to know what is happening (qualitative), how much it's happening (quantitative), and why it's happening (qualitative)."

## Research-question discipline

"The most significant source of confusion in design research is the difference between *research questions* and *interview questions*. This confusion costs time and money."

- Problem statement = topic + goal, built on an **outcome verb**: "base your statement on a verb that indicates an outcome, such as 'describe,' 'evaluate,' or 'identify.' Avoid using open-ended words like 'understand' or 'explore.'"
- A good research question is "specific, actionable, and practical" — answerable with the methods available, with decision-grade confidence.
- In the field, indirection: "The questions you ask directly and the questions you want answered are two different sets of questions. If you ask 'Would you spend $50 on my product?' you'll be truly misled." "The first rule of user research: never ask anyone what they want." Instead: "If you want to know what people might do in the future, you need them to tell you true stories about what they've done in the past." Her "Everybody lies" gloss (via *House M.D.*): people "lack sufficient self-knowledge to give a true account."

## The bias catalog (her exact checklist, Ch. 2)

"Wherever there is research, there is bias. … You can't eliminate it completely—but the simple act of noting potential or obvious bias… will allow you to weigh the results more appropriately."

1. **Design bias** — bias built into how studies are structured and conducted; creeps in "when you don't acknowledge bias."
2. **Sampling bias** — self-selected survey takers ("often the angry ones"), happy customers from your contact database; counter by being careful how you generalize.
3. **Interviewer bias** — opinions leaking in; "Practice interviews and critiques with an internal team are the best way to develop a neutral interviewing style."
4. **Sponsor bias** — snacks, checks, and being on-site soften evaluations; describe the org generically, don't name the company.
5. **Social desirability bias** — "Everyone wants to look their best… participants will sometimes give the answers that cast the best light." Emphasize honesty, promise confidentiality, approach sensitive topics via general ones.
6. **The Hawthorne effect** — "The behavior of the people you are studying might change just because you are there." Blend in.

She appends a seventh trap, **the curse of knowledge** — "Once you know something, it's impossible to imagine what it's like not to know the thing" (the vasovagal-syncope-vs-fainting doctor) — "both a reason to do research and a caution when creating interview questions." Related warnings: confirmation bias via "validation" theater ("avoid the phrase *validate the design*… Aim to be proven wrong"), acquiescence bias in agree/disagree survey statements, and **surrogation** — managers substituting metrics for meaning.

## Assumptions as risk

Research "is essential to reducing your risk—the risk you incur by relying on assumptions that turn out to be wrong." Method: "try to identify your highest-priority questions—your assumptions that carry the biggest risk." Her risk inventory — what does it cost if, six months in, you realize: wrong problem; less organizational support than you thought; missing/missed competitive advantage; features you loved that customers don't care about; users' real priorities unreflected; labels users don't understand; a missed environmental factor; wrong about habits and preferences; misuse you didn't consider. "If there is no risk associated with an assumption… you don't need to spend time investigating that assumption."

Tone-setter: the chapter heading **"Assumptions Are Insults."** "When you embed wrong assumptions in the design of your product or service, you alienate people… By designing for yourself or your team, you are potentially building discrimination right into your product."

## Personas done right (or honestly fictional)

"A persona is a fictional user archetype—a composite model you create from the data you've gathered by talking to real people." Rules:
- "**Design targets are not marketing targets.** (Stamp that on every persona document you create.)"
- Research-based or it's fiction: "Otherwise you're just making up a character that might be as relevant to the design process as any given imaginary friend."
- Design for lower-expertise users and you often cover the rest; plausible and representative ("no teenage marketing VPs who model and fight crime on the side"); few key details beat a CV; real interview quote; goals + behaviors are the core; intentional naming (thwart stereotypes); real relatable photo, never stock; question demographic fields ("Do age and gender really matter?"); capture accessibility factors.
- Working test: "your personas are working when they become the first people you want to see any new idea."
- Scenarios are the plots to persona characters — flexible format, grounded in gathered data.

## Surveys — her core warnings (Ch. 9)

"Surveys are the most dangerous research tool — misunderstood and misused." "It is too easy to run a survey… our poor human brains are biased toward information that feels easy for us to process." "A bad survey won't tell you it's bad. Bad code will have bugs. … But feedback from a bad survey can only come in the form of a secondary source of information contradicting your analysis." "A survey should never be a fallback for when you can't do the right type of research… Surveys are the most difficult research method of all."

Mechanics to respect: sampling frame vs sample; margin of error, confidence level, p-values, and the "-ish" in all of it; response rate and **nonresponse bias**; survey fatigue. Question craft: general before narrow; one question at a time; no loaded questions; "Don't ask people to recall the distant past, or to predict the future"; closed answers must be mutually exclusive and comprehensive. Likert scales: bipolar, symmetrical, keep the neutral option ("it's critical to understand the degree to which people don't know and don't care"); Likert data is ordinal — "the mean is meaningless here," use the mode/distribution. NPS: "NPS is not a research tool… misused as a substitute for research. NPS feels like useful data when it isn't." Farrell's rule, endorsed: "If you won't act on the data, don't ask that question."

## Analytics — core warnings (Ch. 10)

"Just because something is measurable doesn't make it meaningful; tracking the wrong metrics can be worse than not tracking any." Beware the "funhouse mirror of **vanity metrics**—big numbers devoid of context that tell you nothing." Split testing is "tweaking and knob-twiddling—and not a source of high-level strategic guidance"; the **local maximum** trap means "you can only do so much optimizing within an existing design system. … All the split testing in the world never managed to turn Yahoo! into Google." Pair numbers with humans: "The numbers will tell you what's going on; humans will help you understand why it's happening." "You can optimize everything and still fail, because you have to optimize for the right things."

## Her maxims (all verified)

- "Make friends with reality. Cultivate a desire to be proven wrong as quickly as possible and for the lowest cost."
- "Research is just another name for critical thinking."
- "The first rule of user research: never ask anyone what they want."
- "An ego is a beautiful thing to incinerate."
- "Nothing is slower than rushing to ship the wrong thing and having to clean up afterwards."
- "Questions are more powerful than answers."
- "No matter how much research you do, facts will never change minds."
- "Wherever there is research, there is bias."
- "Recruiting and scheduling participants is the most difficult part, so always be recruiting."
- On skepticism (her closest formulations — the phrase "applied skepticism" does not appear in the text): "once you start getting answers, you'll keep asking more questions. And that skeptical mindset is more valuable than any specific methodology"; and the heading "Be a skeptic" — "Get in the habit of asking a lot of questions. Question all your assumptions."
