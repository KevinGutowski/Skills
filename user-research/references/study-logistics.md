# Study Logistics — Plans, Recruiting, Field Prep, Ethics

Sources: Erika Hall, *Just Enough Research* (2nd ed., A Book Apart, 2019); Steve Portigal, *Interviewing Users* (Rosenfeld Media, 2013); Tomer Sharon, *Validating Product Ideas* (Rosenfeld Media, 2016). Quotes verified.

## Stakeholder interviews (before user research)

Portigal: "The first interviews you conduct should be with the stakeholders… I typically aim for 6–8 stakeholders… one-on-one conversations either on-site or on the phone. They run between 30 and 60 minutes and are used to dig deeper into objectives and establish collaboration."

Hall's organizational-research version: interview leaders, managers, subject-matter experts — and "balance out the executive perspective with people who do the day-to-day work" across silos. Interview individually ("the more political an organization, the more important private conversations are"); promise non-attribution; 30–60 minutes, private place, separate notetaker. Core questions: role and duties, typical day, who they work with, "how would you define success?", concerns, greatest challenges, "Is there anyone else I should talk to?" Hostility playbook: stay calm, restate the goal, ask one open question, or cut it short — "Never let anyone take control of the interview from you." Rationale: "Asking someone for input before you get started is a peerless prophylactic against that person rearing up late in the game with insurmountable objections" (Hall, citing Ford's WWIC).

Portigal's stakeholder-alignment move when factions disagree ("group therapy"): go around the circle on feelings about the field guide — excited about, nervous about, uncertain about — until everyone is heard.

## One-page plan (Sharon's template)

1. **Background** — what led here
2. **Goals** — why this study; what it ends in
3. **Research questions** — what specifically you want to learn
4. **Methodology** — technique, duration, where
5. **Participants** — who, with characteristics
6. **Schedule** — when results are needed; when the team can synthesize together

Hall's plan adds: problem statement (outcome verb: describe/evaluate/identify — never "understand"/"explore"), roles, incentives, materials. Hall's six-step spine: define the problem → select the approach → plan and prepare → collect → analyze → report.

## Screener pipeline

Sharon's three-step transform: **criteria → measurable benchmark → masked question.** "Uses Facebook" → "Posts on Facebook at least once a week" → "How often do you post something on the following websites?" (list decoys; right answer screens in). "You don't need people who just want to participate; you want to find people who qualify to participate."

Hall's screener rules (after Perfetti): screen for (1) specific *behaviors* ("If you're designing an app for cyclists, you need to test the app with people who ride bikes, not people who love bikes"), (2) tool knowledge and access, (3) domain knowledge. Keep it short; "Don't ask anything on the screener that you can find out searching the web"; be vague about the study to defeat professional participants; phone-screen finalists for in-person studies ("weed out axe murderers and the fatally inarticulate"). Hall: "Good recruiting puts the quality in your qualitative research… If you're talking to the wrong people, it doesn't matter what you ask."

Portigal's criteria style: behavior over attitude ("active," "more than 100 feed subscriptions" — not "dramatic storytellers"); cover multiple sides of a transaction; mix specific and descriptive. "Recruiting is data": "If it's very challenging to find the people that you expect… that's data."

## Recruiting channels and timing

- **Sharon (DIY, social media):** identify audience keywords → find target groups/pages/hashtags → post the screener → track responses and select. Channels named in 2016 (Facebook, Twitter, Google Plus, LinkedIn, Xing) are examples; the pipeline is the durable part. "Recruiting participants is the greatest bottleneck of user research. Start as soon as you can."
- **Hall:** recruit your own ("Why should the recruiting agency learn the most about your audience?"); post anywhere your users or "their forward-happy friends and family" look; call it "design research," not "marketing research"; for Agile cadence, "always be recruiting" — standing windows every three weeks, plus a living participant database.
- **Portigal (agency route):** full-service recruiters with their own facilities; expect ~2 weeks to recruit, $150–225/participant fee (2013 US consumer figures) plus the incentive. Incentives are "an enthusiastically demonstrative thank you" — for professionals, food or charity donations may fit better than cash.

## Field guide + shot list

Portigal's flow: **introduction and participant background → main body (subsections + exercises/stimuli) → projection/dream questions → wrap-up** ("Did we miss anything?" "Anything you want to ask us?" → thanks + incentive). Assign durations per section. Write questions as you'd ask them, not as topics — drafting is "leading a mock interview in my head." Share it with the caveat: "Remember, this is not a script. … it's really just a tool to prepare to be flexible."

**Shot list** appendix: head shot; participant + key equipment; close-up of key equipment; establishing shots interior and exterior; two-shot of interviewer and participant.

## Scheduling

Portigal: "two interviews a day is reasonable. … Quality work doesn't come from being rushed, exhausted, harried, or overwhelmed." Leave gaps to debrief, adjust, travel, eat. Keep travel days and interview days separate; international fieldwork gets an acclimatization day. Adapt to participants' hours (consumers: evenings/weekends). Sharon's lean alternative: 10 interviews, two pairs × five/day, or two per evening over three days — pick your school deliberately.

## Releases, consent, ethics

Portigal's release covers: **consent** (voluntary, can stop anytime), **incentive** (payment ≠ employment), **model release** (image/video use), **non-disclosure** (cut the NDA when nothing confidential is shown). Sign *before* the interview starts; agency studies can stay "blind."

Sharon's informed-consent points — participants truly understand and agree that: they agree to participate; they know what will happen; how data will be used; they're being recorded; they agree to recording; how recordings will be used; identity/privacy protected; participation voluntary; they can stop at any point; they'll raise concerns immediately; they can ask questions first. "Half-assing your research ethics, means you're half-assing your learning process, means you are half-assing your product development."

Hall's ethics floor (after Michael Quinn Patton): explain purpose plainly; describe benefits; note risks; mutual confidentiality promises you can keep; informed consent; who sees the data; interviewer effects; an ethics advisor; how hard to push; a code beyond minimal rules. Plus: project-level ethics (is the thing itself harmful?), minors need parental consent, "never, ever agree to do telephone interviews when anyone involved is driving," and Castillo's caution that unexamined privilege turns pity into fake "empathy."

## Equipment

Hall's field bag: dedicated/shared laptop(s); data storage and transfer (cards, drives, adapters — assume no network); field recorder; webcam; small notebooks and good pens; sign-making and paper-prototyping materials; checklists ("Why rely on memory when you don't have to?"). Analysis room: 3×5 cards, sticky notes, pads, whiteboards, markers for the right surfaces, a big shared screen. File hygiene: shared drive immediately, naming convention ("Study-Subject Name-Year-Month-Day"), backup drive in the field. "It's too easy to become addicted to cheap, bad data" — prefer familiar tools over magic services.

## Hall's team roles (verified — her full list is eight)

Roles are "clusters of tasks, not individual people"; one person can hold several, and roles can be shared. Always make them explicit in advance:

- **Author** — plans and writes the study (problem statement, questions, guide/script); ideally a team activity
- **Recruiter** — screens and selects participants; keep this skill in-house
- **Coordinator/Scheduler** — plans time, books sessions
- **Interviewer/Moderator** — interacts directly with participants
- **Observer** — clients/team watching, so long as presence doesn't influence the research
- **Notetaker/Recorder** — frees the interviewer; fallback for recording; watches the clock
- **Analyst** — finds patterns; "More than one person should have this role" to reduce bias
- **Documenter** — reports findings

Hall: "Someone needs to be the research lead — the person who keeps everyone on track and on protocol and takes ultimate responsibility for the quality of the work."
