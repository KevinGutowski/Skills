# Synthesis and Impact — From Field Data to Decisions

Sources: Steve Portigal, *Interviewing Users* (Rosenfeld Media, 2013); Tomer Sharon, *Validating Product Ideas* (Rosenfeld Media, 2016); Erika Hall, *Just Enough Research* (2nd ed., A Book Apart, 2019); MailChimp, *The UX Reader* (2014). Quotes verified.

## Debrief worksheets — same day, every session

Portigal: "you must absolutely allow time for debriefing after each interview. After you leave the fieldwork site, go for food and drink and talk about the interview." Use a debrief worksheet; ask **"What would we design for this user?"** — "hypothetical, speculative, and easily discarded when future data takes you in a different direction." Notes are **descriptive, not interpretive**: "If Larry tells you he has worked 14 hours a day for the last 10 years, your notes should read 'Worked 14 hrs/day for 10 years,' not 'Larry is a workaholic.'" If you must capture interpretation, mark it visually ("IS LARRY A WORKAHOLIC?").

Sharon's **quick debrief** — four questions (inspired by IDEO's human-centered design kit):
1. What did the participant say or do that surprised you? Any memorable quotes?
2. What mattered most to the participant?
3. What stood out during this session? What are the big lessons?
4. What should we do differently in future sessions?

Plus five quiet minutes writing a one-paragraph session summary.

## Daily affinity wall

Sharon's **daily debrief** (multi-session days): team at a large wall, all Post-it observations up, sort into temporary logical groups (groups can change day to day), unique note color per participant so popular vs one-off observations jump out, photograph the wall, log groups into a spreadsheet, keep iterating until data collection ends. Photos from the field: organize by observation group, curate topical galleries, print one photo per participant, cover a wall and tag with observations.

Hall's analysis ground rules: focus only on understanding users; respect the session structure; "Clearly differentiate observations from interpretations (what happened versus what it means)"; **no solutions yet**. What counts as data: goals, priorities, tasks, motivators, barriers, habits, relationships, tools, environment. Code every sticky to its source. Set outliers aside (pay them anyway). Beware pattern-hunger: "human brains are pattern-recognition machines" — team interpretation counters wishful patterns.

## Topline report

Portigal: right after fieldwork, compile a **starter set of 5–15 thematic areas** building on debriefs and highlights — "patterns and weak signals," provocative questions, not conclusions. Then the topline report keeps stakeholders warm while deep analysis proceeds. Hall's reporting rule: "a quick sketch of a persona or a photo of findings documented in sticky notes on a whiteboard in a visible location is far superior to a lengthy written report that no one reads" — but always also write the brief, well-organized summary (goals, methods, insights, recommendations) for your future self.

## KJ Technique (Sharon)

Named for Jiro Kawakita; "allows teams to reach a consensus quickly on results of qualitative data." Cross-discipline group, one hour, hundreds of stickies, big wall:
1. Everyone posts their observation notes and reads others'.
2. **Silent grouping** — "no discussion allowed at this point since it will always sway the focus to borderline notes"; anyone may move anyone's notes.
3. **Silent naming** — each member names groups; split or merge as needed (forces everyone to read everything).
4. **Silent voting** — each person picks their three most important groups against the research question, ranks them, marks X / XX / XXX on the group names.
5. Tally and order. "The team has reached consensus quickly and effectively."

Use it to turn interview data into personas: BS personas → interviews → KJ → real personas (add habits paragraph, a real quote in a speech bubble, behavioral similarities/differences, open questions).

## Storytelling (Sharon)

After affinity/KJ, per important observation group, "write a short story that describes a future scenario of a person using a product or feature that doesn't exist yet. The story can be very short — about **150–200 words**. Base the story on a problem or need you identified during observation." Share for feedback and shared understanding. (Hall's equivalent: personas + scenarios — "If personas are your characters, scenarios are your plots.")

## Rainbow spreadsheet (Sharon, online usability testing)

One spreadsheet, whole team, one color column per participant. Sheets: **Observations** (pre-seed expected behaviors; never double-barreled entries), **Metrics** (time on task, success, satisfaction), **Participants**, **Raw data**, **Summary** (answers to research questions + action items). Repeated observations light up across colored columns. ~10 important action items is healthy — "Avoid 'analysis paralysis.'" When sharing beyond the team, "don't call it the rainbow spreadsheet. Instead, call it the report."

## Prioritizing changes

Sharon: "An online usability test's primary goal is making changes. … **If you don't make any changes, your online usability test has failed.**" Choose changes by:
- **Impact** — maximum positive effect on the user; skip right-but-secondary fixes
- **Persistence** — prioritize recurring issues over ones users learn past
- **Frequency** — more users hitting it = higher priority
- **Politics** — "Develop changes as if you live in a bubble. Don't exclude the right changes because someone else might not like them."
- Plus: split short- vs long-term; some problems need more research, not a fix.

Hall's severity × frequency tiers for usability findings: severity high/moderate/low (blocks task / causes difficulty / minor) × frequency high ≥30% / moderate 11–29% / low ≤10% → Tier 1 (fix or risk the product), Tier 2, Tier 3. Weight by how core the task is. Start with Tier 1, lowest-effort fixes first; then test again. "The most expensive usability testing of all is the kind your customers do for you after launch by way of customer service."

## Research as leadership (Portigal, Ch. 9)

- **Field participation is the highest-leverage impact move:** "The most impact for the least effort comes from your colleagues joining you in the field. … even a single interview can show individuals that how the organization has been thinking about user needs is incomplete."
- **Make the process visible:** the client who debriefed in the kitchen drew curious passersby; prototype at your desk, not in a lab.
- **Posters and museums:** one team "mounted an ever-growing number of user profile posters on the walls around their workspace"; another "built a 'museum' from our research in Japan… in place for over a year and prompted conversations for years beyond that." One client gave 30 presentations of a single deliverable.
- **Speak the consumer's format:** "Users need it to be easy to clean" became "Surfaces should be made from elastomeric materials and all joining pieces should have no more than a 1mm gap." Deliver findings in whatever form the internal customer can act on — stories, requirements, prototypes.
- Be proactive: propose strategic studies ("digital daily lives") instead of waiting for tactical requests; train others — research isn't just "talking to people."

For winning budget, executive sponsorship, and the business case around research → see the **design-org-influence** skill; Hall's framing to carry there: "facts will never change minds. … In order to influence decisions with evidence, you have to work with existing beliefs, not against them," and "evidence-based design" as the rallying cry.

## Research distribution channels (MailChimp)

MailChimp's *The UX Reader* makes Portigal's impact rule operational: "Research that goes unapplied is of little value," and research "can’t create change in an organization until it has been turned into a compelling story." Do not treat the formal report as the deliverable. Pick channels by attention mode:

- **Posters** for ambient reminders and persona/customer quotes in shared space.
- **Coffee Hours / Lunch & Learns** for company-wide themes or cross-functional footage review.
- **Internal newsletters** for small stats, current work, and links to the deeper source.
- **Mini-documentaries** for empathy transfer: faces, workplaces, tools, and daily struggles.
- **Collaborative reports** for the durable record and async discussion.
- **Research evangelists** for the hallway/slack connective tissue: route "you are working on X" to "talk to this person / read this study."

Their distribution rule: "repeat information in different ways through multiple channels." The same finding should become a skimmable artifact, a live conversation, and a durable reference.
