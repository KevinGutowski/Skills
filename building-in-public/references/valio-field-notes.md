## Todd Garland (BuySellAds)

Five years in at talk time: **19 people, all remote, ~$50M in transactions across those five years, $0 raised.**

- **Spreadsheet before code**: the first thing he did with the idea was "just open up excel" — "the first spreadsheet i built to validate the money side." The model surfaced a real constraint early: ad serving "isn't going to scale at 140k a month to serve ads." The lesson he draws: make sure the math works before you dive in.
- **The ship gap was his biggest mistake**: "a six-month hiatus there between when i actually finished writing the the software and launching the product… mine was just not shipping… you just got a ship no matter what it looks like no matter how many things are broken."
- **Fake what can be faked**: "don't waste time building features that can be easily faked." The private-beta invitation code "didn't actually do anything — i would just make up some random string of numbers every time i sent out an email… just the presence of that string meant you could register" ("for the record we still do that today"). "Automatic" listing screenshots were him frantically screenshotting, resizing in Photoshop, and "manually ftping it to a server."
- **The bootstrapping press law — nobody cares**: search "bootstrap" on TechCrunch ≈ 9,370 results (14,100 before cleaning out Bootstrap-the-framework); "venture capital" ≈ 143,000. "If you need to build traction through press coverage go raise some money." Bootstrapped products earn attention through the product, not coverage.
- **Counter-narrative stats** (his slides): Inc 500 (2012) — 77% founded on personal savings, 33% started with less than $10k; "less than one percent of companies each year raise venture capital"; "76% of tech companies acquired in 2012 had not raised institutional investment."
- **Exit math**: a $50M sale (half of exits are below $50M) leaves a 10%-equity VC-backed founder ~$4M after taxes and preferences; bootstrapped at 50/50, "each founder takes home 18 million bucks." And the deeper point: "all money comes with strings attached so you just got to be sure you're ready to accept those strings."
- **Kill products that don't pay**: on their private-label spinoff — "if it's not making money just get rid of it" (he cites 37signals shutting Breeze four months in the same week).
- **The jump trigger was arithmetic, not courage**: "2008 earnings ended up around 80 grand" → plotted scenarios → quit. Timeline: idea Feb 2007, v1 Sept 2007, launch Feb 2008, full-time Jan 2009 — "about two years passed from concept to real working product to replacing my full-time salary."

## Jeff Sheldon (Ugmonk)

- **The personal-taste QA test**: "this is the product test… If I won't wear it, I won't I won't sell it." Design to your own durable taste, not trends — shirts designed four years earlier still sold.
- **"I could do that." "Yeah, but you didn't."** — his answer to the armchair-founder objection. Execution is the moat.
- **Reinvest everything**: "every single penny that I made, I put right back into the business" for "almost two and a half years" while he and his wife both worked full-time.
- **Limited editions as cadence, not identity**: only the anniversary tee is limited — "rather than making everything we do limited edition… to keep that drive, but not to kill ourselves by having to come up with designs every single week."
- **Name for headroom + searchability**: "I never called it UGG Monk T-shirts" (caption sic) — "I knew that I didn't want to limit myself" when the product line broadened. And a unique string wins search: a made-up name means every result "somehow make[s] their way back" to you.
- **Show imperfect process**: on sharing how you make things — "someone's going to figure out how to do it, too. So, if you just show them, they're a lot more likely to come to you."
- **Find a niche** (his rule #3): the leather journals are "a really niche thing… you'll appreciate these… from a different standpoint than the average consumer" — depth with a small audience beats breadth with none.
- **The unglamorous ratio**: "it's not the 10% or the 2% glamorous side of the press coverage… it's 98% when you're home on a Friday night answering customer service emails and all your friends are out partying."

## Kai Brach (Offscreen Magazine)

- **The maker's curse**: as the maker "you see all the decisions you made" — you can never read your own product the way a reader does.
- **Sponsorship as design**: eight center pages on different stock, every sponsor "presented the same way… very consistent and very unified… a black page with the logo in the middle." Readers reported that "for the first time ever [they] read every single word of the magazine including the ads." Constrain advertisers harder and the ads get *more* attention.
- **Distribution economics force direct sales**: distributors don't buy inventory, they take it on consignment; distributor takes ~20% of cover price, retail ~40%, "you're left with about 40%"; some pay up to 365 days later by paper check; unsold copies "they just destroy everything else." So the website became the main sales channel.
- **Perfectionism is inertia**: "the need for Perfection is sort of a source of an anxiety… really creates a lot of self-doubt… leads to a lot of inertia and… you simply just don't get started."
- **The publisher's treadmill**: "after one issue is always before the next" — recurring products convert a launch into a lifestyle; price that in.
- **Outsider advantage**: breaking into print as a web person, "it's really great being that Rebel or that Misfit" — uncovering inefficiencies "with a bit of… dumb naivity."
- **The content plan**: one spreadsheet flat-plan; the first week it grew to "like 260 rows and I suddenly realized oh I have to fill 260 pages" — culled to 112 pages. Plan the container before commissioning the content.

## Aubrey Johnson — the counterweight voice

Holds the opposite pole from most of this corpus (and from this skill's audience-led, build-for-yourself leanings). Keep both in tension:

- **Against raising too early, but for raising**: "don't raise a silly amount of money when you don't need it" — cash blindness "takes away your hunger to to win." But the bootstrap-purist consolation prize is also fake: "if the product fails and you didn't raise money you still failed it's still zero at the end of the day."
- **What VCs weigh — traction > team > product**: traction is "the super secret sauce"; team next; "the last thing in order of importance is product."
- **Test before you build**: "before we build anything to scale we test it… a landing page or something very… lightweight or cheap and we see if there's interest… we don't rely on our own oh yeah that's a thing."
- **Build-for-yourself is "a little bit lazy"** — the explicit counter-position to the build-for-yourself thread elsewhere in this corpus (Josh Long's 1000-true-fans below, the skill's own maker-led content): "it's so easy to check your own biases… if you're talking about doing really big things doing it for yourself sometimes I think feels a little bit lazy." Designers ship "trinkets… marginally increasing productivity or marginally increasing happiness." Route by ambition: lifestyle business → build for yourself; venture-scale problem → get out of your own head.
- **The designer-equity datum**: "a lot of designers hold less than five percent of the company that they're at but they provide more than half of the value." Know your leverage before you negotiate.

## Josh Long

Deltas only (the rest overlaps the skill's existing content):

- **Pre-sell before you make**: his book *Execute* went "idea to execution of a product — a sold product… actually having everything completely sold in eight days." And he scrapped the draft "with about three days to go" and rewrote — the pre-committed audience forced the finish.
- **Deadline compression**: reframe procrastination — "take a deadline that you normally would have done and slide it up the scale" — compress on purpose to force momentum.
- **"Know your critical three"**: his are writing, design, development — "if you understand what you want to accomplish get it down to three things."
- **Kevin Kelly's 1000 true fans** as the build-for-yourself economic floor: "when you build something for yourself there's going to be a small group of people that have the same prob[lems]." (Johnson, above, is the rebuttal.)
- **Momentum is the asset**: "if you lose momentum in something it's so hard to get back."
- Permission slip: "you don't necessarily have to be an entrepreneur" — maker ≠ founder; you can ship things without running a company.

## Shane Mielke

- **The uncommittable outside commitment as forcing function**: 16 years coaching football alongside a full creative-director career — a fixed obligation he "couldn't get away from" forced "working effectively and working efficiently" during the hours that remained. An immovable commitment is a productivity system.
- **Foosball-time compounding**: at his early job, in the two-three unbillable hours when "they were all playing foosball I was off to learn in Flash… the things that I did after that led to… everything that I ever dreamed about." Unique skill → "suddenly you have a little bit of power." His maxim: "foosball is the enemy" (it isn't about foosball).
- **Boundary mechanics**: "don't answer emails late at night… you're answering responding at 10 11 12 at night… they're gonna get used to that." And the identity trap: "Superman never gets a day off" — be in-and-out of hero mode, never permanently in it.
- **"Playing mental Tetris"** — slotting work into the gaps a constrained life leaves; **"run yourself like a business"** — your time, energy, and skills are the P&L.
- **The core claim**: "more time does not equal better work."

## Michael Flarup

- **The story filter**: "ask yourself if this would make a great story — if the answer is yes there's probably some sort of fun in that process." He runs "an alarmingly" large share of business choices on it.
- **Ideas aren't precious**: society overvalues ideas relative to execution — "enjoy your ideas discuss them openly… send them out on the town." (Sheldon's "Yeah, but you didn't" is the same law.)
- **"Form follows fun"** — and it compounds: "fun creates more fun boring creates more boring." Fun projects attract the next fun project; joyless ones attract more of themselves.
- **Product from a one-star complaint**: a user one-starred their thermometer app because the phone couldn't actually measure temperature → they built Thermodo, the hardware thermometer. "Every developer knows the feeling of a one star app review" — the complaint *was* the story, and the market.
- **People pay premiums for delight**: every theme in the thermometer app was ~$1; the Bling theme (diamonds, a chain that jiggles when you move the phone) was $10 — "and people buy it… because it's fun and it's weird."
- **Hidden URL "inside the glue"**: Thermodo's packaging hid a URL inside the glue seam — "not a lot of people found that but if you went to that URL something fun happened." Delight for a fraction of users still shapes "the entire perception of a product."
- **Crowdfunding success explodes complexity**: the Kickstarter goal was $35k (raised from $30k overnight for shipping costs); it hit $336k — "10 times as much as we asked for" — and the project "just completely exploded" in complexity, eating most of 2013. Budget for the oversubscribed case.

## GMUNK (Bradley Munkowitz)

Career items (his craft/process notes live in `design-craft`):

- **The personal-project engine**: "I never wait for someone to hire me to make work… There's never a moment where I am not doing a personal project on the side." The side project is the portfolio, the skill lab, and the pitch all at once.
- **Client detachment**: "you put your heart into it but it's not yours and it never will be yours… you need to take a very… detached" stance — "you give 100%. We're professionals of course, but it's not yours." Save ownership-energy for the personal work.
- **Show everything**: "all of this work is valid" — process frames, tests, and outtakes all belong in public, not just the hero shots.

## App-maker panel + Dann Petty — scraps

- **Localization horror** (app panel): a playful "your friends are lame" string came out in German "like death to your friends… we were getting… tons of one star reviews." The fix: "I would rather have some native speaker do localizations and take longer and make sure they're perfect" than machine-translate and infuriate a continent.
- **VIP-featured-users growth tactic** (Seesaw): "we picked some like VIP featured users… if you just email someone like hey I want you to be like an all-star in my app," people say yes — flattery-as-distribution, and they bring their audience.
- **Cheddar post-mortem** (Sam Soffes): "the core reason it feels like lack of focus and trying to do too much… I launched with iPhone iPad the web and an open API and then added a Mac app which is a ton of stuff for one person."
- **Health boundary** (Dann Petty, with Mielke's email rule above): "one day to finish five projects at once," then a 2 a.m. rush to the ER — a massive anxiety attack plus a gastritis attack that "mimics a heart attack": "I thought I was dying and it was all because of my work." The 98%-unglamorous reality (Sheldon) compounds into this if unbounded; boundaries are product-survival infrastructure, not indulgence.
