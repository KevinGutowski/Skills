## Core stance

- **"You price the client and not the job."** The same logo drawn for a mom-and-pop and for a corporation with a $40M printing budget is worth wildly different amounts — the work is identical, the risk and value are not. Paul Rand for a mom-and-pop "is going to do it for a sandwich. Cuz that's what it's worth to them."
- **"Clients don't choose the best option. They choose the least risky option."** Pricing power is risk-reduction power: referrals, case studies, social proof, business fluency. Ask "What criteria are you using to make your decision?" — you should already know the answer.
- **Never anchor on your cost or your hours.** A transaction happens when both parties see an exchange of value greater than what they're giving up — "It has nothing to do with what it costs you to make."
- **"Pricing the moment" (Mall — school difference).** Mall extends price-the-customer with the provider side: "The largest factor in your current value is your ability to walk away from a prospect" — booked solid, you price up ("If you won't take their project for $10K, the only recourse they have is to offer you $20K, then $50K, then $100K"); and, explicitly, "if you're struggling to pay the bills and have no projects on deck, you take what you can get… The scarcity principle works both ways" (Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016). **Mark the fork:** the Do school treats down-pricing on a dry pipeline as the race to zero — desperation prices, keep-the-lights-on runway instead. Mall says scarcity honestly cuts both directions. Present both; don't silently blend.
- **When build-cost → 0, sell "great," not "good enough"** (Brandon Jacoby, ex-Cash App/first hire at Capital/X, now independent — Dive Club RaKFP_DuqpA, auto-captioned). The AI-era positioning: "software is this form of hospitality where you're welcoming a user base into a product" — and with "cost of materials and labor rapidly decreasing," what a client can no longer buy cheaply is the last mile. His pitch, verbatim: "in a world where it's like super easy to build like good enough, the thing that I'm like selling is how to be great." Good-enough is now the commodity baseline; price the gap between it and great, not the production.

## The Sequence Flip (master flow)

The broken default: **scope → time × rate → price** (value rarely discussed, success never measured). This hands the client an itemized estimate — "you served it on a silver platter… you gave it to them to tear apart" — and every negotiation becomes rate-and-hours unbundling.

The flip: **desired future state → success metrics → value → price → scope.**

1. **Desired future state** — "Why are you even doing this? Where would you like to be a year from now?" Let the client dream.
2. **Success metrics** — refuse fluffy answers; quantifiable only.
3. **Value** — the roleplay arithmetic: client wants engagement up from 400K to 600K impressions/month; that's worth +$40K profit/month to them; ×12 = **$480K/year**. "What would be a good amount of money… to spend against this number?" Client offers $10K → "You would spend ten thousand to make four hundred eighty thousand?" 10% would be $48K.
4. **Price** — "Spending 48 thousand to get 480 is a bargain. Here's the good news: I won't even charge you 48, I'll charge you 40."
5. **Scope last** — notice the roleplay never discussed deliverables. "We discounted for uncertainty… we're also doing something called price anchoring" — against $480K, $40K reads small.

## Key rules

- **"You say a price before you show a price."** Get verbal agreement on the number ("how does 40,000 sound?") before building any document — "when you get an agreement on the number, only then build your bid."
- **One page, one price** *(Do's school)*. Go "from a multi-page bid to a single-page bid with one price." **Competing default — Mall's Goldilocks:** always three options, tiered on different client values, in deliberately incomparable units, so "you encourage your client to compare you with you" (Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016). Stark also runs three options (priced on attention). Two-against-one on options vs. one price — but Do's one-pager is downstream of a verbal yes, which changes the job the document does. See pricing-mechanics.md.
- **Say "money," not "budget" (Mall).** "The word budget connotes cheap… Focusing on 'money' keeps the conversation on maximum value, whereas 'budget' draws attention to minimum spending."
- **Count the competition (Mall).** "If you're one out of twenty competitors, run! The odds aren't in your favor, or worse: your customer doesn't know what they want or how to make decisions. I've rarely entered situations where more than three studios were pitching." >3 studios pitching → run.
- **Round numbers.** $40,000, never $40,272 — "a number that's this accurate implies there's a pricing formula… anytime you have a formula I can argue with you," and then they unbundle you.
- **"When you say it you're selling; when they say it you're closing."** Ask until the client names the problem, the value, and ideally the budget. "I don't want to say it. I just want to ask you questions until you say it. When you say it, you believe it."
- **"Amateurs give advice; experts diagnose."** Zero questions asked = zero diagnosis = a pitch, and the guard goes up.
- **"Whoever asks more questions in a conversation is in control of the conversation."** (Dan Lok, quoted constantly.) Speak less; never defend a position — probe the logic of theirs.
- **Discount for uncertainty.** You can't guarantee outcomes you don't control, so charge ~10% of one-year value as the no-brainer price ("a 90% discount") — and they keep years two, three, four.
- **Never negotiate at your floor.** Open above it: "I don't want to negotiate at $500 because I'm gonna get dragged down."
- **"Never assume it's too much."** When they hem and haw, ask: too much or too little? Some clients are nervous about spending too *little* — you don't buy the cheapest LASIK; "you only have two eyes."

## When to walk

- **Stakeholder + entrepreneur filter:** value conversations only work with a decision-maker with skin in the game; "a person who doesn't value their time doesn't value your time." Otherwise refer and leave.
- **Dinner test (Stark):** "Would you have dinner with this person? If not, why are you doing business with them?"
- **Fear-of-loss reframe:** "I didn't have this job." And on inbound: "You didn't have that $1,000 before they called you. You were going to make life work regardless."
- **Runway to say no:** keep enough financial slack (keep-the-lights-on money) that any single deal is optional — desperation prices are the race to zero.
- **The gut walkaway:** Chris left a swanky Hollywood meeting at "So, what are you going to do for us?" — and has walked from seven-figure jobs on the same instinct.
- **Expect to lose well (Mall):** "Since I've started using value pricing, my loss rate on proposals has gone way up… (But we make way more money now.)" And across a few hundred lost proposals: "Only one of those rejections has involved an objection to price" (Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016). A good loss is the client saying "We see the value. This isn't the right thing, but we'll be back."
- **Meal-test convergence:** Mall's last questionnaire check — "ask yourself if they're the type of client you'd have a meal with. If you can't hang out, what makes you think you'll be able to work together?" — is Stark's dinner test, arrived at independently. Three schools, same filter.

## Checklist

- [ ] Talking to a stakeholder/entrepreneur, not a gatekeeper or errand-runner?
- [ ] Diagnosed before advising — more questions asked than answered?
- [ ] Future state and success metrics quantified before any price talk?
- [ ] Client said the value/budget number, not you?
- [ ] Price is a round number, said aloud and agreed before any document?
- [ ] Proposal: one page one price, or three options priced on attention?
- [ ] Floor known (cost) but never the opening position?
- [ ] Concessions extract their ceiling first and come with a condition?
- [ ] Walk-away conditions defined before the call?
- [ ] If Goldilocks: options named, tiered on different client values, in incomparable units?
- [ ] Options presented live (phone/in person), not email-only?

## Staleness note

The corpus is 2017–2019; Mall's book is 2016. Specific dollar figures — Blind's rate card (logo $15–30K, strategy $20–50K), Stark's $7.5K/6-mo coaching, the roleplay budgets, and Mall's case-study numbers (the $800K expertise option / $160K per phase, the $100K/month exclusivity price built off SuperFriendly's 2013 ~$1M gross) — are era-priced and decay; treat them as worked examples, not benchmarks. The frameworks (sequence flip, price-the-client, options-on-attention, Goldilocks options, pricing-the-moment, discount-for-uncertainty, objection scripts) are durable.
