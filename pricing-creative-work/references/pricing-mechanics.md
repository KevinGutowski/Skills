## Stark's starter formula

(Jonathan Stark — "controversial in the value pricing community, but… important for people who are just starting out.")

1. **Cost floor:** estimate what the project would cost you if you hired freelancers to do it on your behalf. "That's your floor. You cannot go below that number or you will almost certainly be losing money."
2. **Value ceiling-anchor:** establish the one-year annualized value to the client, then **divide by 10** — "a no-brainer type price… I'm going to deliver something worth a million dollars to you this year, so I can price that at $100,000 for sure."
3. **Take the greater of the two.**

Why beginners need it: they sell to their own wallet — "they can't imagine somebody would write a check that big for little old me and they talk themselves down into lower and lower fixed prices," get burned, "and hourly billing lives another day." If the value isn't comfortably above your cost, the problem is too small — solve a bigger problem (or solve a smaller one: mom-and-pop's mobile orders worth $30K/yr → you can charge $3K → don't build a website, set them up on Instacart).

## Three options priced on attention, not labor

Pick the prices first ("bottom $10,000, middle $22,000, top $50,000 — increasing order of magnitude"), *then* ask "what can I do for $50,000 that's going to move the needle? What could I guarantee?" Scope is derived from price, never the reverse.

- **Top:** most of *your attention* — "not necessarily the most labor." You take ownership: staff it, bring in outside people, buy tools, minimize what the client's people must do.
- **Middle:** collaboration — shared accountabilities, joint meetings, client implements with your oversight.
- **Bottom:** DIY — diagnose, prescribe, hand over a roadmap/report/brand guide.

Real example: Stark bid one at **$65,000 / $125,000 / $3.5 million** — the huge top jump because he'd take over implementation entirely, subcontracting a design team and an implementation team. (His own product ladder mirrors it: book → book bundle → call with him — "more attention, therefore more money… not because it's more work for me.")

## Guarantees

"All profit comes from risk" (Peter Drucker, deployed by Chris mid-negotiation). The guarantee toolkit:

- **The 3x rule (Chris):** client demands a guarantee on a $1M engagement? "If you want a guarantee, I'm going to charge you $3 million… I just triple the rate." If the metric doesn't hit, the money comes back — "That's what a guarantee is, right? I stand behind my work."
- **Pro-rata:** "Let's give it a realistic timetable. If I hit half, I make half… I'll pro-rate. How's that for a deal?" Frame as ROI: "If you give me 75 cents, I'll give you a dollar — would you do that deal? That's a 25% return. What are you getting in the bank right now?"
- **Stark's symmetric version:** "If you could guarantee it, you could charge the million. If you can't guarantee it, you charge a hundred thousand" — discounting for uncertainty (Blair Enns's phrase) is *why* the 10% works.
- **Guarantee the leading indicator you control:** you can't guarantee "we won't lose Home Depot," but if everyone agrees that, say, customer complaints about unreadable reports are a leading indicator of churn, guarantee *that*: "guarantee the piece that you can control."
- Mechanics: a guarantee ≠ a refund (the remedy can be more work); time-limit it (full refund on month one of a retainer, none after); cheap guarantees on products ("100% money back, boom, instant refund") buy trust cheaply.

## Performance / risk-share pricing as a close lever

The RFk8ZmIDrFM pivot, when the client is locked at $1,000: "I understand. $1,000 is a lot for you… I'd like to propose something else. **I'll do the video for you for free. If we get the results that you want, I'd like a percentage of the results.** No risk to you at all." Each new customer is worth $100 → "let's go half, 50 bucks [per new customer who walks in]. I take all the risk and I rely on your honesty."

The trap closes itself: the client believes the video will work (that's why they called), so unlimited upside for you suddenly looks expensive — "you're saying, please help me do this, but if it works so well, I don't want to pay you anymore." Then present the two clean options: "You pay me $4,000, we're done — you keep all the money from that point forward. Or I do it for free and take 50% of all new net revenue, because I'm taking all the risk." Client takes the $4,000 flat fee — 4x the original budget. (Soften with a cap if needed: "How about for the first year? Does that make you feel better?") Meta-lesson from the pro group: "If they're not meeting you at your price, you don't have to meet them at their deliverable" — counter-scope, not counter-price.

## Advisory retainers — selling access

(Stark, core of his business for a decade.) "Retainer" is a landmine: pre-paying for a chunk of hours "is not a retainer… that's hourly billing and it's a trap." An **advisory retainer** sells access: "my retainer clients pay me to answer their email, basically." Maybe a quarterly office visit; mostly they're buying insurance on a risky, must-be-right initiative — when a fork in the road appears, they ask which way to go, and an answer that takes you minutes saves them astronomical money downstream.

Price it **on the website like a hamburger**: "How much is the monthly advisory retainer? Oh, it's $20,000, and here's what it includes" — no value conversation; they see the price and self-select. The fee filters time vampires, and "anybody in a position to pay you five figures per month to call you occasionally is a very busy person… they just need their answer quickly. Okay, thanks. Bye."

## No change orders

"I don't do change orders. Change orders are not a thing in my world. That's phony value pricing. That's phony fixed pricing. Here's the outcome, here are the things I'm going to guarantee, and this is the price. Full stop." Fixed-price means you carry the risk — so build in profits big enough that scope creep doesn't matter, and never go back to the well. (Distinguish from genuinely *new* scope, which gets pinned and priced separately — see objection-scripts.md, the overage sequence.)

## Keep-the-lights-on transition

You can't value-price from desperation — "razor-thin margins… it's really hard to push back when you don't know how you're going to pay your rent." Stark's exit ramp from hourly: keep billing hourly as long as it takes, **but cap it at 20 hours/week**; the other 20 go to working *on* the business, not in it — positioning, marketing, specializing — until you land **one high-profit engagement** and feel what profit is ("if you're billing by the hour, you probably have no profits" — profit is what's left after you'd paid someone else to do the work). It's a virtuous cycle: profit → confidence → pushback → better clients.

## The Blind rate card (dated worked example — late-2010s numbers)

What Chris's studio Blind actually charged, as stated in RKXZ7t_RiOE: **logo $15–30K · strategy before the logo $20–50K · messaging ~$10K · applications ~$10K** ("pick three": business card, letterhead, email blast; usage guide included). On the proposal it's one line: "Line item number three. Logo. Three rounds. 18k. That's it." Context that set the floor: studio overhead ("I can only manage so many jobs per month… my rates are going to begin much higher than yours") and the top of market — Landor "get a million dollars a logo" (FedEx-tier; "green square, but it's a good green square") because at that scale the client's risk and printing exposure are enormous. Use the *structure* (strategy priced separately and larger than the artifact; flat line items; rounds capped), not the 2018 dollars.

## Confidence precedents

- **Paula Scher** drew the Citi logo on a cocktail napkin in the first meeting. To the skeptical client: "Well, it took an education that cost me six figures and 34 years of experience to draw that in 30 seconds." You're not paying for time.
- **Paul Rand** ("like a million dollars for a logo"): "Sometimes it takes me 2 weeks. Sometimes it takes a year. I'll know when I'm ready." One logo, no revisions, "you don't have to use it, but either way you have to pay me." He's not apologizing.
- "There's an issue of confidence to be able to walk into the room and say: that's the number. That's how I work. That's our policy."

## When value pricing "goes wrong"

Stark's worst case: third call with a great-fit client, he half-joked "Well, I can do anything for $200,000" — dead silence — "Seriously?" — "Done deal." The one-year project took two. On hourly that's a micromanaging nightmare ("you're sucking money out of their bank account… I've seen people fired, I've seen people sued — to me that's the immoral option"); on a fixed value price "everybody was all smiles — hey, keep going." Even gone-wrong, both sides win — Blair Enns's "double thank-you" — *if* you priced in real profit.
