## The stakeholder + entrepreneur filter

"Almost all of my clients — there's very few that would have a normal rational conversation — [you need] a **stakeholder** of the business who's an **entrepreneur**. If you're not speaking to a stakeholder, somebody's got skin in the game, they're just thinking about their boss — this will not work, because they don't know what to do. They also need to be entrepreneurial, because **a person who doesn't value their time doesn't value your time**." Meet someone who's neither a decision-maker nor entrepreneurial: "say thank you very much, let me refer you to somebody else, and leave."

The ideal buyer, demonstrated live (the conference-ticket roleplay): "a decision maker, an entrepreneur who's not afraid to take risk… when you speak to an entrepreneur, a decision maker who's not risk-averse, the maximum value can be extracted" — they take a little information and spin it into gold themselves.

## Time-vampire red flags and the dinner test

(Stark.) Red flags, visible even in a first email: they reply in a way that shows **they didn't read what you wrote**; "could we just jump on a call to brainstorm? **I just want to pick your brain**" — "people who have time to sit around picking someone's brain are not busy people," and they usually want validation, not advice; they ignore or reject pushback; bad listeners. "You'll know at the end of half an hour if you're in trouble."

The summary filter: **"I only work with people I'd like to have dinner with."** "Would you have dinner with this person? If not, why are you doing business with them?"

## Gatekeepers and the range float

The person "tasked to go out and find me a designer" can't answer the why questions — "you have to get past that person, and if you don't, you're almost certainly not going to get the job — and you're probably lucky if you don't." Ask: "Who does have the answers? Could we set up a meeting with them?" Find "the person whose money it is."

If you're stuck with the gatekeeper and still want to bid, float the range: "I can think of three different ways we could engage on this. At the high end we're talking like **$250,000**, and at the low end is probably **50,000**. **Does that fit into what you're picturing?**" You get a reaction (sticker shock at which end?), and "you planted the seed that there's going to be a quarter-of-a-million-dollar option on that proposal." If they bite — "$250,000 for what?" — you've earned the value conversation: "if we save one client, and Home Depot's paying you a lot of money, it'd be well worth $250,000." Odds stay low through a gatekeeper; usually pass ("I hate writing proposals").

## High-risk projects are the value-pricing fit

"When I see a high-risk project come through the door, it's a no-brainer" — the Blockbuster-facing-Netflix moment, an existential, urgent, expensive-to-get-wrong situation. Value pricing cuts both ways: it pushes you toward bigger clients ("I'd rather work with a regional chain of pizza restaurants than a mom and pop") because the client must be able to capture the investment back.

## Fear of loss — the inbound-call mindset

"You go into the transaction thinking, I don't want to lose this — assume you already have it. So you're working against a fear of loss. Why don't you walk into the room like: **I didn't have this job.** Maybe it'll work, maybe it won't. If they come to me with the right terms and I like them and I believe in what they're doing, yes, I will help them. Otherwise I'm out of here." (People act more from fear of loss than desire for gain — so don't hand the dynamic to the client.) The fish on the line you're terrified to lose "was just a rock… an old leather shoe."

Same move on inbound budget pressure: **"You didn't have that $1,000 before they called you. You were going to make life work regardless.** Don't fall into that mindset — 'because they called, I have to close the job' — that puts you in a horrible, disadvantageous position." Prerequisite (from the pro member who raised it): "you got to have a **runway to be able to tell people no**" — see keep-the-lights-on in pricing-mechanics.md.

## Confidence: "the first time I charged $10,000"

"Everybody always thinks: because I'm this person, I've done this, I've had these awards — no, it doesn't matter, cuz the first day I started, I wanted to charge these rates anyways… **The first time I charged $10,000 for strategy was the first time I charged $10,000 for strategy. I didn't do it for 100, and then 200, and 250. I just went for it.** And when they paid, I'm like, oh, this is really good. I need to charge $20,000 for strategy." Then he discovered "some other fool's charging a million dollars — what is wrong with me?"

The price must be "relative to the risk of the company" — and remember the buying psychology: "Clients don't choose the best option. They choose the least risky option. You need to appear as the least risky thing. So just ask them, 'What criteria are you using to make your decision?' You should already know this" — credit checks, referrals, social proof, evidence of expertise ("I wrote the book, literally").

## Context makes numbers high or low

"This is what my business coach taught me: **no matter what the number is, it's neither high nor low. Context allows us to decide.**" (The marker in his hand looks five inches long; photographed next to a building it's an art installation.) Never throw a number before the value exists: "Would you consider giving me $30,000?" is unanswerable — but "Is $30,000 a lot of money compared to quadrupling your business?" answers itself. "If you throw out a number without me understanding the value — the *client* needs to understand the value."

## Let them name the price (it comes in at the top)

The live demo: attendees paid $1,100 for the conference; asked "based on the value you got, name your own price," the answers came back at or above what they paid. "When you name a price, you could be underselling yourself. When you talk about value and you let the client decide how valuable it is, **in all instances it's at the top end.**" (This is the closing half of "when you say it you're selling; when they say it you're closing.")

## Pricing the moment + scarcity (Mall — school difference)

All quotes: Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016. Mall's twist on price-the-customer: "As I'm pricing a customer, I can't help but think about another crucial part of the equation: me. How badly do I need this work?… For me, value pricing is even more than pricing the customer. It's about pricing the moment." Same-project proof that nothing has an inherent price: "My agency once designed and built a website for $18,000, and we did the same project for a different client for $230,000 that year."

The engine is the **scarcity principle**: "The largest factor in your current value is your ability to walk away from a prospect." Full bank account + stacked portfolio = "If you won't take their project for $10K, the only recourse they have is to offer you $20K, then $50K, then $100K, until you finally take it—or don't." A set rate fails in both directions — at peak demand "an industry standard price makes you seem less scarce than you actually might be"; and, the contested half: "if you're struggling to pay the bills and have no projects on deck, you take what you can get. The scarcity principle works both ways." **Mark the fork:** the Do school calls down-pricing on a dry pipeline the race to zero — its answer is keep-the-lights-on runway so you never price desperate (see pricing-mechanics.md). Present both positions.

## The Esther story + pricing as a muscle (Mall)

Mall's go-away price that came back (Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016): booked five months out, he tried to repel a referral with a "Save the Date" contract — "You agree to pay a one-time, additional, nonrefundable fee of $7,000." "Esther never responded. Five days later, I received a check for $7,000 in the mail. I learned a terrific lesson that day: people will pay for things they value, no matter how wildly out of reach you might think it is."

The training principle: "Pricing is a muscle. You need to keep exercising it to get stronger."

The tempering counter-position — value pricing is not maximal pricing: "value pricing is about making the price match the value. If the value of what you're doing is small, it's okay for the price to be small. If the value is immense, the price should follow suit."

Book credit, from Mike Monteiro's foreword: "You are going to make more money from this book than Dan Mall ever will." Why: "that's what Dan does for a living. He builds value… his clients will make more money from his work than he ever will, especially if he does it right."

## The walkaway

Chris's Hollywood story: invited in by a former client's new partner, drove out, swanky office, and the first words were "So, what are you going to do for us?" — "I'm out of here." Every power-player stereotype confirmed; he left politely without answering a question. The friend who stayed signed a "partnership" that was a name with no equity and spent years trying to escape. The lesson: "As soon as I see somebody exhibit those traits, I'll ask a few questions to make sure I'm not crazy, and then I'm leaving… I've had feelings like that on really big jobs — seven-figure jobs — and I'm like, I don't like the feeling, I'm out." (To his own sales team: "If you like it so much, you do the job, not me.")

## Losing well (Mall)

The Mega Agency case study ends in a loss — by design as much as accident (Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016): "Since I've started using value pricing, my loss rate on proposals has gone way up—my agency lands significantly fewer projects than when we had a standard hourly rate. (But we make way more money now.)" Across a few hundred lost proposals: "Only one of those rejections has involved an objection to price." Of the rejection letter itself: "There isn't one word about price." The options made the client "reexamine the project at a fundamental level. (I'm adding value without even being hired yet.)" The good loss: "If you're going to lose a project, this is the way to do it: when a customer says, 'We see the value. This isn't the right thing, but we'll be back.'" (They came back.) High proposal loss-rate at higher money is the expected shape of value pricing, not a failure signal.

## Specialize or be a commodity

(Stark.) Hourly billing "sets you up as an apples-to-apples commodity — this guy's $10 an hour, this guy's $100, I'll get the $10 guy." The escape is becoming **the go-to person for something** — a skill, an audience, a problem ("nonprofits, who have a lot of money, by the way"). "A wristwatch is a commodity — you can get a Timex for $5, but people still buy Patek Philippe… You're not buying a watch, you're buying a Rolex. How do I turn myself into a Rolex?"

And the closing line of the whole corpus, Chris on what's actually for sale: **"You guys sell what you do. I sell what the world can do."**
