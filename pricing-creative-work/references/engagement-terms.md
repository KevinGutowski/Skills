## Money up front: the deposit

"Most studios, us included, insist on a deposit based on a percentage of the project before we start work. Depending on the project's size, it could range from 25% to 50% of the total project cost. Design studios aren't a high-margin business. Getting that money up front gives us the running room to get the project going and dedicate people to your job."

Larger projects: payments at agreed-upon milestones, with a final payment on completion — but see the completion rule below before you sign that.

## The kill fee

The clause Monteiro refuses to negotiate: "Of all the clauses in our master services agreement, none has caused more grief than the kill fee. To sum it up: if you hire us for a project and your company wants out of the contract for reasons that have nothing to do with the quality of our work or service, you owe us a percentage of the remaining project budget."

Why it's non-negotiable: "if you've committed to do a project, we've reserved that time on our calendar for you. We've stopped looking for jobs to fill that timeframe as it's now yours." Quality failure is different — "if we're working on the project and doing a terrible job, you can flat out fire us. That's on us." But if the client eighty-sixes the project for reasons beyond the designer's control, "the kill fee kicks in. Because now we've got a hole in our schedule, and we need to protect ourselves against that."

The analogy that sells it: "It's no different from when your doctor charges you for missing an appointment without twenty-four-hours notice. You're not giving them enough time to fill that appointment, and you're affecting their livelihood."

The script when legal pushes: "if we're working on a contract together and your lawyer says, 'I'm gonna strike the kill fee,' do me a favor and tell them not to bother, because I'm not budging on that one. If you were in my shoes, you wouldn't either."

## Define completion as something within your control

The milestone trap: tying your final payment to events you can't cause. "I advise designers to define completion as something within their control. For example, if your designers are delivering front-end code for your internal team to implement, completion should be defined as the delivery of final code, and not implementation or launch. Because those things are beyond the designer's control." If the client's implementation team gets "called away to put out a fire on another project, we can't in good conscience have the design team waiting for them… to get paid."

Rule: payment triggers on *your* deliverable (code delivered, files handed over), never on the client's act (launch, implementation, approval-by-committee).

## How designers determine estimates — the nine factors

"If you ask five designers for estimates on the same project, you'll get five prices that vary wildly." The data points behind a proposal:

1. **Scope.** "The goals, size, and complexity of the project—the amount of work required to solve the problem." By far the biggest component — and "once it gets decided and signed off on by both parties, it doesn't increase. We're building what we're building."
2. **Experience.** "Experienced designers are more expensive… You benefit from all the mistakes they made in the past that they won't repeat with you."
3. **Value.** "The more influence the work has on your organization and the world at large, the more the work should cost."
4. **Availability.** "Supply and demand are factors." (Mall's "pricing the moment," in four words.)
5. **Perceived awesomeness of the project.** "Awesomeness is in the eye of the designer. Some clients try to abuse this by describing the work as a 'great portfolio piece.'" The flip side is the secrecy premium: "if the work is secret and the designer can't promote it in their portfolio, it'll probably cost more to compensate for the lost value."
6. **Size and complexity of the client's organization.** "Coordinating schedules and communication takes longer with twenty people than five. If it takes time, it takes money."
7. **Timeline.** "I've had a few clients think that needing something fast means we charge less since they're in and out quicker. Because FedEx also charges you less for delivery overnight when your package doesn't spend as much time on their trucks."
8. **Costs of doing business.** "skilled people and productive offices cost money… Rent and salaries are higher in places like San Francisco, New York, and London."
9. **The asshole tax.** "This is a thing. Everything else being equal, if you're a jerk during the business development process, designers assume you'll be a jerk during the project, and they'll charge you more to compensate for the pain in their ass. On the other hand, an angel discount often exists for nonprofits doing something admirable and previous clients with a good working relationship. If you try to skeeze your way to a discount, you will definitely be quoted a higher rate."

The consequence for comparing bids: "you're not comparing apples to apples. You're seeing an intricate model of supply and demand and timing. The company that sent you a bid for $100K may have bid $80K if you'd shown up a month earlier." And the closing rule: "nothing is more important than picking the right people to work with… **Choose who you want to work with first, and then decide whether you'll pay them what they ask.**"

On multiple bids: it's fine — "feel free to let the high bidder know they're charging more than someone else. Ask them why they're worth more. If they can't tell you, it's a red flag."

## Budget first: the car-dealer story

Young Monteiro translates at the dealership while his immigrant father test-drives a Dodge St. Regis "ridiculously beyond our budget" ("Shut up. Ask him if the seats are heated"). When the truth finally comes out, the salesman's reply is the whole lesson: **"Why didn't you tell me what you could afford?"**

Applied: "Buying design, like buying a car, is a financial transaction… budget is the first thing we ask prospective clients." To clients who fear that disclosing the number means that's what the work will cost: "That's partially true. I'll tell you what you can get for that amount. Then we can talk about whether you need that much design… that number tells me how to guide you toward the appropriate solution and avoid solutions outside of your price range." No number yet? "That's okay, we'll discuss a few options. Some below your price range, some above."

The closer: "Tell me your budget. It saves both of us time and money. And if you don't think you have a budget, someone's probably withholding it from you. I just blew your mind."

## Refusing bake-offs and spec work

A dream client shortlisted three studios and "asked each studio to create a concept for the site and even offered to pay for the work. (In the industry, we call this a bake-off. It's a terrible practice.)" Mule refused — not for lack of wanting the job, but because "Our work relies on research as the first step of the design process… Until we've done so, we're guessing."

They won anyway — not because the argument convinced the client, but because "The other two studios, who agreed to do initial concepts without any research, did. Their concepts were nothing close to what that client needed. How could they have been? **Beware a designer who tells you they can solve your problem without doing the legwork of research. That's not good design, it's a magic trick.** They're either lying to you or themselves."

The principle that follows: "**A designer only has to win a job once.**" Once hired, "they've earned the right to succeed at it" — no re-auditioning at every milestone, no rogue competing concepts from internal designers.

Spec work, per the book's glossary: "asking your designer to do the work first, with payment dependent on getting the work approved. Which is like agreeing to pay your therapist only if you stop doing terrible things like asking designers to work on spec."

## Money at termination

When a client fires an outside designer, the rules are simpler than they look — "You pay people for the work they did. This is exactly the kind of situation that should be addressed in the contract… specifically in the kill fee."

- **Approved work stays paid.** "you most definitely owe people for the work you've approved. If they did good work for two-thirds of the project and you signed off on it and paid them, that money is gone." "But it ended up failing" doesn't claw it back: "the fact that you signed off on stuff means that you were satisfied."
- **Work in progress** is trickier, contract-governed, with one guideline: "if you're going to use the work, even after firing them, pay them for it. But if you're not using the work, well, fuck 'em."

## Things cost more when they matter more

"…and this is right and good. Why are some logos $99 and others $1 million (or, like the 2008 rebranding of British Petroleum, $211 million)? The million-dollar logo didn't take ten thousand times as long to make. Say it with me: it's about the value." For an established organization, "you pay more for design because the cost of fucking things up is much higher."

Bonus, on paying in-house designers: "pay your designers as much as you pay your engineers… And pay the ones willing to disagree with you twice as much as the ones who aren't."

## Convergences with the other schools

- **BP logo = price-the-client.** Monteiro's $99-vs-$211M logo is the same argument as Do's Paul Rand / $40M-printing-budget example and the risk framing in value-conversation.md — third independent arrival.
- **Budget-first vs. Mall's "money, not budget."** Both ask about money early; Mall deliberately avoids the *word* "budget" ("connotes cheap"), Monteiro leads with it. Same move, different vocabulary — a wording fork, not a strategy fork.
- **Bake-off refusal ↔ Mall's count-the-competition** (">3 studios pitching → run") and Enns's win-without-pitching: don't audition with free (or even paid) concepts.
- **"Choose who you want to work with first"** is the buyer-side mirror of the Stark dinner test / Mall meal test — pick the person, then settle the price.
- **Availability factor = Mall's "pricing the moment"**; the asshole tax / angel discount is price-the-client applied to temperament.
- **Kill fee + deposit** extend the corpus's keep-the-lights-on runway (qualifying-and-mindset.md): contractual slack instead of savings-account slack.
