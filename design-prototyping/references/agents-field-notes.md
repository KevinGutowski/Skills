# Agents-Tier Field Notes — Cursor School & MDS Workflow

Long-form field notes distilled in `SKILL.md`'s agents tier. Everything here is verbatim from the skill's source notes.

## Field amplifiers & the mock-to-code caution

Two field amplifiers (Evil Martians' "weeks → days" design-engineering case, 2025): **prototype with the full dataset, not sample points** ("working with the full dataset helped us make better design decisions early on… I even tested a few bold chart types I'd never bother coding manually"), and **prototype in the target production stack** so handoff is copy-paste — "the frontend engineer… started by copying the code from my prototype." The expert-led qualifier holds: "my technical knowledge, multiplied by AI" — AI removes friction, not judgment.

A field caution for the mock-to-code direction (Paul Stamatiou testing Claude Design against his own app mock, Apr 2026 — https://x.com/Stammy/status/2045186427491930523): the agent's first pass scored "65/100. promising though" — text weights and sizes "all over the place and inconsistent," colors and rounded corners not respected, spacing inconsistencies, parts of the design missing, a gradient "way too large." When you hand an agent a *finished design* rather than a prompt, **diff the output against the mock element-by-element** — type weights/sizes, exact colors, corner radii, spacing, completeness, gradient scale — before iterating on anything else. The collaborator-not-designer stance covers visual QA too.

## The endpoint of this tier — Cursor's design org

**The endpoint of this tier — Cursor's design org** (Ryo Lu, Head of Design; jdahl Substack 2025, Creator Economy 2025, a16z Show notes — https://jdahl.substack.com/p/cursors-ryo-lu-on-soulful-design · https://creatoreconomy.so/p/design-to-code-with-cursor-head-of-design-ryo-lu · https://www.podchemy.com/notes/ryo-lu-cursor-ai-turns-designers-to-developers-46831311791): designers ship code instead of mocks, and the prototype *is* the product. The stance restated from the material side — **"Mocks and renders show possibilities. Prototypes show reality"**; "you learn by making, not planning"; Figma "felt like painting," designing in code is "sculpting clay or finding David in the marble." Their version of collaborator-not-designer: agents get you "60–70% complete on the first attempt," and **"the difference between slop and soulful products is iteration and taste. Most people stop at the first output, rather than using it to begin"** — taste is "a process of selection," the human specifying "what is good, what is right, how I want to do it." Organizationally it collapses handoff entirely: roles between designers/PMs/engineers are deliberately "muddy… we just do what it takes based on each person's unique strengths and use the AI agent to tie everything together" — the team-process side of this school (Linear's reference-not-deliverable version) lives in `shape-up`'s Linear-school extension.

## Field notes from inside that school

Field notes from inside that school (Ryo Lu; Jin Park/Notion; Catherine/Ramp — "Building with Cursor," 2025):
- **The baby-cursor pattern:** keep a reusable lightweight prototyping environment — never ideate in "your like gigantic codebase that takes… 30 seconds to refresh." "We built cursor by using cursor to build baby cursor that makes cursor" — a stripped replica that "feels like the thing you have, maybe even better."
- **AI interactions can't be faked in mocks:** "the input, the output, the context, the response, what's in it — they all matter. You just can't fake them." AI behavior questions force the code tier.
- **Ramp's three-tier fidelity ladder:** standalone vibes-only stack (Astro + Tailwind vars matched to Figma tokens + Alpine; ~3 days to set up) → a sandbox preloaded with the real component library and templates ("these are our real components… a little bit closer to the level of fidelity") → the real web repo with real data, "as close to production as possible." Adoption mechanics: one designer's 15-minute Loom ("80% of a working prototype… using three tools I'd never even heard of") spread it; a design-engineering lead ran group setup sessions — "you just need to sit down and do it together," building "critical mass" instead of expecting solo ramp-up.
- **Jin Park's Figma-MCP pipeline:** "previbe cleanup" (group, name, auto-layout the Figma first so it translates) → a one-to-one static pass → fix tokens (the AI substitutes Tailwind defaults — "pull the hex values and apply them as custom arbitrary values") → a manual icon/SVG and spacing pass. Net: a 3-week animation job "down to like literally 45 minutes."
- **UI archaeology:** recreate old interfaces to surface "what kind of constraints were philosophies they were thinking about."
- **Medium-matching:** walks + bullet lists for conceptual problems; Figma when you "need exact pixel placement" and fast 2D layout iteration; code when states and AI behavior matter.

## MDS workflow notes

**MDS workflow notes** (Matt D. Smith, Shift Nudge — Dive Club interview, youtube.com/watch?v=K_7ECqNlTtE; Shift Nudge YouTube 7zBp4ZNDto4, Uno5dpotRgo, cpLkOqHUQyc, k8dcRRgA3T8):
- **Tool-making reflex:** when exploring a visual idea, build a small parametric tool first — his pixel-icon exploration opened with "I'd like to build a small tool that functions as a bit map icon builder." Ask the agent to "generate all these parameters as modifiable variables in a control panel versus like hard coding every single one of them" (the tuning-panel move, pattern 3). Agent sessions lack undo history, so **save iteration states** — "keep saving these so we're not redoing them"; build your own command-D. Batch-generate then cull: "five vastly different hover effects," keep the fun one, iterate on *it* — "you're just kind of trying to fine-tune your taste."
- **85%-in-Figma, finish in code:** "I will only get it to, you know, I don't know, 85 90% complete" in Figma, then move to class names ("I'm adding like PT30… Tailwind class names") and figure out the rest where it's real. Hover states get designed *only* in code ("that hover state's a perfect example of something that I probably would just do in code every time… it's all about how it feels"); and the round-trip runs in reverse too — "I'll take a screenshot and then bring it back into Figma and adjust it." Diagrams are agent context: "This is a diagram I made for Claude."
- **Prototype vs system components — know which you're building:** "System components optimize for flexibility and minimal duplication and developer handoff. Prototype components sometimes might even be broken and they optimize for animation fidelity" — "you just need to know which one you're solving for." When Smart Animate misbehaves, "it's usually a naming issue" (same layer names + order across frames). For realistic assets, steal production SVGs via devtools ("hover the SVG tag").
- **Components only when they pay** (the Stamatiou auto-layout caution, MDS edition): "too many components too much Auto layout too early it can constrain your creativity"; he's "painted myself into an auto layout Corner" mid-redesign. Gate process by complexity: ask "is this a low medium or high complexity project in terms of feature set or team size" — simple/small goes straight to visual design; complex/large earns wireframe-first. And keep the fork trail: "pixels are free — just duplicate it and move on," deciding by elimination while "leaving a trail of your design decisions along the way."

## Dive Club field notes (2025–26) — full quotes

All citations: (speaker, Dive Club podcast, YouTube ID). Distilled in `SKILL.md`'s "Dive Club field notes" block.

- **Brian Lovin (Notion, dvEwb1Ajkwo) — playground mechanics.** The kit is deliberately 80/20: "we've tried to do like the 80/20 thing. Like get it pretty close. Like we have a sidebar that looks kind of like Notion sidebar, buttons and drop-downs that are kind of like Notion buttons." One shared codebase makes prototypes communal: "You can like go and poke around other people's stuff and like yoink cool interactions from other people's prototypes or just like duplicate theirs and riff on it." Fidelity is per-designer, per-question: Will Dawson "more or less recreated like a very simplified version of the Notion editor with slash commands and inline AI" because "the only way to really feel what it's going to be like to interact with AI while you're typing a page… is to actually be in an editor." The medium shift: "it just moves from, 'Hey, check out this Figma URL.' to, 'Hey, check out this deploy preview.'"
- **Geoffrey Litt (zJf0UeCwQqE) — jigs.** "I call these tools jigs from woodworking. You know, you make like a tool just for the purpose of making one project" — affordable now only "cuz AI exists." His migration example: a long port script he couldn't trust became "can you make me a command center that will walk me through the process of doing this port myself but visualize the entire process."
- **Steve Ruiz (tldraw, 3SvL0r-Lhh8) — Make Real.** Draw a website, "select it and click this button called make real and it would create a website." The prompt persona: "you're a 4,000-year-old senior web developer who loves their designers and wants them to be happy." Iteration is drawing on the output: "what I really want to be able to do is just draw on top of my screen… big fat yellow arrows type of stuff." Reference ripping: "put screenshots next to it and say like hey make this look like you know stripe.com."
- **Luis Ouriach (Pn2G7JhxNKc) — start from anywhere.** "You might start a document, you might start in a canvas, you might start in the front end… ultimately you're going to be bouncing through the tools." Internal phrase: "not faster, but further." Division of labor: "the canvas is very important for ideation, collaboration and general direction exploration, but the browser is where we commit to it."
- **Kyle Santos (HcLz3ikw-n0) — effect studios + Leva.** Hunting an animated state he couldn't find a reference for: "I just ended up building a studio basically that gave me a bunch of options to play around with." The on-demand control-panel recipe lives in global memory: "Whenever I say give me controls to tweak something about UI… you should know to go grab the leave a [Leva] component, bring it into our app and ask me any clarifying questions."
- **Josh Puckett (wym3V9FycTk) — storyboard-as-prompt.** "Command shift C, copy as a PNG, put that in the Claude and be like, cool, here's a storyboard of like what I want to create." Why not dial it in there: "Figma's a lie. It's not your actual code base. Even how it represents color and type is different than how that is actually going to be implemented in a browser." And on his own storyboards: "These actually are too high fidelity. They should just be color blocks… All I'm trying to express is my idea here for… the interaction model."
- **Andy Madrick (Notion, IfPK0LwbX_0) — stakeholder prototypes and the production seam.** Editable prototypes: "different folks at Notion are able to go in, click around… change the titles… change the section headers… and then they can take screenshots and send it back to me. And then we're all like co-designing together… at the end of the day, you're still like on Apple's rails" (bounded co-design, like rearranging an iPhone home screen). Start-in-code threshold: "the bigger the feature, the more likely I am to start in code. But if it's a smaller thing, like a modal or polish… I find Figma to still be really" useful. Hedged shipping: "we had to build three different versions of this because internally we're like, this new modal variant is really sick, but it could be confusing" — and on which won: "I hate to say that the safe one did." Playground→production: "I took the directory that this was in in the playground, fed it to an LLM, and said, 'Make this work in production.' And it one-shotted it… flawless" — agents "can be really good at acting as translation layers" for a non-engineer designer. Source of truth: "you'd be surprised how many Figma files in Notion are just screenshots of the product, and then like a little chunk of it is… a new Figma frame on a little tab."
- **Katie Dill (Stripe, Dpy-yyYXhgU) — Protodash.** "We built a internal tool we call protodash where we can create AI prototypes extremely rapidly that look and feel like the stripe dashboard" — context-first because "no product is like ever felt by your user in isolation. It's like they usually got there from something else or they're bouncing off to something next." Review stance: "Please don't present. Please don't pitch your ideas. Please don't take a week making a deck… I just want to see it like a user."
