## The Like button — graph-level testing, full story

Blocked state (Soleio, PBS): "the Like button was a feature that we had in what I like to call product purgatory for, I think, multiple years." Why: "We were concerned that the Like button would cannibalize commenting… And as it turned out, our intuition was just dead wrong."

The experiment (Soleio, Design MBA): "we chose two pairs of networks where there was high friend density" — Colombia and Iceland got the feature; "the controls are going to be I think it was H vuela and Norway" [caption garble: "H vuela" = Venezuela]. A purpose of running it as a real experiment at all: "we need to dampen the all the debate club that was happening at the company."

The result: "the like button actually increased the likelihood of people commenting on a given post that it almost Act that it's like a social lubricant it gave people permission to leave a comment because they would see this little blue pool appear beneath posts."

Lesson: social mechanics only reveal themselves where the graph is dense enough for network effects to appear — and the metric to watch is the adjacent behavior you fear the feature will suppress (or hope it will unlock), not the feature's own usage.

## Project Motion — code wins arguments

(Soleio, Dive Club.) A hackathon-built Flash video recorder; the head of engineering ordered it sunset. Soleio instead demoed it to Adam D'Angelo and Zuckerberg — and won. "code wins arguments was a poster that we had at Facebook."

The mechanism — a working artifact accretes inevitability: "kind of creating this Aura of inevitability around it because it was functional it was working people inside the company could use it and we were running out of obstacles keeping us from shipping it."

The general claim: "how small teams that are enabled to ship and… get to production quickly are often the ones that can make huge impacts on the direction of a company."

## The songwriter model

(Soleio, Dive Club — both episodes.) Treat design output like a songwriter's catalog, not a masterpiece-in-progress: "sometimes you'll solve a problem and it's like Ah that's not quite right but this this is actually kind of interesting so I'm gonna Park this over here maybe I'll come back to it maybe I'll self plagiarize later on."

The designers worth emulating are "designers who are prolific that just are very generative that don't fall in love with their work because they're producing so much of it they can't pick a favorite child." The failure mode: designers who "marry themselves to that first song."

On impermanence of shipped work: "it's like making sand castles it's going to get washed away pretty fast even the really great ones that's fine."

## Two buckets of designers

(Soleio, Dive Club.) "you can segment designers into these two buckets there are people who have experienced getting work out there in the world and bending in the Wind of reality and being accountable for said work… and then there's everyone else everyone else who sort of sits in the safety of producing a blueprint producing a faximile of a thing um making concept cars rather than shipping product" [caption garble: "faximile" = facsimile].

With current tooling, "designers who are not shipping are running out of excuses" — and "there's nobody faster than the designer who's shipping product."

## Granola — utility-first prototypes

(Soleio, Dive Club.) On his own agent-built personal tools: "these are things that I could actually use and that I did use on my own device you know no craft at all applied to it because that sort of defeated the point." Utility prototypes earn their keep by being used, not by being finished — polish would have answered a question nobody was asking.

## Jurassic Park moments

(Soleio, Design MBA.) On seeing Evan Wallace's earliest Figma multiplayer prototypes: "when you get to see the first version of a thing that is gooey and harmless and is going to need a lot of work and a lot of growth but man it's a predator." Judge an early prototype by the size of the bet it embodies, not its current state — the multiplayer bet "forgave a lot of the performance issues."

## Dogfooding kills features too

(Soleio, Design MBA.) Internal shipping is a filter, not just a polish loop: "sometimes the dog food didn't taste great the awesome button which is the code name for this project was a bizarrely cursed project." A prototype that survives building but dies in dogfood died correctly.

## Capacity for speed (and its trust trade-off)

(Soleio, Cults & Culture.) "that's how we thought about design at at Facebook was a it was a capacity for Speed how can we like prototype and build something so we don't spend a bunch of engineering resources on it… we would ask designers to to to push the production like write code on their first day."

The honest trade-off: "if you move really rapidly that's sometimes hard to align with trust building it can be unpredictable and I think that trust often requires predictability."

## Software is malleable

(Soleio, Dive Club — re an Andy Allen quote-tweet.) "there's nothing intrinsic about software that requires a ton of deliberation it's malleable it can evolve if you get it wrong in hour one you can get it right an hour or two you can course correct we're not shipping software on CD ROMs around the world anymore."

## The ship-first school, scoped

Soleio names the school explicitly (SPC fireside, to Steve Ruiz): "you and I are of a common school of like designers who ship… you have to kind of confront reality head on rather than in the safety of the ivory tower."

Scope note — this is a *school*, not a settled average: hold it by name against the craft-school positions already in this corpus (Stamatiou's SLC bar, Puckett's depth spectrum in `design-principles`/`design-craft`). Soleio's own boundary is that speed is for colliding with reality sooner, and dogfood — not taste-talk — is the evidence layer.
