# Portrait And Field Lighting

## Portrait Key Light

A good portrait starts with one usable key light. Extra lights are rescue or refinement, not the foundation.

Key setup:
- Use a source large enough to soften skin texture but directional enough to show facial shape.
- Place it above and to one side.
- Watch the shadow side of the nose, cheek, brow, and chin.
- If the face is flat, move the key farther to the side.
- If eyes go dark, lower the source or move it forward until catchlights return.
- If half the face blocks up, move the source forward or add fill.

Classic portrait diagnosis:
- Too frontal: face lacks contour.
- Too high: eye sockets fall into shadow.
- Too far side: the shadow side collapses and the key triangle disappears.
- Too small/hard: skin texture and nose/chin shadows dominate.

Fill should reduce contrast without removing the form. Use a reflector, bounce, second light, or flash exposure compensation; stop when the face still has shape.

## Catchlights

Catchlights are small but diagnostic. If the eyes look dull, find or add a reflected source:

- Move the subject toward open sky, a window, or a bright wall.
- Raise/lower the key until the eyes catch it without brow shadow.
- Add fill flash or a reflector.
- Avoid placing the source so far to the side that one eye loses the catchlight.

For portraits, the catchlight is often a better first check than the light stand diagram.

## Outdoor Portraits

Use the environment as part of the lighting setup.

Common moves:
- Cloud cover: nature's diffuser.
- Shade/open sky: soft light with fewer squints; watch blue/green color casts.
- Sun behind subject: rim/hair separation and comfortable eyes, but face needs fill.
- Sun low/front: warm direct face light and blue sky, but watch squinting.
- Noon sun: avoid for faces unless you can diffuse, shade, reflect, or fill.

Backlit portrait sequence:

1. Place the subject with sun behind or side-behind for rim/hair separation.
2. Expose for the background/sky if it matters.
3. Add face light with fill flash or a reflector.
4. Check catchlights and shadow density.
5. Reduce fill if the flash look becomes obvious.

## Indoor And Event Flash

On-camera direct flash is a small frontal source: useful when there is no time, but usually harsh and flat.

Improve it:
- Bounce from a neutral ceiling/wall to make the source larger.
- Rotate flash head slightly forward for moving event work.
- Use flash exposure compensation; fill should not overpower ambient unless that is the style.
- Avoid colored bounce surfaces unless the color cast is intentional.
- If there is no usable bounce surface, use a modifier, off-camera flash, or reflector.

Failure checks:
- Dark background with flashed face: balance ambient exposure or bounce to light the room.
- Orange/green room with cool flash face: gel flash or correct mixed color deliberately.
- Slow recycle: lower flash output, raise ISO, use closer bounce, add batteries, or reduce diffusion loss.

## Product And Still-Life Practice

Practice with simple objects before a paid or fragile subject:

- Orange or textured object: compare hard side light vs soft front light.
- Glossy phone/tablet: move light and camera until glare appears/disappears.
- Metal utensil: use a white card as the reflected source.
- Glass cup: try bright-field and dark-field edges.
- Black object on black: add a controlled rim.
- White object on white: separate edge/background and preserve highlights.

The point is not to memorize diagrams. Build the habit: surface -> shadow/reflection -> camera/light geometry -> fill/control.
