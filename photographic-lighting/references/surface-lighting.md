# Surface Lighting

## Matte And Textured

Matte and textured subjects mainly need shadow control. Direct reflections are scattered, so the viewer reads shape from tonal variation and texture from micro-shadows.

Use harder side/top-side light when the assignment is to reveal texture: wood grain, fabric weave, food texture, stone, skin character, scratches, embossed details. Use softer front-ish light when the assignment is to flatter or simplify: beauty portraits, clean product shapes, or low-drama catalog images.

If a textured subject looks flat:
- Move the key farther off camera axis.
- Reduce fill.
- Make the source smaller or farther away if texture should bite.
- Lower the camera/light enough that surface relief casts visible micro-shadows.

If a textured subject looks too harsh:
- Enlarge the source with diffusion, bounce, softbox, umbrella, shade, or clouds.
- Bring the source closer so it is effectively larger.
- Add fill from the shadow side.
- Rotate the subject so the strongest texture is not perpendicular to the key.

## Metal

Polished metal is predictable because it behaves like a mirror. You are photographing the metal and the environment reflected in it.

Workflow:

1. Lock camera position first; the family of angles depends on camera-subject geometry.
2. Decide the metal tone: bright, dark, gradient, or controlled stripe.
3. Place a bright card/source in the family of angles for bright metal.
4. Keep bright sources out of that family for dark metal.
5. Use large cards, panels, or diffusion surfaces as reflected objects; do not rely only on direct beams.
6. For curved metal, expect many families of angles; make the reflected environment intentional.

Bright flat metal: fill the relevant family of angles with a white card, diffusion panel, or large source.

Dark flat metal: move the source outside that family and let the metal reflect dark surroundings.

Round metal: use larger controlled reflections, often strips or broad panels, because every visible part faces a different angle.

Polarizer warning: lens polarizers can help with some reflected environments, but metal direct reflection is not usually the main polarized case. Do not expect a polarizer alone to fix metal glare.

## Glass And Liquids

Glass is transparent and reflective, so front-surface lighting often gives distracting hot spots without defining the object. Edge definition is the first job.

Bright-field glass:
- Bright background fills the frame.
- Keep the glass edges dark by keeping light away from the edge-defining limits of the family of angles.
- Use when you want black/dark outlines on a clean light background.

Dark-field glass:
- Dark background fills the frame.
- Put light at the edge-defining limits so glass edges glow.
- Use when you want luminous outlines against black.

Rules:
- Set the background and edge relationship before adding props.
- If the glass disappears, increase edge contrast; do not just add front light.
- If reflections distract, move the source/camera/card out of the family of angles or flag it.
- If a label/liquid must read, treat it as a second subject with its own exposure and reflection problem.

## White-On-White And Black-On-Black

These are separation problems.

White-on-white:
- Preserve highlight detail with exposure discipline.
- Keep the background or subject edge a different tone so shape does not disappear.
- A true white background with a slightly darker subject often still reads as white-on-white; a gray background can read as white-on-gray.

Black-on-black:
- Give the subject a visible edge, rim, or controlled reflection.
- Put something bright where the black surface can reflect it.
- Keep fill low enough that the scene still reads black.

In both cases, the viewer needs edges before nuance. Solve edge separation, then tune texture, mood, and exact tone.
