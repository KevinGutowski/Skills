# Objection Scripts — Verbatim Lines, Keyed by Objection

All lines verified against the transcripts (Do/Stark) or the book text (Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016). "Whoever says the price first wins" — walk in knowing your number; if you never say a price, "he can't negotiate with you cuz you've given him no amount of money to negotiate with." You get to determine the price; they get to determine if it's fair for them.

## "What's your rate?"

Stark: **"I say, I don't have one."** Then let the silence stretch — don't fill the pregnant pause. When they ask how you do bill: "I'll price the project for you so you'll know in advance how much it's going to be. Is that acceptable?" They always say yes — "nobody wants to make a big purchasing decision based on an estimate. Everyone would much prefer to have a price." (Shoe-store test: "How much are these shoes?" "I'll tell you later — wear them for a few months.")

**Three-school verbatim convergence flag:** Mall's published script opens with the identical four words. "Client: What's your rate? / Me: I don't have one. / Client: So how do you know what to charge me? / Me: I'll spend as much time with you as I can to understand what you need and want. Based on what I hear, I'll come up with a few options that simultaneously seem like a steal for you and are lucrative for me. I want to make sure that we can both profit by working together." (Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016.) Stark, Do, and Mall all refuse the rate question with the same move: no rate exists; the price comes after the value conversation.

## "We want to know how much YOU think it'll cost"

Mall's read: "Sometimes they honestly don't know. Most times, though, they have a number in mind and they're hoping you come in lower." The counter is the **sticker-shock bracket**: "Ask: 'Are you aiming to spend closer to $500K, $50K, or $5K?' You're trying to force sticker shock to get a feel for a range. Look for a gut reaction." — "Oh, definitely not $500K." → "Of course not! How do you feel about spending $50K?" → "Yeah, if we can stick to about $50K, that'd feel right." → "Voilà: you've got a starting point for your project price" (Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016). Same maneuver as Stark's range float (qualifying-and-mindset.md), run on a direct prospect instead of a gatekeeper.

## "I need to see the hours breakdown" (the accountants demand it)

Chris's symmetry-of-logic sequence (RKXZ7t_RiOE roleplay, $200/hr × 90 hrs = $18K logo):

1. Reverse it: **"Why do you want to know my hourly rate?"** "So if I work fewer hours, I should charge you less?" "Yeah." "So if I go over those hours, I should charge you more? …So you're saying you value this logo taking longer rather than shorter. If it took me 4 months, you'd owe me $36,000."
2. Expose the asymmetry: "Clients can't have it both ways. They can't say tell me how many hours, but you can't go over. It has to have some symmetry to the logic."
3. Land it: "If I can do a logo for you right now and you love it in 5 minutes, is it worth less than 18 — or more? Cuz I saved you a lot of time. We can go to market right now." → **"Charging by the hour punishes me for being good."**
4. The alternative, painted: "You can hire four interns straight out of school. They'll charge a couple hundred bucks total, and you'll wait. And then you'll say it's not right, but you can't articulate it… and you keep waiting, you keep revising. And what aren't you doing now? You're not running your business."
5. Everyday analogies: house cleaning before a dinner party (you value it *done by the time you're back*); the Nikes ("Do you care how those shoes are made?… You just care that they're Nikes").

Crucially, Chris never claims he's better — the client concludes it: "When I sell him that I'm better and worth all this stuff, he's not going to believe me. I didn't say any of those words."

## "You're four times more expensive than the other two people we talked to"

Stark: **"I would say you should go with them."** (Retreat-and-follow — never convincing; the client convinces themselves.) Then the saver: **"Well, are the other people giving you an estimate, or are they willing to stand behind the price?"** Pre-empt the whole category by positioning early: "Obviously I'm going to be the most expensive option, so why would you guys not go with something cheaper like X?" — by proposal time the price is no surprise.

## "Last month another designer charged us $200"

Agree and undercut (FcyIGgFELGw, quoting $1,000–2,000): **"Wow, you got a really good deal… I'm just curious though — if it worked out so well, why are we talking?"** ("Well, he missed the deadline…") "So you're okay with missing deadlines? How much did that cost you in terms of opportunity?"

Then anchor on *their* hourly: "You're a successful entrepreneur — how much is your time worth? When you're not doing business development, not managing your operations, what is that costing you? I have to imagine it's in excess of $500 an hour, am I correct?" Position against the spectrum, not the $200: "You can pay any kind of price, from $5 on a site like Fiverr on up to an international branding firm… what I'm going to give you is on par with international standards at a much more affordable price." And leave the door open on *their* future pain: "If you start to feel like you're spending too much time managing people, getting subpar work, missing deadlines that cost you real money — please do reach out. I will be here."

## "Help us this time and we'll have more business for you later"

Mirror it back: **"It sounds horrible."** "As a vendor, when somebody says I'll give you half of what you're worth, and if I like it I'll come back and give you something more, maybe — **would you do that deal?**" ("No.") "It doesn't sound good, right?" Repeat business is table stakes, not a discount currency: "I sure hope so — most of my clients are repeat customers because I give them what they need. That should just be part of a working relationship."

## Extract their ceiling, then concede with a condition

Before any concession: **"Let me ask you just one question before I say goodbye — what's the maximum amount of money that you're willing to spend on this?"** (Client who opened at $200 says $500–600.) Now concede *above* their ceiling, never at your floor: "I see the gesture you're making and I want to meet you somewhere in the middle… if we did something for, say, $750, would you be okay with that?" And attach the condition: "For this time, for the first time, I'm willing to do it for 750 — but should you want to work with me in the future, I have to charge you where I normally charge, which is somewhere between $1,000 to $2,000."

Why not open at $500? "I don't want to negotiate at my final price… I'd have to then meet him at 400 or 350. There's no way I'm going to do that." The client who "won" at $750 just contemplated paying 50% over his stated max.

## "That's crazy!"

"I know, it's totally crazy… **my price is crazy until you start to compare it to people who charge a lot more than me for the same kind of work. I think I'm a bargain.**" Plus the risk analogies: "You want to spend $200 and roll the dice — are you a gambler? Me neither. That's why I pay for certain kinds of insurance. You don't want to buy a cheap stroller for your child… you don't want to go to a discount LASIK surgeon. **You only have two eyes.** You have a business to run." (Same family: "When you have eye surgery, you don't want to go for the discount broker. You won't risk it.")

## The overage / scope-creep master sequence

Client added stills to an agreed $800 video shoot ("the camera's already there — just snap a couple"). Chris's demonstration run (7FWGJ3CZdCM), in order:

1. **Buy time, go transparent:** "I need a minute to process this. I came into this conversation with a totally different assumption… I just need a minute to figure it out." (Tip for slow processors: buying time IS the technique.)
2. **Pin the change — one question, repeated calmly through every deflection:** "When did you know you needed more stills? …**Does that seem like a new request, or is this part of the original agreement?**" Client dodges three times ("I want someone flexible…" "is this going to be a big problem?"); hold: "All I asked you was one question, which for whatever reason you feel really uncomfortable to answer… Can you just answer that for me, please?"
3. **Name the emotion:** "I can tell there's some heat behind what you're saying. Is it something I said? Did I do something to bring this on?" — de-escalate by stepping beside them, not squaring up ("if somebody throws a punch, the last thing you want to be is at arm's length… move really close").
4. **Reset:** "You know what? Fair enough. I have a responsibility in this. Why don't we start over?"
5. **Separate fee + package reframe:** "I'd be happy to, but that falls outside the scope we talked about. Normally when people hire me to do photographs, I charge a totally separate fee" — anchor the standalone price ("if you hired me just to do event photos, I'd probably charge you $800 just for that"), then package: "The package price is usually $1,400, but I want to do this project with you — I'll do it for 1,200."
6. **Catch the fake split-the-difference:** client offers to "chop that right down the middle… an extra 100 for the stills." Do the math out loud: "So 1,200 is a lot, that's $400 extra, chop it in the middle — that'll be a thousand. You're throwing an extra 100 in, so that'll be 1,100?" (He meant 900.) "That doesn't sound quite like in the middle, does it? If we can do it for a thousand, let's do it."
7. **Detach — give them a graceful exit and use the clock honestly:** "If it doesn't work for you, why don't you think about it? But the event's in 2 days… you probably need to find another person right now, or you're going to pay rush fees." Client folds back to original scope: "Maybe I can find someone else to do the stills. Let's just stick with the video." "I like that. That sounds fair to me."

Companion lessons from the failed first run: ask **"Why do you need these photos?"** — the surface is photos-for-$100, the root is something else (product shots = a separate job); and "I agree, but…" followed by a hard pivot reads as a tell — agree-and-pivot only works if the pivot is a question. Also: clients this aggressive are rare — "if it happens in reality, you're dealing with a scumbag… I don't want to work with those people, no matter how good the work is."

## The speed trap

"You could do it faster now, right? So it'll take you less time — 500 bucks is even more money, cuz now I'm paying you more per hour." Counter by making them disown the frame: **"Is the speed at which it's done really the most important thing to you?"** ("Of course I want to sell it fast—") Then reorient to the outcome's worth: "How much is selling this listing worth to you?" ($1M house, 2.5% = ~$20K commission) — now $500 vs. the value is the absurdity, not your rate.

Flip side (Mall): real urgency is a value you *charge* for — "customers that value speed would pay double for a project that gets done in half the time they expect—charge for that." But run the **false-urgency test** first: "'Our CEO wants it done by May' isn't a real deadline, but 'We want this fantasy football app done two weeks before the season starts' is" (Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016). Manufactured deadlines earn no rush premium and signal a client who'll manufacture other pressures too.

## The package-deal trap

"I sell about six houses a month. What if I give you 10 of these?" — at the lowball rate. Unpack it: "Oh, you mean at 500 a piece. I see. The thing is, now I feel like I'm doing five times the work… that sounds like a lot of work for a little bit of money. I just can't make it feasible." Volume of a bad deal is a worse deal. (Chris's debrief: Matthew was being *nice* — "usually you have to negotiate for that; most clients just dangle the carrot.")

## Values evidence — "I saw the BMW"

When "I don't have a lot of money" collides with visible spending (RFk8ZmIDrFM): "If you look at the expenses you spend on, personal and business, where does $1,000 rank? How much did that refrigeration unit cost you? The AC? The polished floors — what did you spend on that, and how did that have any material impact on your business? …**And I saw the BMW parked outside — the 5 series, the M5.** That was really important to you. People tend to spend money on what they value."

Then force the sort: "When somebody comes to me and says I don't have a lot of money to do something, my first instinct is to say: let's not do it. It's not important… **Is it important or isn't it?**" Backed by Jim Rohn's disproportion principle (quoted in both RFk8ZmIDrFM and ivKnj9ffcmE): "Do not spend a small effort for a big result, and don't spend a big effort for a small result… Is this a big result for your company? And you want to spend a small effort — is that consistent with the way you do your business?"
