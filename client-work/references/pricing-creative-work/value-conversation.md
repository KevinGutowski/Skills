## Amateurs advise, experts diagnose

"Amateurs give advice; experts diagnose. How much time did you spend diagnosing the problem? Zero." The mechanic analogy: you come in with a broken headlight and he says you need a new transmission — "you're out, gone in 60 seconds." Opening any client conversation with a pitch ("I also do brand strategy, I'd love to…") reads exactly like that. Professionals work like doctors: ask questions, eliminate possibilities, *then* prescribe. First establish whether there is a problem at all — "our goal is to figure out: is there a problem we can solve, or is there a problem, period, to be solved." And don't only ask about problems you can solve — "then you're serving yourself once again."

Stop selling, start serving: "You want to close more jobs? Start serving. Different S-word. How can I be of service to you?"

## The Seven Essential Questions (The Coaching Habit)

In the TqczYbFPWnk demo Chris runs an entire strategic diagnosis by — his words — "literally reading a script that was posted inside the pro group": the seven essential questions from *The Coaching Habit: Say Less, Ask More & Change the Way You Lead Forever* (Michael Bungay Stanier). The ones visible in the demo:

- "What's on your mind? Where should we start? What do you need help with?"
- "And what else?"
- "What's the real challenge here for you?"

Plus Chris's own closer: "What does the business represent to you?" — the bridge from coaching to assigning a dollar value. "I've taken you basically through a strategic conversation and assigned a dollar value to it. So I know exactly how much to bid."

## Mall's questionnaire (third school)

All quotes: Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016. Mall's structured counterpart to the Seven Essential Questions: **eight rapid-fire questions, emailed first, then a follow-up phone call**. "The trick is to keep your ears open for value, not just the specifics of the answers."

1. **"What's the timeframe? Does a certain event depend on this project launching?"** — speed is a value; charge for it. **False-urgency test:** "Look out for customers who manufacture false urgency. 'Our CEO wants it done by May' isn't a real deadline, but 'We want this fantasy football app done two weeks before the season starts' is."
2. **"What are you looking for from us?"** — actual scope, fit, tasteful up-sell openings, and a timeline cross-check (six months of work in six weeks = misaligned expectations).
3. **"Have you already started on any part of the project?"** — execute-without-questions jobs make it "difficult to provide real value—and charge for it"; fired-their-last-agency baggage; existing partners.
4. **"How large is your team?"** — **team size is a value signal:** "Team size says a lot about how much an organization values the project they're hiring you for." Whole marketing team for a year = priority; "something they're sticking the new guy on" = "they likely won't spend a lot either." Also reveals stakeholders → scope and price accordingly (pain-in-the-butt surcharge).
5. **"How did you hear about our work? What specifically interests you about it?"** — the weed-out. "If they don't know you from a hole in the wall, make like a tree and stop wasting your time."
6. **"How much money have you set aside for this project?"** — "If you're not asking about money when you talk to a prospective client, you're doing it wrong." **Money, not budget:** "Notice that I asked about 'money,' not 'budget.' The word budget connotes cheap… Focusing on 'money' keeps the conversation on maximum value, whereas 'budget' draws attention to minimum spending." **Placement:** "about three-quarters of the way through, after I've lobbed them an easy one—why they thought to contact me. Bonus: that question perfectly lines me up to say I'm worth a premium."
   **The sticker-shock bracket** when they counter with "we want to know how much you think it'll cost": "Ask: 'Are you aiming to spend closer to $500K, $50K, or $5K?' You're trying to force sticker shock to get a feel for a range." — "Oh, definitely not $500K." → "Of course not! How do you feel about spending $50K?" → "Voilà: you've got a starting point." (Cross-ref Stark's range float in qualifying-and-mindset.md — same maneuver run through a gatekeeper.)
7. **"Are you talking to other companies about this project? Might we ask how many?"** — "If you're one out of twenty competitors, run!… I've rarely entered situations where more than three studios were pitching."
8. **"Anything else we should know about?"** — the catch-all, plus the meal test: would you have a meal with this client? Close by proving you listened (rush → "turn around a proposal before the end of the week"; portfolio doubt → introduce a vouching former client). "Projects where the client feels heard often end in success."

## Propose the job you want (Mall, RFP reshaping)

From the Mega Agency case study (Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016). Faced with a rigid 22-page RFP, Mall replied: "we can't really do our best work under a rigid RFP process. We're different from a lot of other agencies, and a process that forces us to look the same as everyone else makes us look … well, kinda the same as everyone else" — offering to "politely bow out" otherwise (honesty, differentiation, and "establishing our ability to walk away" in one email). On the call, learning it was development-only with comps already done, he put up roadblocks: "if they're after a chop shop, I'm probably way too expensive and others would be better; in fact, I'm happy to recommend some." Then: "I offer to write a proposal that restructures their design process to get to their desired outcome. Essentially, I'll write a proposal for the job I want, not the job they're offering. They agree vigorously."

## Value = both profits (Mall's blackjack model)

"Every transaction has another kind of profit that most people forget about: the customer's profit. The combination of your profit and your customer's profit is called value" (Dan Mall, *Pricing Design*, A Book Apart 2016). "The more profit—read: value—you can create for them, the more of that profit you're entitled to."

The **blackjack aligning-value model**: you and the client each write an honest number on paper — theirs, what they're willing to spend ("'As little as possible' is not an appropriate response"); yours, what you'd do it for ("'As much as possible' is not an appropriate response"). When both papers say $10,000, each number encodes a stack of values (their failed $5K freelancer, reclaimed weekends, half their audience-development budget; your booked pipeline, reusable tools, niche expertise, $4K/month family needs). "The magic in pricing is when you can match what your customer values with how valuable you are."

## Stark's why-conversation: why this / why now / why me

(Jonathan Stark, B1b7QlQILRo.) After letting the client brain-dump their feature list for 15–20 minutes: "Can we back up for a second so I can understand the context of this project in your overall business?" Then three whys, each trying to **talk them out of hiring you** (Win Without Pitching's "killing the engagement" — never convincing, making them convince themselves):

- **Why this?** "Why not do Y instead? Wouldn't Y be easier? Less risky? Maybe we could try Z." They'll have answers — now the project is justified in *their* words.
- **Why now?** "You've been thinking about this for 12 months. What changed? Why is it suddenly urgent? Couldn't you push this off six more months?" Surfaces the business reasons — risk, cost, churn ("our clients are complaining… we're nervous we're going to lose a client." "Like what kind of client?" "Home Depot." — now you know the stakes).
- **Why me?** "You know I'm going to be probably the highest-priced option. Why don't you do this in-house? Why not outsource it to Eastern Europe?" They eliminate every cheaper alternative themselves — which also pre-positions your price ("Obviously I'm going to be the most expensive option. Don't you get it? Obviously").

## Home run / disaster pair

"What would a home run look like? What if I knock this out of the park — how will we know? My goal is to deliver 100% customer satisfaction, so I want to know how to satisfy you 100%." If they can't answer, invert: "What would be a complete disaster? The worst possible thing that could happen?" — and extrapolate the home run from the disaster. After this, Stark writes a 3–5 page proposal with three options, priced on the outcome, having "barely thought about scope."

## The revenue-channel + margin roleplay (RKXZ7t_RiOE)

How to learn a company's finances without asking "what's your revenue?" (which gets a "we just met" reaction): "I have a roundabout way… that makes it more comfortable for the client to disclose financial information to me in small steps" — and it establishes you as a business person.

1. "I want to understand how you guys generate revenue… Is increasing revenue important to your company?" List channels: e-commerce 45%, retail 25%, reps 10%, international 20% (must sum to 100).
2. Teach the **double keystone** while estimating margins: "If this costs a dollar to buy [at retail], the retailer bought it from you for 50 cents. You have to make it for 25 cents in order for you to survive… It doubles each time." So margins: direct e-commerce ~65% gross, retail ~25%, reps similar, international as low as 20% ("they want even more money from you, cuz you're an unproven thing").
3. "Miss client, what area seems to make the most sense to focus our efforts?" — almost always direct/e-commerce: biggest share, highest margin. ("Most clients are the opposite: 80% wholesale, 5% direct — so if we move the needle, we do really well.")
4. Size the move: a realistic ~5% revenue-mix boost → "$20 million in revenue… at 65% gross margins… a gross profit of $12 million. Is that true, Miss Client?" (Bonus: she said the 5% was worth $20M — "I take that number times 20 and I know you are a $400 million company.")
5. Let her price it: "What kind of money should we spend to build a better site?" — "10% of that." — "So let's spend $1.2 million to build you a new website. Are you cool?" Then Chris hires the best builder he knows at full rate ($6K in the room) and keeps the difference: "I'm an entrepreneur."

The asymmetric framing: "I'm establishing in the client's mind that what we're going to do is worth $2 million" — quantitative (conversion %, dollars), not qualitative (prettier, more premium).

## Risk-discovery questions

For work whose value is risk-reduction rather than revenue (logos, identity): "How many things will this logo touch that you print? **What's your printing budget?**" ("$40 million." — "Well, I'm not going to charge you $2,000. You won't get the job, by the way. If you underbid that project they'll be really scared.") "**What happens when this goes wrong?**" — Uber's backlash, reprinting airplanes and every brochure forever. "What do you think you should spend on that? I just try and get them to say it… When you say it, you believe it."

## The TqczYbFPWnk demo — probe, worth, propose less

Chris diagnoses a designer's failed lead-gen campaign live:

- **Reframe with expertise (the overperforming probe):** 14 signups from a $100 boost, 2 replies — the client thinks it failed. "Two of 14 is pretty good in terms of people responding… you're overperforming on that front. You just need more people to sign up." The problem isn't the funnel's conversion, it's that "it was just too small a probe" — you need 200 signups, a list in the thousands, not tens.
- **Anchor on client worth:** "You spent $100. So, **what's one client worth to you?**" ~$1,000 × 5 desired clients = $5,000. "How much money makes sense to spend against that to get $5,000?" — client volunteers he'd spend up to the full $5,000.
- **Propose LESS than their number:** "I don't think we need to spend $5,000 to do this, but maybe we'll have to spend $3,000. Would that be okay with you, Stanley? I'm going to submit a proposal for $3,000. We can start next week." Coming in under the client's own stated ceiling makes the close frictionless.
- **The guarantee boundary:** "I could not guarantee results for 3,000 bucks, but if it was 300 [thousand] or 3 million, yeah — we'll work on it until it works." And scope the accountability: "I was just brought in to help you fix your funnel… if you can't close them, that's your problem."

## Cold-call opener + pre-empting objections (7FWGJ3CZdCM)

The cold-call demo: "Hey Matthew. I don't mean to bother you — I just need 10 seconds of your time. I got one of your email marketing campaigns in my inbox. I just wanted to ask you one question: **is this campaign working for you?**" Then specifics: compliment what's genuinely good ("beautifully designed… most people in your industry don't"), ask for numbers ("What's your open rate? Are those converting?"), and exit small: "I'd love to take 10 more minutes of your time, put a customized presentation together… would you be open to that?" Why it works: no small talk, did the homework, one specific intriguing question — and sellers/owners never believe their thing is doing well enough ("I could be doing $10 million of business — you ask me how would you like to do 50? Let's do it").

**Pre-empt every objection** (the sales call Chris transcribed because it was "a thing of beauty"): "You're probably thinking, oh no, not another salesperson, but… these are the typical pain points of who we talk to: sales takes six or seven months, it's expensive, you have no visibility… We don't do any of that." Name the client's objections before they can — including your own weaknesses: "You're talking to the owner and there's only two other people that work for me… you might be concerned that we're too small, but we've done six-figure jobs for multi-billion-dollar brands for the last 10 years." Now they can't bring it up.

## Compelling events

"A compelling event in sales terms is something where they need to act right away." Look for companies in your niche that just IPO'd or got funded — "a big infusion of money, and ready to spend it… usually they get that money for reasons for growth," and growth means marketing and brand. Pair the short-term door-knocking with the long game (content marketing) — "if you don't start today, you're adding another day behind."

## Presenting strategy as a journey (0begsejrxjI ride-alongs)

From the Trollbäck creative-strategy interview — how strategy (and by extension a priced recommendation) gets *bought*:

- **The not-words rule:** brand strategy docs use "we're X, not Y" pairs — but "for those not-words, don't use something that no one would ever try to be… we're authentic not fake — who would ever build a brand around being fake?" Split hairs that actually exclude: "rebellious but not brash." (Chris connects it to Seth Godin's market-splitting.)
- **Setup ≥ statement:** "I spend as much if not more time building the setup for what that brand strategy is as I do on the positioning statement… if you just show up and show a positioning statement and they don't know how you got there, it's really hard for them to buy in." Leave breadcrumbs — audience, culture, competition, strengths — "and together we created this statement."
- **"Just last week I gave a presentation and 70% of it was what we decided not to do."** The paths tried and killed, with reasons. Why clients value it: "they pay for our thinking just as much as the solutions we arrive at" — the controlled elimination is what lets them feel safe signing. Chris's pricing tie-in: when the public says "you paid 100 grand for *that*?", the due diligence they never saw is what was bought. Reference point: Paul Rand's process books — one logo, no revisions, "you don't have to use it, but either way you have to pay me" — sold via a logical narrative of how he got there.
