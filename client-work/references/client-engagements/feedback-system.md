## Why the system exists: the too-nice client

Early-Mule story: a smart, engaged client gave consistently positive feedback whose tone didn't match the words. They signed off on the final concept. Monteiro felt something was off and called: "Are you guys happy with the concept you signed off on?" Long silence. "Not really." Their explanation became the book's most illuminating client-services lesson: **"We didn't want to hurt your feelings, and we hoped you'd figure out that we weren't happy."** That hope cost four weeks of project time and the money attached.

Corollaries:

- "Designers are terrible at reading minds. Unless you tell them to stop doing something, they'll keep doing it."
- "You can't hope someone into a course-correction."
- "You have my permission to hurt your designers' feelings. It's a good thing."
- "Good feedback isn't synonymous with positive feedback." Late-arriving honesty ("raved about the work for most of the project only to get to a point far along into implementation and decide the design was all wrong") tanks budget and deadline. If you're absolutely sure something won't work and the designer hasn't convinced you otherwise, say so now — "It's not going to grow on you."

## The honesty preamble (verbatim script)

Delivered before entering the concept phase, tailored per client:

> "Some of the things you see will be right, some will be wrong. It's important you tell me which is which. You can't hurt my feelings—it's part of the job. Your negative feedback is more valuable than your positive feedback. If you don't speak up when something isn't working, I won't have a chance to fix it, which will cost us time and money we don't have. Let's promise to be totally, brutally honest with one another. Ready?"

— Mike Monteiro, *You're My Favorite Client*, A Book Apart, 2014.

## Setting the stage: where are you?

You can't evaluate work without knowing which phase you're in and what the deliverable is for (rough direction-setting sketch vs. realized system; real labels vs. placeholders; prototype vs. production code). The Bunny Colvin test (via *The Wire*): you can't evaluate your situation unless you know where you are.

So **every design presentation opens with**: "Here's what we need your feedback on today." — optionally plus "Here's what we're not ready to talk about yet." If the designer doesn't say it, the client should ask: "What kind of feedback do you need? What should I focus on and what should I ignore?" Otherwise you burn thirty minutes on JavaScript that isn't due to work for three weeks.

Same rule asynchronously: a share with only "What do you think of this?" attached is a problem — too vague, opens everything. (The book's office vignette: client writes a long thorough email; designer sighs, "I just wanted to know about the typeface"; design director: "Did you tell the client that?" "Well, no.")

## The like-ban and subjectivity

- "No word is more useless in a design context than the word like." "Million-dollar projects have been derailed by the word like."
- "Your personal tastes are not a success metric. This is a business!" Evaluate against revenue, retention, the metrics set at the start. "I don't care if you hate green. If the research points to green making you buckets of money, we're using it."
- The mechanism: *like* moves success onto someone's subjective mood; designers are human, enjoy hearing it, and start designing to hear it again — six months later the project has failed and "we won't get much relief from the fact that we all liked it."
- "Making you happy is never a project goal." (Ch. 1 echo: "Making the client happy is a by-product of doing good design… your happiness should never be the goal.")
- Substitutes: "This works." "This seems correct." Also avoid *great*, *pretty*, *happy*.
- It cuts both ways: "Nobody here gets to like anything." The designer's rationale must be research-based too; watch their subjectivity as closely as your own.
- The framing question: "The right question isn't 'Do I like it?' but 'Does this meet our goals?' If it's blue, don't ask yourself whether you like blue. Ask yourself if blue will help you sell sprockets."

## Good vs. bad feedback (goals, not prescriptions)

"Good feedback relates back to goals and user needs. Bad feedback is subjective and prescriptive."

- **Excellent:** "There's way too much going on here and the Add to Cart button gets lost." — names the goal (sell the thing) and the problem (clutter), leaves the solution to the designer.
- **Useless (subjective):** "I hate scrolling." — "I can do absolutely nothing with that statement… You're not every reader." Building to one person's preferences makes one person happy; not enough people for success.
- **Useless (prescriptive):** "Move the buttons over here." / "Make the logo bigger!" — maybe decent ideas, but talking about the underlying problem may surface better solutions or a larger system flaw. The doctor analogy: demand penicillin all you want, "there's no way you're walking out of that office without the pants coming down."
- Rationale standard for the designer side: "I've placed this ad so that it intersects the article body, thereby placing it within the reader's reading flow…" is expert reasoning; "I put the ad here, because it fit" is not. No explainable rationale = "you have nothing to give feedback on. Get them back in the room."

## Delivery mechanics

- **Lead with the general.** Summary verdict first ("Overall, this is going in the right direction" / "Overall, this sucks"), then the why, then details. The why is the most important piece.
- **Don't bury the lede.** Ten pages of detailed, mostly helpful feedback; two-thirds down page eight: "Overall, the design feels sterile." That line is the feedback. "No amount of tweaking will turn a potato into a schnauzer. If it doesn't work for you at a big-picture level, don't sweat the details."
- **Be direct.** "There's only one way to take, 'This work sucks.' There are many ways to take, 'I'm not sure this is doing it for me.'" Soften if you can, but never at the cost of clarity.
- **Distill to one voice.** "John in Sales wants to log in on the homepage, Tina in Engineering wants it on its own page. Can we compromise?" No. "If you tell your barber that you like it short but your significant other likes it long, you're gonna get a mullet." Weigh the team's input, decide, deliver one clearly written document of strong decisions — invite the design team into the debate if needed, but never forward the debate itself. "Nor should you pass that debate along for your customers to suffer through."
- **Present your feedback.** "Just as we don't believe good design sells itself, we also don't believe good feedback explains itself." Schedule a walkthrough (in person or call), resolve sticking points, bring the internal dissenter onto the call. "Solid decisions, well communicated and well executed, are the path to success."

## Feedback-by-comp: the fired-over offense

"Nothing is less helpful than getting feedback in the form of a comp (whether committed in Photoshop, PowerPoint, or Word). Nothing." In ~10 years of Mule, **the only thing they ever fired a client over** — once, after repeated warnings and a contract violation. Why it's poison: a comp hides the reasoning, so the designer must reverse-engineer what problem you were solving; correcting without saying why leaves the designer ignorant, repeating the mistake, and you correcting forever. Lost time, lost budget. (Glossary: prescriptive feedback is "also known as trying to do the designer's job.")

## Keeping the designer honest

The client's standing question on every share: **"What led you down this path?"** Any designer doing their job can answer it. The designer's standing obligations: tie decisions to goals, back them with research/data/user quotes, and set feedback context on every share. "A good client knows the difference between personal opinion and goal-driven, informed evaluation."

## Shared vocabulary

The "extreme" story: a client said the concepts weren't extreme enough. The next sentence should have been **"What do you mean by extreme?"** — Monteiro instead presented a second round and discovered his extreme and theirs were very different things. Clients and designers use the same words for different things (e.g., *template* to a UX designer vs. a developer). It's the designer's responsibility — as a communication professional — to build the shared vocabulary, but it's in the client's interest too. Inigo Montoya is invoked.

## Defending vs. defensive

- Spool (cited in the book): **"The best designers are passionate about design, but dispassionate about their own designs."** (twitter.com/jmspool/status/391311660062736384)
- "There's a big difference between defending work, which a designer must know how to do, and being defensive about work, which a designer should never do." Past the point where the problem is obvious, continued fighting is for ego, not quality.
- The graceful catch: fifteen minutes into a presentation a client says "You forgot to take x into account" — and they're right. The job shifts to solving for that case and thanking the person who caught it. Rationale built on research sets this up; "inspiration" as rationale invites equally subjective feedback — "Whim begets whim."
- Spool's other line anchors qualification: **"Design is the rendering of intent."** (uie.com/articles/design_rendering_intent)

## Evaluation lenses

What to actually look at when work is on the wall:

- **Tone/brand:** the work carries the client's name; evaluate tone across the lifecycle — palette, navigation language, bylines, social voice.
- **Feasibility:** Can you make it? Can you run it? (Oars Test.) Developer-hesitation trick: they always say "no, not tricky," but a hesitation before the no → get an hourly estimate, double it, ask for an easier way.
- **Flow:** do things happen in the right order, both orders users actually take? "Pave the cowpaths."
- **Simplicity:** Saint-Exupéry (quoted in the book): "A designer knows he has achieved perfection, not when there is nothing left to add, but when there is nothing left to take away." Monteiro's escalation: "A good designer finds an elegant way to put everything you need on a page. A great designer convinces you that half that shit is unnecessary." Removal test: "If you take something away and the site still meets its goals, you don't need it. Burn it with fire." Internal real-estate battles (tragedy-of-the-commons sheep) are the client's to police — the designer can make a stand, but the client keeps it from becoming "a sheep show."
- **Hierarchy:** the receiving-line model — the most important thing gets greeted first; users should feel that order. Beware the **make-it-bigger death spiral**: can't see the text → bigger; now can't see the ads → bigger; then the Buy button, then the logo — "Before you know it, you have the equivalent of a roomful of people shouting at you." The fix: "If you want people to notice something, make the things around it smaller. Your logo is big enough."

## The budget conversation (car-dealer script)

The story: Monteiro's father, dressed in a sharkskin suit, test-drove a top-of-the-line Dodge St. Regis far beyond the family budget; young Mike translated ("Dad, we can't afford this." — "Shut up. Ask him if the seats are heated."). When the truth finally came out, the salesman's reply is the lesson: **"Why didn't you tell me what you could afford?"**

The script that follows: budget is the first thing Mule asks prospects. Clients fear "if I tell you the number, that's what it'll cost." Answer: "That's partially true. I'll tell you what you can get for that amount. Then we can talk about whether you need that much design." The number guides toward solutions inside the price range — "It'll save us from having to look at everything on the lot." And: "if you don't think you have a budget, someone's probably withholding it from you."

Related qualification lines: have the designer walk through their estimate and stand behind it; with multi-bid spreads, ask the high bidder why they're worth more — no answer is a red flag. "Choose who you want to work with first, and then decide whether you'll pay them what they ask."
