## The five seniority anti-patterns

From mentoring 500+ designers — "patterns that stop really talented designers from reaching leading roles."

### 1. Waiting for permission

Good ideas unsaid because you've been shot down before; real product problems left alone ("that's not really my job"); **already doing lead-level work — mentoring, critiques, initiative — without asking for the title or pay**; assuming design improvements are the PM's job to raise. Why: rejection taught you your voice doesn't matter, and early-career lane-staying *was* rewarded. The shift: "The problem isn't that you don't see what needs to [change]. You absolutely do. The real issue is that you don't have the strategies to advocate effectively. Leading designers… know how to get buy-in, and that's a skill you can learn."

**This week:** pick one improvement you've been sitting on. 10 minutes of prep: the business case, "the smallest, lowest-effort version of this idea," who needs to care. Practice one sentence out loud. In the meeting, open a door, don't make a demand: "Could we spend 5 minutes exploring whether this is worth prioritizing?"

### 2. Outputs instead of outcomes

Months on a redesign that won't ship for a year; polishing visuals when the real problem is a UX flow; shipping things you privately doubted and watching them gather dust; three weeks of explorations before anyone validated the problem. Why: low-design-maturity orgs hand you vague briefs ("refresh the dashboard") and you assume someone senior owns the strategy. "Your job isn't to make things look good. Your job is to drive outcomes that matter to the business."

**This week (the pre-Figma gate):** before any new project, write down — (1) what business problem are we solving? (2) what user problem? (3) how will we measure success? "If you don't have answers to those questions, don't open Figma yet. Schedule 15 minutes with your PM and align on these first… outputs don't get you promoted."

### 3. Not building relationships proactively

See the relationship-hygiene section of [pm-partnership.md](pm-partnership.md). **This week:** two recurring one-on-ones — your PM, plus a designer/PM on an adjacent team — built before you need them.

### 4. Avoiding conflict

Saying yes to every request even when it compromises the design; letting substandard work ship to dodge an awkward conversation; choosing "the education route" (workshops, lunch-and-learns, documentation) when a direct conversation about standards is what's needed; withholding critique feedback. Costs: bad work ships, ideas never evolve, "you lose credibility because you don't stand for anything."

**This week:** one small, low-stakes pushback. Write down the shared goal, your concern, an alternative; say "I want us to succeed at [goal]. I'm concerned about X because [reason]. What if we tried Y?" Full conflict playbook: [objections.md](objections.md).

### 5. Underselling your impact

Doing leadership work (mentoring, critiques, process improvements) without tying it to business impact; "I just helped with that" instead of "I led that"; a portfolio that "just lists what you made, like B2B dashboard or mobile app, not the actual impact you drove"; assuming great work auto-converts to recognition. Costs: passed over for promotion because leadership can't see the connection; not top of mind for high-impact staffing; someone else gets credit.

Her mentee's realization: **"No one's going to care about my career as much as I do."** Not even your manager. "Great work doesn't automatically guarantee reward or recognition… Visibility matters." Either roadshow your work broadly, or pick projects whose impact scales horizontally across the org.

**This week:** (1) start a wins doc; (2) share one win with your manager in your next one-on-one — "I did X, which resulted in Y impact," not a description of what you made. "It's going to feel uncomfortable at first. Do it anyway."

## The wins doc

"Track one thing per week that you're proud of, the problem it solved, impact, even if it's just qualitative." One entry per week is the cadence; small things count. This is the raw feed for everything below — self-reviews, promotion packets, portfolio updates, and the manager one-on-one habit.

## Why self-reflections fail + invisible work

"Every designer I've ever spoken to about performance reviews says the same thing. They sit down to write their self-reflection and they just blank." Not for lack of good work — "performance reviews are a retroactive exercise and human memory is terrible at those kinds of exercises."

The biggest reason strong designers get underwhelming reviews: **invisible work** — "everything that doesn't leave a visible artifact":

- You push back in a product review, show the data, get a confusing flow reversed before ship. "There's no Figma file for prevented a bad decision."
- You write the design brief that aligns everyone; the project ships on time. Partly your brief — but no one's tracking that.
- A junior is stuck for two days; you sit with them for 45 minutes and they ship something they're proud of. You forget it by next week.

"All of that is real work. All of it demonstrates exactly the kind of thing that gets people promoted. Strategic thinking, collaboration, leadership." It lives in conversations, meetings, and Slack threads — "if you don't document it at the time, it's gone." The solution: "stop treating your review as a one-time writing project and start treating it as a log that you build throughout the year."

## The four self-reflection mistakes

1. **Responsibilities instead of contributions.** "I designed the onboarding flow" is "a job description, not really an achievement. Every designer at your level designed something." What did *you* specifically do, and what happened because of it?
2. **Outputs instead of outcomes.** "I delivered 12 screens for the new dashboard" — did anybody use it? Did it solve the problem? Outcomes are what your manager cares about.
3. **Vague to seem humble.** "I helped the team with the Q3 launch" — helped how? "This isn't modesty, it's erasure… Your manager needs specifics to advocate for you. Vague is not humble, just unhelpful."
4. **Only writing about wins.** "A self-reflection that's all sunshine reads as unaware." Some of your strongest writing is the project that didn't go as expected — what you learned, what you changed. "That's the stuff that signals growth and maturity and reviewers notice."

All four are "symptoms of writing your self-reflection in one sitting, from memory, at the last minute."

## The SBI framework

**Situation → Behavior → Impact** for every logged entry:

- **Situation** — the context; what problem or moment were you responding to?
- **Behavior** — what *you* specifically did. Not "I contributed" — the actual action.
- **Impact** — what changed. "Ideally with a number, but even a qualitative outcome will work."

Weak: "Worked on the checkout redesign and contributed to improvements." SBI version: "Checkout had a 34% drop-off at the payment step" (situation) → "I ran a 3-day research sprint, identified that users were confused by the card input layout, and redesigned the form with inline validation" (behavior) → after launch, drop-off at that step fell substantially (impact — *her exact figure is garbled in the auto-captions, so it's paraphrased here; the structure is the point*). "Same project, completely different picture. That's what specific evidence writing does."

## Her Claude-skill review logger (reusable architecture)

Femke built (and personally uses) a free Claude skill — the "self-reflection performance coach" — whose architecture generalizes to any stateful logging workflow:

1. **Setup interview (once, ~2 minutes)** — Claude asks your role, "any competencies that your company evaluates you on," and your review period. This becomes persistent context.
2. **Continuous entry logging** — drop in artifacts as they happen: meeting notes, a Slack thread, a project brief, "even just a quick description of something that went well." Claude converts each into a polished SBI entry, "matched to your competencies, dated, saved into your document." "Your review doc is building itself in the background while you're focused on doing the actual work."
3. **Attributed peer-feedback log** — a separate section: paste in shout-outs or 360 comments and each "gets attributed and saved."
4. **End-of-period synthesis** — Claude "walks you through a short reflection, reads your entries, identifies the themes, and helps you write a summary."

Operating cadence: "log one entry every week or two while the details are fresh. Small things count." The failure mode it kills: "waiting until the last minute, and then they're trying to make up for months, or sometimes even a year, of undocumented work."

Reusable pattern: setup-interview-as-state → artifact-to-structured-entry conversion tagged to an evaluation rubric → attributed external-evidence log → periodic synthesis. Works for promotion packets, team health logs, or any review-shaped artifact.

## The credibility ladder

Miranda Slater's progression for earning strategic inclusion (full version in [pitching.md](pitching.md)): master the business language → find small wins and turn them into proposals → build credibility gradually with explorations tied to impact → then ask for the seat: "I know planning's coming up. Can I be included…?" Promotion follows the same physics as pitch approval: "you have to prove your value before they'll even consider including you in strategic conversations."

## Portfolio: the external mirror

The same impact discipline, pointed outward (hh1UXn8ZPwc): "Your design portfolio isn't a resume. It's a product of your work" — and like the wins doc, it should accrete continuously, not get panic-rebuilt while job hunting.

- **Sarah Doody**: her early screenshots-in-a-binder portfolio "got me interviews, but it didn't get me job offers" — the fix was treating the portfolio as a product and telling the story (decisions, trade-offs, why it mattered), not displaying outputs. Unshipped and research-skipped projects are fair game with context: "you just have to add context around why you didn't do research."
- **Aneta Kmiecik**: "you are actually the boss of your portfolio" — you're its product owner; one work project doesn't have to equal one case study (snapshots, themes, single decisions all work), and "the less experienced you are, the more proof you need to show." Progress over perfection: small iterations, "a marathon not a sprint."
- Femke's memorable example: a portfolio section called "five design decisions I'm proud of" — five moments, each a choice that influenced an outcome — "more memorable than any 2,000-word case study."

The connective rule: anti-pattern 5 shows up here too — a portfolio that lists artifacts instead of impact undersells you to exactly the audience (hiring managers, promo committees) you can't talk to directly.
