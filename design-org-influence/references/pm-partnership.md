## The frame: overlap is where innovation happens

"Most innovative products aren't created when designers and PMs stay in their lanes. They happen when both roles know how to dance together effectively." At senior level the blurring is natural — Femke's own story: she once built an entire user flow her PM had already mapped out differently; "we were both wrong about how we were working together." The overlap "isn't something to avoid. It's actually where ideas can flourish" — but it still needs explicitly negotiated responsibilities.

## Roles negotiation (Miranda Slater)

Don't let overlap stay implicit — "otherwise it will always be this tug-of-war constantly back and forth," and an unheard PM "just goes do their own thing." The script:

1. **Build rapport first** — you need a safe space; you "can't just jump in and be like, 'What do you want to do? What do I want to do?'"
2. **Open with your own cards**: "These are what I feel like I should be responsible for. What do you feel like you should be responsible for?" Have the discussion and draw the lines.
3. **Make it time-boxed and iterative, with a retro**: "for Q1, let's try these lines of responsibility. We'll have a little bit of a retro, talk about it. Do we feel like we're still getting quality output?" Not permanent — an iterative process.
4. **Frame the benefit to them**: it gives *them* back capacity for strategic work, "as long as they're still comfortable and confident they're getting the outcome they need."

Designers are well-placed to facilitate this: "design possesses the skill sets and has the resources to really kind of create that collaborative process."

## Problems, not solutions: setting the expectation

From the product-thinking-designer lesson (jhtqP9wT8tg): "The first step towards this type of partnership is letting your PM know that you would like to be approached with problems and not solutions, followed by how you'd like to collaborate on those problems together in the early stages of a project." PMs "may not be aware of the value of design and think it's on them to come to you with refined solutions" — changing that mindset "always starts with a conversation."

Counterarguments to bring to that conversation — a strong product-design partnership:

- **Encourages diversity of thought** — a thought partner for brainstorming leads to more creative solutions and business/customer impact.
- **Solves customer pain through product features** — the designer as early voice of the customer makes the product likelier to meet customer goals, which "ultimately leads to positive business impact."
- **Builds trust** — which is what gets you buy-in and faster conflict resolution when the inevitable disagreement comes.
- **Defines clear responsibilities and ownership** — "the more ownership individuals have over their work, the more high quality their work will be."

The contrast scenario to avoid (the "Chris the PM" case): PM under OKR pressure arrives with a vague solution doc and asks for wireframes by Friday. Executing leaves you "in the dark, undervalued and demoralized"; pure pushback costs relationship capital. The fix is upstream — the expectation-setting above, plus shared roadmap/metric reviews so you spot opportunities *together* and co-write the brief (problem statement, goals, success metrics) before solutioning.

## Empathy-first decode

Miranda: "A lot of designers really butt heads with their product folks… I don't think we do a good enough job having the empathy for our PMs that… we do for our users." Before concluding "the PM is just a control freak," consider: "It could be a past experience or lack of experience" — maybe they've never worked with designers, or only with bad ones. Both are possible; neither is malice.

This is the same move as Florian's objection-decode (see [objections.md](objections.md)), applied to the standing relationship rather than a single pushback.

## Working-style discovery

Get to know the basics of how your PM likes to work with design:

- Do they enjoy jamming in FigJam on early-stage concepts, or prefer to react to proposals?
- Do they follow set processes or stay flexible?
- Are they "an ideas person who thrives on generating lots of possibilities, or more of a synthesizer who likes to reel things in and lock in on a path forward"?

"Understanding these working preferences can make all the difference in how smoothly your partnership functions." And don't fear disagreement: "Some of my best design work has come from product feedback or from a PM pushing our team to think differently."

## Language translation

PMs view products through features; designers through user journeys/stories. Keep your user stories, but translate. The template:

> "Users typically behave in this way, which is leading to this type of business impact. So going forward, I'd recommend we ship our features with this in mind, so that we can continue providing a good experience for our users while also reaching these business milestones."

"This kind of translation shows that you understand both worlds." Test yourself: do OKRs/KPIs/business metrics make you sweat? Miranda's blunt version: "Speaking the same language as product is core to them listening to you. If you always come in with UX jargon, you're setting that barrier for yourself."

## Stakeholders as users (Miranda Slater)

"Think of your stakeholders as your users." Apply your design process to your communication:

- Pull forward the primary point. "Maybe you've done 16 things, but you only highlight four of them" — the four that prove design is "a strategic lever within the company versus just being the executors."
- Consider "what your company actually cares about and then making that connection for them."
- Be strategic about cadence and channel — don't default to "an hour session every two months"; choose when, to whom, and how deliberately.

The outputs-vs-outcomes contrast (2v4mEMAt_80): 12 slides of iterations vs leading with "we reduced drop-off by 23% just by simplifying the signup steps from five down to three — and here's what changed and why." "One buries the value in the process and the other leads with impact."

Steven Steiner's standing door-opener belongs here too: in every one-on-one ask "How can I help you right now?" — "that is your ability to open up the door for them to give you ownership of something that they didn't offer first." And when you complain, pitch: "always have a pitch for your complaints."

## Design as a shared capability

Challenge the design-as-deliverables mindset: "the most important impact we can have happens off the canvas" — defining problems worth solving, facilitating, synthesizing research, "creating alignment before a single pixel is even placed." See design "as more of a shared capability rather than a siloed skill":

- Invite the PM into stages of the design process — wireframing if they enjoy it, defining evaluation criteria for concepts, co-creating user journeys "rather than presenting them in isolation as finished work."
- This requires intention: define a framework for how people get involved; create clear touchpoints where collaboration is most valuable (not every meeting needs both roles); document the collaboration points so they're workflow, not one-offs.
- "This isn't about surrendering ownership… The best senior designers I know aren't protective of their territory. They expand their influence by bringing others into the design thinking process."

Ask yourself: where in my process could my PM's perspective strengthen the outcome? How might I create space for their input without compromising design quality?

## The Craig and Julie Zhuo framings

**Craig** (design leader, crAaxd30NMg) on the overlap: "Why avoid? What's wrong with overlap?" He's acted as PM himself; "honestly the main difference is that PMs are spending way more time in prioritization and strategy stuff than most designers." PMs vary enormously — from ML-org PMs who've never worked with a designer to ex-designer PMs — so calibrate per person. "If you're a good strategic partner and you show the value… then the overlap isn't that much of a concern long term." His one red line: the dictating PM who hands you finished wireframes as orders — "why am I here? I'm just a decorator. That's a different conversation."

**Julie Zhuo** (quoted in jhtqP9wT8tg): "If you expand your definition of design, if you think of it as translating problems into solutions… design isn't just what it looks and feels like, design is how it works — then you will see the design arena expand. Problems are eternal. Tomorrow's design jobs will simply be product jobs." The partnership skills in this file *are* the career path.

## The VP-timing rule and feedback triage

From rO68VwacBoE (stakeholder management):

- **VP-timing rule**: know who to pull in when. "It might not necessarily make sense to get the VP of product into your brainstorming session," but it's the right moment "when you're ready to share some designs for feedback and you're maybe at a crossroads of making a really important product decision." Map each stakeholder's influence and pull them in at the stage where their input changes the outcome.
- **Expectation-setting**: communicate realistic timelines, scope, and when stakeholders can expect milestones.
- **Feedback triage**: you don't have to address every piece of design-review feedback — "knowing which feedback to address when and why strategically" is the skill. Same for performance feedback: build the resilience muscle, decide what to act on.

## Relationship hygiene (the anti-pattern)

From fRdUMGzMNLo (pattern 3): only talking to your PM at deadlines, weeks of heads-down work with no WIP shared, no idea what adjacent teams are doing — then surprise pushback from stakeholders you never built a relationship with. "You treat your PM like they're your boss… instead of seeing them as a peer or a partner."

The fix: schedule two recurring one-on-ones — one with your PM, one with a designer or PM from another team whose work touches yours. Ask about their goals and where you might collaborate, "not because you need something right now, but because you're building the relationship before you need it." At senior+ levels, that horizontal influence — thinking in systems, not just features — is "what differentiates senior designers from mid-level designers."
