## Read the PM's position first

Put yourself in the PM's shoes before responding: they're "in a very strategic role where they're thinking about the holistic overall experience," driven by business metrics and project goals, responsible for keeping the team moving. They're "wary of any blockers" — their job is to mitigate them. Their constant mental math: "how can we have the most amount of impact with the least amount of resources and in the shortest time possible." Every playbook below starts from that frame.

Baseline hygiene that prevents many objections: come to reviews with **multiple options** ("it helps when people have something to compare something to"), lay concepts side by side with rationale and pros/cons, and always include your recommendation.

## Phrase 1: "I don't like this"

Subjective-sounding, but usually hiding a deeper reason.

1. **Ask why.** If the feedback is "I don't think people will notice it here" — what's the underlying reason? Did the company try it before and it failed? Is there data or prior research about this placement? Those reasons are what you want surfaced, not the subjective wrapper.
2. **Reframe to the goal.** "Remind your product manager of the goal and ask them which of these concepts best helps us achieve that goal" — it forces evaluation through the goal's lens and strips the personal-taste layer.
3. **Pros/cons together.** Sit down (sync or async) and "write a list of pros and cons together" for each concept — it gives the PM a sanctioned place to air the cons while weighing them against the pros, and pulls them into your process.
4. **Check your own presentation.** Maybe they misunderstood. Lay out concepts side by side with design rationale, pros/cons, and your recommendation.

## Phrase 2: "This will take too long / we don't have the resources"

1. **Ask why.** Usually this is technical implementation or eng staffing. Understanding the limitation lets you discuss what's needed to overcome it.
2. **Reshuffle resources.** Femke's lived examples: teams "temporarily borrowed engineers from other teams" to fill a resourcing gap; she herself was loaned to designer-less teams. Ask whether that's on the table.
3. **Prevent it next time: engineers from day one.** "It always pays to have engineers involved early on in your design process" — invite them to design reviews from day one, get size estimates and feasibility per concept, and present the eng lift alongside each option.
4. **Break it into milestones.** Maybe the PM is right about the North Star's cost right now. Break the North Star into smaller iterative cycles so something ships now while building toward it. (Cross-ref: the phased plan in [pitching.md](pitching.md) — 80%-of-value MVP, phases with weeks/risk/impact labels.)

## Phrase 3: "It looks great, but this just isn't a priority right now"

1. **Show how investing now saves time later** — e.g., a more scalable design framework means less design debt later.
2. **Decouple the proposal from the priority.** Agree the full concept isn't a priority; scale it back to a version that fits current priorities, while keeping the full version as the agreed goal you design toward.
3. **Re-attach it to a bigger priority.** Her example: a misused banner/notification system. Fixing banners in isolation doesn't feel worth prioritizing — but "solving banners from a more platform perspective" has huge potential impact. Relate the isolated thing back to the bigger problem that *is* a priority.
4. Timing lever: if "not now" is really "not ever scheduled," manufacture the window — see timing in [pitching.md](pitching.md).

## Phrase 4: "I don't think it's gonna work"

1. **Ask why — get their hypothesis.** "If we did go with this concept, what do you think is going to happen? What's your hypothesis as to why this concept is going to fail?"
2. **Propose user testing.** Light testing for confidence: "validating the concept... with user testing is often really hard to argue against if it turns out that it is working well."
3. **Propose an A/B test / experiment.** Put concepts live and compare: "data doesn't lie — the metrics will tell you if the concept is truly working or not." Costs extra time/resources; push for it when you're genuinely confident.

## The objection → response map

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "This will take too long" | Phased rollout plan |
| "Users won't understand this" | Usability test results |
| "It's too risky" | A/B testing approach |
| "I don't like this" | Ask why → goal lens → pros/cons together |
| "Not a priority" | Save-time-later case, decouple/scale back, or re-attach to a bigger priority |
| "It won't work" | Their hypothesis → user test → experiment |
| "That's opportunity cost" | Four lenses + engineering allies ([pitching.md](pitching.md)) |

## Decode the motivation (Florian)

When someone pushes back, ask yourself: "What is their goal in the project? What is their responsibility? What might make them think that they can reach their goals quicker by doing something different?" And the key line: **"Sometimes a push back isn't about your design. It's about a perceived gap in your process."**

Florian's method: "I always try to see where people are coming from… see if you can find out if it's just this person generally shooting over the line or if they see something missing on the team or with you and then see if you can resolve that together." Signals to read:

- A PM doing user research themselves → they may feel you're not doing enough of it.
- Engineering questioning feasibility → they may need more detailed specs up front.
- Leadership disengaged → they may not see how your work connects to their priorities.

The fix is "honest, consistent conversations about expectations and working styles, not just defending your design decisions."

## Productive conflict: the script

From fRdUMGzMNLo (anti-pattern 4, avoiding conflict). The costs of staying quiet: poor work ships and you resent it; ideas never evolve past first drafts; critical user problems go unquestioned; "you lose credibility because you don't stand for anything."

Reframe: "Conflict isn't personal. It's about the goal. When you disagree with someone, you're not attacking them. You're protecting the outcome." Move the conversation from solutions ("do it this way") to outcomes ("here's what we're trying to achieve"), framed around shared success.

The script — write down the shared goal, your specific concern, and an alternative, then say:

> **"I want us to succeed at [goal]. I'm concerned about X because [reason]. What if we tried Y?"**

Worked version: instead of "that won't work" — "I want to make sure we hit our retention goal. I'm worried this approach might create friction at checkout because of [what the data or research shows]. Could we test a version that addresses that concern?"

Practice on something low-stakes first. "Productive conflict actually builds trust and influence… People respect colleagues who stand for something and have an opinion."

Anti-pattern to avoid: defaulting to "the education route" — workshops, lunch-and-learns, a "craft committee" — when what's needed is a direct conversation. Her mentee's real problem wasn't engineer education; it was a CTO who "saw design engineering as an opportunity cost versus feature development." What he needed was "a direct conversation about business impact and ROI" (the four-lens case in [pitching.md](pitching.md)).

## When in doubt / escalation

If you've tried the playbooks and you still can't move forward:

- **Escalate to your manager** for guidance and support. A designer and PM "should have trust and have a good working relationship and that should be a really strong and equal partnership" — if that's structurally absent, raising it is legitimate, not tattling.
- **Default to evidence**: propose user research/testing even when you agree on direction — "it's hard to argue directly with feedback you get from users."
- **A/B or experiment-driven approach** where the org supports it: get multiple concepts live and let the data decide.

And remember disagreement itself is healthy: "Some of my best design work has come from product feedback or from a PM pushing our team to think differently. These moments of creative tension, when navigated well, often lead to breakthrough solutions."
