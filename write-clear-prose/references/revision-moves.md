# Revision Moves

Use this reference when the user wants deep editing, writing coaching, a full rewrite, or a craft diagnosis. It synthesizes practical principles associated with acclaimed writing books, especially William Zinsser's focus on clear nonfiction, rewriting, simplicity, unity, audience, voice, and enjoyment.

## Diagnostic Ladder

Move from largest to smallest. Do not polish sentences before the piece knows what it is doing.

1. **Purpose**: What is the one sentence version of the piece?
2. **Reader contract**: What does the opening promise, and does the body deliver it?
3. **Unity**: Does every section belong to the same piece?
4. **Order**: Is the sequence logical, emotional, chronological, spatial, or argumentative? If it mixes modes, is that deliberate?
5. **Proportion**: Are important ideas given space and minor ideas kept brief?
6. **Paragraphs**: Does each paragraph have one job?
7. **Sentences**: Is the actor, action, and consequence clear?
8. **Words**: Is each word doing useful work?

## Clarity and Clutter

Cut:

- Throat-clearing: "It is important to note," "Needless to say," "In today's world."
- Inflators: "utilize," "facilitate," "leverage" when "use," "help," or a concrete verb works better.
- Redundant pairs: "each and every," "basic fundamentals," "future plans."
- Hedging by reflex: "sort of," "kind of," "perhaps," "somewhat," "arguably." Keep hedges only when truth requires them.
- Zombie nouns: "implementation," "optimization," "utilization," "alignment" when a verb can carry the action.
- Passive evasions: "mistakes were made," "it was decided." Name the actor when the actor matters.
- Abstract fog: claims about excellence, impact, innovation, authenticity, empowerment, or transformation with no observable detail.

Keep:

- Necessary technical terms.
- Repetition that creates rhythm, emphasis, or structure.
- A long sentence when its shape helps the thought unfold.
- A plain word when a fancy word would announce insecurity.

Test every sentence with two questions:

- What am I trying to say?
- Have I said it so a new reader can follow it?

## Structure

Choose the organizing spine before line editing:

- **Argument spine**: claim, reason, evidence, objection, consequence.
- **Narrative spine**: situation, pressure, choice, result, meaning.
- **Explanatory spine**: problem, model, example, exception, application.
- **Memo spine**: recommendation, rationale, tradeoffs, next steps.
- **Profile spine**: revealing scene, context, tension, quotes, broader meaning.
- **Quest spine**: desire, search, obstacle, discovery, cost, changed understanding.
- **Decision spine**: context, options, criteria, tradeoffs, recommendation, next action.

Use transitions to express the relationship between ideas, not merely to decorate the gap. A good transition can say "because," "but," "meanwhile," "for example," "as a result," or "the real issue is."

For long pieces, write a scratch outline from the draft as it currently exists. Then write the outline the piece should have. The gap between those outlines is the developmental edit.

## Leads and Endings

Strong leads tend to:

- Begin close to the subject.
- Establish a human question, tension, image, claim, or promise.
- Avoid throat-clearing, dictionary definitions, and generic context.
- Create enough momentum that the second paragraph feels necessary.

Strong endings tend to:

- Return to the central pressure with changed understanding.
- Make the consequence clear.
- Stop after the landing sentence.
- Avoid summary phrases such as "in conclusion" unless the form requires them.

## Voice

Voice is not decoration. It is the sound of a particular mind noticing, choosing, and caring.

Preserve voice by keeping:

- The user's best verbs.
- Their natural level of formality.
- Their earned oddities of phrasing.
- Their humor, restraint, doubt, or warmth when it serves the piece.

Improve voice by removing:

- Poses of authority.
- Borrowed institutional language.
- Over-cleverness that makes the reader admire the writer instead of understand the point.
- Faux intimacy that the subject has not earned.

Voice questions:

- What does this writer notice that another writer would miss?
- Where does the prose sound borrowed from a profession, institution, trend, or template?
- Which sentence feels most alive? Use it as the tuning fork.
- Which sentence sounds like the writer left the room? Rewrite it in human language.

## Sentence Music

Read the draft aloud when rhythm matters. Watch for:

- Too many sentences with the same length.
- Sentences that bury the main verb.
- Strings of prepositional phrases.
- Multiple abstractions stacked together.
- Important words stranded in weak positions.

Revision moves:

- Put emphasis near the end of a sentence.
- Put old information before new information when explaining.
- Use short sentences after long ones for force.
- Break a sentence when the reader must carry too much.
- Combine sentences when the logic is choppy but connected.

## Diction

Prefer:

- Concrete over abstract.
- Exact over grand.
- Familiar over ornate.
- Active over evasive.
- Fresh over canned.

Do not ban large words. Ban lazy large words.

Use metaphor sparingly. A metaphor should clarify an idea or create felt recognition. If it asks the reader to admire the writer, cut it.

## AI-Default Pass

When prose "sounds like AI," diagnose the system failure instead of banning individual tokens. Most generic assistant prose shares a few visible habits:

- Stock openings that announce the category before saying anything specific.
- Smooth but weightless abstractions: impact, unlock, elevate, transform, seamless, robust, leverage.
- Symmetrical list cadence where every paragraph has the same length and pressure.
- Passive or actorless claims that hide who chose, built, shipped, failed, or changed something.
- Safe conclusions that summarize rather than land on a consequence, decision, image, or question.
- Punctuation choices used as a house tic instead of a rhythm choice.

Fix it with the ordinary prose craft stack:

1. Write the real claim in one plain sentence.
2. Replace template phrases with the specific actor, action, object, number, or scene.
3. Cut any word that exists mainly to make the sentence sound polished.
4. Use active voice when ownership matters; keep passive voice when the actor is unknown or irrelevant.
5. Swap jargon for everyday language unless the reader needs the technical term.
6. Break any local style rule when the alternative sounds false, stiff, or less clear.

Ban lists are allowed only as local evidence: if the same writer repeatedly deletes a phrase from accepted drafts, promote that phrase to a house-style warning with the reason attached.

## Evidence and Specificity

Ask:

- What can the reader see, hear, count, compare, or verify?
- Which detail carries the whole point?
- Where does the piece rely on vibe instead of evidence?
- What would a skeptical reader challenge first?

For nonfiction, do not improve drama by bending facts. If a scene, quotation, chronology, or composite is uncertain, mark it or recast it honestly.

## Paragraph Work

A paragraph should give the reader one manageable unit of thought. Revise paragraphs by asking:

- What is this paragraph doing?
- Does the first sentence orient the reader?
- Does the last sentence push the piece forward?
- Is there a buried topic sentence that should move up?
- Are two jobs fighting inside one paragraph?
- Is this paragraph only throat-clearing before the real point?

Useful paragraph moves:

- **Split** when the paragraph changes subject, time, place, speaker, or logical function.
- **Merge** when consecutive paragraphs repeat the same job.
- **Reorder** when a paragraph explains something the reader needed earlier.
- **Cut** when the next paragraph works without it.
- **Bridge** when the move is sound but the relationship is hidden.

## Revision Tactics

Use these tactics during a deep edit:

- **Bracket clutter**: Mark every word or phrase that is not doing useful work.
- **Verb audit**: Circle weak verbs and hidden actions. Replace noun-heavy phrasing with actors doing things.
- **Abstraction ladder**: For every broad claim, move one rung down toward a concrete example.
- **Skeptic pass**: Ask what a doubtful but fair reader would challenge.
- **Continuity pass**: Track names, dates, pronouns, tense, sequence, and cause.
- **Heat map**: Mark the most alive passages. Move the piece toward that energy.
- **Cold spot pass**: Mark where attention drops. Cut, compress, or add tension.
- **Read-aloud pass**: Fix stumbles, unintended repetition, weak endings, and monotone cadence.
- **Last-line test**: Try ending earlier than the draft currently ends.
- **Title promise test**: Make sure the title and opening promise the same piece the body delivers.

## Before and After (Illustrative)

These are illustrative examples written for this skill, not from the sources. Use them to calibrate what each pass does; do not attribute them to any author.

**Clutter pass**

> Before: It is important to note that, in today's fast-paced business environment, the utilization of clear communication practices can potentially serve to facilitate better outcomes for teams.
>
> After: Clear communication helps teams work better.

The edit cuts throat-clearing ("It is important to note"), stock context ("in today's fast-paced business environment"), inflators ("utilization," "facilitate"), and reflex hedges ("can potentially serve to").

**Structure pass**

> Before: We tested the new checkout flow last month. Some background: the old flow had five steps. Users complained about it in surveys. We had discussed redesigning it in Q1. The test showed a 12 percent drop in abandonment.
>
> After: Last month's test of the new checkout flow cut abandonment by 12 percent. The old five-step flow had drawn complaints in surveys, which is why we redesigned it.

The edit moves the strongest material (the result) to the front and folds the background into one subordinate sentence, instead of making the reader wade through history to reach the point.

**Lead**

> Before: Communication is one of the most important skills in the modern workplace. Many experts agree that writing well matters. In this post, I want to share some thoughts about status updates.
>
> After: Half the status updates I read this year could have been one sentence. Here is the sentence, and how to find yours.

The edit replaces generic context and an announcement of intent with a specific, mildly provocative claim that creates pressure to keep reading.

**Ending**

> Before: In conclusion, there are many factors to consider when writing status updates, and each team should decide what works best for their own unique situation and needs.
>
> After: Next Monday, before you hit send, delete everything but the one sentence your manager would repeat in her own update. That sentence is the report.

The edit removes the summary formula and the all-purpose hedge, and lands on a concrete action with a final sentence that feels finished rather than stopped.

## Common Edits by Form

**Email**

- Put the ask or answer in the first few lines.
- Use bullets for multiple actions.
- Make deadlines and owners explicit.
- Cut apologies that do not change the situation.

**Essay**

- Find the central question.
- Make the title and opening create pressure.
- Balance scene, idea, and reflection.
- Keep the ending earned rather than merely elegant.

**Op-ed**

- State the claim early.
- Name the stakes.
- Address the strongest objection.
- End with consequence or action.

**Technical explanation**

- Start with the user's goal or confusion.
- Define terms only when needed.
- Use one concrete example before abstractions multiply.
- Separate "how it works" from "how to use it."

**Business memo**

- Lead with recommendation or decision.
- Explain why now.
- Separate facts, interpretation, and asks.
- Make tradeoffs visible.

**Memoir**

- Use scene as proof, not ornament.
- Let reflection show why the memory matters now.
- Avoid explaining every feeling.
- Keep names, timelines, and composites ethically clear.

For more form-specific guidance, see [form-playbooks.md](form-playbooks.md).

## Coaching Exercises

Use one of these when the user wants to improve as a writer:

- **One-sentence promise**: Write the whole piece as one plain sentence. Revise the draft to honor it.
- **Cut 20 percent**: Remove every word that does not add meaning, rhythm, or necessary tone.
- **Actor-action pass**: Underline the actor and verb in every sentence. Rewrite sentences where the action hides inside nouns.
- **Concrete detail pass**: Add one specific detail for every broad claim.
- **Lead swap**: Write three openings: scene, claim, and question. Choose the one with the most pressure.
- **Ending test**: Delete the final paragraph. If the piece improves, the true ending was earlier.
- **Read-aloud pass**: Mark every stumble, breathless stretch, and false note. Revise those places only.
- **Imitation study**: Choose a strong paragraph by a writer the user admires. Identify its moves: sentence length, order, specificity, transitions, voice. Do not copy the wording.
- **Reader handoff**: After each paragraph, write what the reader now knows and what question pulls them onward.
- **Three cuts**: Cut the safest sentence, the prettiest sentence, and the most self-protective sentence. Restore only what the piece truly needs.
