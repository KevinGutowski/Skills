# Editing Rubric

Use this reference when the user asks for a deep critique, a scored review, multiple edit levels, or an explanation of what changed.

- Scoring Rubric

## Edit Levels

**Level 1: Clean**

- Fix grammar, punctuation, typos, and obvious awkwardness.
- Preserve structure and voice.
- Use when the draft is nearly final.

**Level 2: Clarify**

- Tighten sentences, cut clutter, improve transitions, and make claims more precise.
- Preserve the writer's argument and most paragraph order.
- Use when the draft is sound but heavy or foggy.

**Level 3: Strengthen**

- Rework lead, order, paragraphing, emphasis, rhythm, and evidence.
- Rewrite freely while preserving intent.
- Use when the piece has good material but weak shape.

**Level 4: Rebuild**

- Recast the piece from notes or a flawed draft.
- Identify the real thesis, choose a structure, and draft anew.
- Use when the original is raw material rather than a working draft.

## Scoring Rubric

Score only when useful. A 1 to 5 score works well:

- **Purpose**: Is the central promise clear?
- **Audience fit**: Does it meet the reader where they are?
- **Structure**: Does the order create momentum?
- **Evidence**: Are claims supported by detail, examples, facts, or scenes?
- **Clarity**: Can a new reader follow without strain?
- **Voice**: Does it sound human, confident, and particular?
- **Rhythm**: Does the prose move well in the ear?
- **Concison**: Is every part doing useful work?
- **Ending**: Does the piece land?
- **Trust**: Are facts, uncertainty, and tone handled honestly?

When scoring, include the two highest-leverage improvements rather than a long report card.

## Feedback Formats

**Fast critique**

```text
Biggest opportunity:
What is working:
What to cut:
What to add:
Best next move:
```

**Deep edit note**

```text
Intent I see:
Reader I infer:
Main structural issue:
Line-level pattern:
Voice to preserve:
Revision plan:
```

**Rewrite handoff**

```text
Here is a stronger version:

[rewrite]

What changed:
[3-5 bullets]

Meaning watch:
[anything that may need user confirmation]
```

**Coach mode**

```text
Craft principle:
Where it appears in your draft:
Try this:
One example:
```

## Transformation Patterns

Use these patterns to revise without flattening voice.

**Inflated to plain**

- Before pattern: "At this point in time, we are in the process of evaluating..."
- After pattern: "We are evaluating..."

**Hidden action to verb**

- Before pattern: "The implementation of the plan produced an improvement..."
- After pattern: "The plan improved..."

**Vague praise to evidence**

- Before pattern: "The product is powerful and innovative."
- After pattern: "The product cuts the weekly reporting process from three hours to ten minutes."

**Throat-clearing to direct opening**

- Before pattern: "In today's rapidly changing environment, it is important to consider..."
- After pattern: Begin with the actual change, question, or recommendation.

**Overcertain to honest**

- Before pattern: "This proves the strategy will work."
- After pattern: "This suggests the strategy can work under the same conditions."

**Summary to scene**

- Before pattern: "The meeting was tense."
- After pattern: Show the silence, interruption, changed agenda, or exact decision that made it tense.

**Generic ending to landed ending**

- Before pattern: "Only time will tell."
- After pattern: End on the consequence, choice, image, or unresolved question the piece has earned.

## Meaning Preservation

Before returning a rewrite, check whether you changed:

- Who did what.
- The degree of certainty.
- The timeline.
- The writer's stance.
- The emotional temperature.
- A technical or legal claim.
- A promise, deadline, price, number, or scope.

If meaning changed, flag it. If a claim needs verification, say so.

## Final Quality Gate

Before considering the edit done, ask:

- Can the reader say what this is about after the first paragraph?
- Does the piece start at the real beginning?
- Does every section answer a question the reader might have?
- Are the strongest nouns and verbs carrying the load?
- Is anything merely impressive-sounding?
- Does the ending leave an aftertaste?
- Does the final version still sound like the writer, only clearer?
