## Universal Nonfiction Commitments

Good nonfiction gives the reader a trustworthy guide. Across forms, protect:

- **Intention**: Know what the piece is trying to do.
- **Unity**: Keep one controlling idea, question, or journey.
- **Sequence**: Make each sentence and section feel like it follows.
- **Humanity**: Let people, stakes, and lived texture appear.
- **Specificity**: Prefer observed detail over summary judgment.
- **Compression**: Cut what the reader does not need.
- **Sound**: Prose is read by the inner ear.
- **Integrity**: Do not fake certainty, scenes, quotes, or causality.

## Essay and Personal Essay

Reader promise: "I will think with you toward a changed understanding."

Strong essay moves:

- Open with a question, pressure, image, contradiction, or claim.
- Let the essay discover, not merely announce.
- Use personal material as evidence, not as entitlement.
- Move between scene and reflection.
- Make the ending feel earned by the thinking that came before it.

Watch for:

- Anecdotes that do not deepen the idea.
- Abstract conclusions not supported by concrete moments.
- Overexplaining the meaning of every scene.
- A final paragraph that repeats the essay instead of landing it.

## Memoir

Reader promise: "I will show what happened and why it matters now."

Strong memoir moves:

- Distinguish the younger self who experienced events from the older narrator who understands them.
- Use scenes to carry evidence: gesture, setting, objects, dialogue, silence.
- Let reflection interpret consequence, not decorate memory.
- Be careful with other people's privacy and complexity.
- Admit uncertainty where memory is uncertain.

Watch for:

- Revenge disguised as reflection.
- Therapy notes that have not become art.
- Chronology with no pressure.
- Summary of feelings in place of dramatized moments.

## Profile and Interview

Reader promise: "I will reveal a person through selection, scene, context, and voice."

Strong profile moves:

- Find the tension: public image versus private behavior, ambition versus obstacle, talent versus cost.
- Use quotes as moments of character, not transcript filler.
- Surround quotes with observation and context.
- Let physical detail, setting, and choices reveal the subject.
- Keep the writer present as guide, not as rival performer.

Watch for:

- Chronological biography without shape.
- Too many quotes that say the same thing.
- Empty praise.
- Access becoming admiration.

## Travel and Place Writing

Reader promise: "I will take you through this place with curiosity, humility, and a reason to care."

Strong place-writing moves:

- Make the writer a guide, not a tourist brochure.
- Use specific sensory detail, but choose details that mean something.
- Connect place to history, people, power, language, food, work, weather, or memory.
- Let expectations meet reality.
- Avoid treating local people as scenery.

Watch for:

- Lists of sights.
- Generic wonder.
- Imported assumptions.
- Exoticizing language.
- A journey with no inner change or outer discovery.

## Science and Technical Explanation

Reader promise: "I will make a hard thing clear without lying about its complexity."

Strong explanation moves:

- Start from the reader's question or practical need.
- Define terms at the point of use.
- Use one good example before branching into exceptions.
- Separate what is known, unknown, debated, and speculative.
- Use analogy to open understanding, then name where the analogy breaks.

Watch for:

- Acronym fog.
- Expert order instead of learner order.
- Caveats before the reader understands the main idea.
- False simplicity that erases uncertainty.

## Business Writing

Reader promise: "I will help you decide or act."

Strong business moves:

- Put the recommendation, ask, risk, or decision near the top.
- Separate facts from interpretation.
- Name owners, dates, costs, tradeoffs, and next steps.
- Use bullets when parallel items must be scanned.
- Cut performance language that hides responsibility.

Watch for:

- Strategy fog.
- Passive voice that hides who must act.
- Long context before the ask.
- Polite vagueness where a decision is needed.

## Opinion and Criticism

Reader promise: "I will make a judgment and show you how I arrived there."

Strong critical moves:

- State the judgment with confidence.
- Describe the work, event, or issue accurately before judging it.
- Use evidence the reader can inspect.
- Admit the strongest counterpoint.
- Keep taste, ethics, craft, and impact distinct when needed.

Watch for:

- Fence-sitting.
- Snark replacing judgment.
- Plot summary replacing criticism.
- Claims that outpace evidence.

## Sports and Performance Writing

Reader promise: "I will show the human stakes inside competition, discipline, skill, and pressure."

Strong sports moves:

- Use the event as structure, but look beyond the scoreboard.
- Explain skill in plain language.
- Show preparation, repetition, injury, fear, teamwork, and consequence.
- Use numbers as evidence, not as a substitute for story.
- Let action verbs do the work.

Watch for:

- Cliches about grit, heart, destiny, and greatness.
- Play-by-play with no meaning.
- Hero worship.
- Abstract praise without physical detail.

## Humor

Reader promise: "I will make a serious recognition arrive through surprise."

Strong humor moves:

- Anchor exaggeration in a recognizable truth.
- Choose a comic frame: parody, mock instruction, faux memo, over-literal analysis, reversal, escalation, contrast.
- Keep control. The straighter the face, the better the joke often lands.
- Cut repeated jokes once the pattern is clear.
- Let understatement do more than exclamation.

Watch for:

- Straining for laughs.
- Explaining the joke.
- Comic names or random absurdity without pressure.
- Cruelty aimed downward.

## Speeches and Spoken Prose

Reader promise: "I will sound clear to the ear in real time."

Strong spoken moves:

- Use shorter sentences than written prose.
- Repeat key phrases deliberately.
- Put signposts where a listener cannot reread.
- Prefer concrete images and clean contrasts.
- Read aloud and cut anything the mouth resists.

Watch for:

- Dense subordinate clauses.
- Visual-only structure.
- Long lists with no rhythm.
- Endings that fade instead of resolve.
