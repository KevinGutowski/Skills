# First Impressions & First-Run Experience

What users see immediately after signup and during their first session.

## The Moment After Signup

Users just left the familiar territory of your marketing site to strike out for something new: your app. They expect to see the thing they signed up for. Get them back on solid ground inside your app as fast as possible.

## Limbo Screens

Wizards, showcases, tours, and questionnaires that appear between signup and the real app. These serve two purposes:
1. **Introduce the product** — but this should already be done by marketing
2. **Customize the experience** — this is the legit reason

### When to use limbo screens
Only when you need information that's critical for the first-run experience AND isn't likely to be changed often (or ever) after setup. Example: Netflix asking "Who will be watching?" to set up profiles.

### The Emerald City in the Distance
If you must use limbo screens, overlay them on top of the real app interface (like Quora does with topic selection). Users can see their destination while completing setup, which reduces anxiety and provides motivation.

## Coachmarks & UI Tours

**Coachmarks are NOT onboarding.** They're often a symptom that the UI itself is confusing.

If your interface needs *more interface* to explain itself, fix the interface. Tacking callouts on top of a poorly-designed UI doesn't fix it — it just points out how poorly designed it is.

Coachmarks also:
- Interrupt momentum
- Grind progress to a halt
- Must be dismissed before users can continue

### When product tours ARE useful
When they teach through action, not memorization. Good example: Optimizely guides users through *performing* the activity — "Here's how to create more variations. Try it out by clicking 'Add Variation.'" This is teaching by doing, not describing.

**Test**: Does this tour describe what a UI element does, or guide the user through actually using it?

## Welcome Messages

A brief note of encouragement goes a surprisingly long way. You want your site to be the world's best butler, not a disinterested DMV teller.

### Design exercise
Imagine yourself standing in for the website, personally helping the user:
- How would you greet them?
- What's the first thing you'd help them accomplish?
- Are there questions you'd ask right off the bat?
- What would your tone be?

Then design the site to replace yourself.

## Planning the First-Run Experience

### Define it by user improvement, not steps
- Beginning state: Who the user is when they arrive (in terms of capability/confidence)
- End state: Who they are after reaching "base camp" (their first taste of success)

### Don't mistake activity for achievement
Amazon reduced checkout from a full shopping cart flow to 1-Click Purchase. The number of steps doesn't matter — what matters is whether the user achieves their goal.

### Ruthlessly cut steps
Every step is a potential drop-off point. For each step ask:
- "Does this help them accomplish their immediate goal?"
- "Can a machine do this better?" (e.g., autosave instead of manual save)
- "Can we use a smart default?" (e.g., "My First Survey" as the default name)
- "Can we defer this until after they've experienced value?"

Example: An 8-step survey creation flow was cut to 3 steps by eliminating confirm email, sign back in, name survey, save survey, copy embed code, and paste on site — replaced with: click create, fill questions, share a ready-to-go link.

### The Quick Win (Base Camp)
The first experience should end with a small taste of success — the "ice cream spoon" that gives just enough flavor to make them want the full scoop. A good quick win:
- Demonstrates the core product essence
- Is achievable in a single sitting
- Doesn't depend on other people
- Harnesses the user's current intent

Remember the **Peak-End rule**: People judge experiences by the peak moment and the ending, not the average. Make sure the first session ends on a high note.
