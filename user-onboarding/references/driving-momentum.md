# Driving Momentum

How to keep users moving forward after their first impression — setup quests, progress indicators, encouragement, and lifecycle emails.

## Setup Quests (To-Do Lists & Progress Bars)

Break the big goal (reaching "base camp") into smaller, immediately-achievable tasks. This creates a series of "impulse buys" — trivial steps that, after completing one after another, result in the user being fully up and running.

### The Endowed Progress Effect
People complete tasks faster when they perceive they've already started. The car wash study: a 10-punch card with 2 starter punches was completed at **nearly twice the rate** of an 8-punch card with 0 punches — even though both required 8 more punches.

**Apply this**: If signup already accomplished 3 of 5 setup steps, show "3/5 complete" rather than "2 remaining." Quora does this — the first screen shows 3 of 5 items already checked off.

### Quest Design Rules
- Each quest should lead to a real accomplishment, not just activity
- Use to-do lists or completion meters to show progress
- Quests encourage self-directed exploration (unlike tours which constrain focus)
- Frame as what the user achieves, not what the product needs from them

## Celebrating Completion

**Always congratulate users when they complete something important.**

It's mind-boggling that many sites have setup processes that simply disappear when completed, with no acknowledgment.

When they complete a task, their brain is flooding with positive chemicals. Be the thing that bonds with them in that moment, not a stone wall they have to work around.

### Post-success messages
- Acknowledge the achievement
- This is the perfect time for additional recommendations ("While we have this good thing going, how about uploading a profile picture?")
- Tie it back to their improvement: "You're all set! Leads will start appearing here as visitors fill out your form."

## Positive Encouragement Copy

Cost: nothing to implement. Effect: outsized impact on emotional adoption.

**Apply at key inflection points:**
- Completing signup
- Finishing a difficult setup step
- First successful use of a core feature
- Reaching a milestone

**Remember the Peak-End rule**: Make sure journeys end on a high note. The ending moment disproportionately shapes how people remember the entire experience.

## Social Proof Inside the Product

Interpersonal motivators (faces, social proof) that drive engagement in marketing shouldn't disappear once someone's inside the product.

- Surface what other users are doing as behavioral cues
- Show successful users as examples of what new users could become ("here's one I prepared earlier!")
- Include human help availability (faces of real team members, like a landing page)

**Warning**: Don't use gamification as manipulation. People are more motivated to improve their real selves than to collect virtual badges. Using tricks to advance people through an irrelevant funnel isn't sustainable.

## Creating Regulars (Social Accountability)

One positive experience doesn't make a thriving user. The real value lies in *sustained* improvement, which requires full adoption of the behavior change.

### The Gym Buddy Effect
Social accountability drives repeat use. The more you can get users to connect with others inside your app, the stronger their commitment becomes. Think about how not wanting to let a "gym buddy" down is the only reason many people work out.

Ways to build social ties:
- Public reputation (Stack Overflow karma, contribution history)
- Shared workspaces (Dropbox shared folders, Basecamp projects)
- Activity broadcasting (RunKeeper posting completed runs, Spotify listening history)
- Team adoption makes the product more indispensable as each person joins

### The Internal Champion (B2B)
For B2B products, the first user is often the "internal champion" who needs to get their whole team on board. Help them succeed at this — provide invite flows, team onboarding materials, admin dashboards that show adoption. An assist to any part of the adoption process lifts all ships.

### Designing for Return Visits
Visits have to be meaningful ones, centered around accomplishment — not just showing up. Engineering ways to simply get people to return will eventually fail if they aren't seeing results. Act like their personal trainer: totally invested in their improvement.

## Lifecycle Emails

Connective tissue between key activities. Like training wheels — the goal is for users to eventually not need them.

### The Mount Rushmore of Lifecycle Emails

1. **Welcome email** — Triggered by signup completion. Goal: get the user to initiate a conversation or return to the app. Can be a personal welcome from the CEO or a note from support letting them know help is available.

2. **Progress email** — Triggered by a user event (e.g., first sale). Goal: build on positive momentum. "Your first customer just placed an order — congratulations!" Also powerful when triggered by someone else's action (e.g., "Someone endorsed your skill on LinkedIn").

3. **Come-back email** — Triggered by inactivity. Goal: a specific, goal-oriented return visit. The more specific the better — don't just say "we miss you."

4. **Trial expiring email** — Triggered by approaching trial end. Often feels desperate if the other emails haven't done their job. For a high-vitality user, it's a pleasant reminder of value.

### Lifecycle Email Tone
- Be helpful, not desperate
- Focus on what they can accomplish by returning, not on what you need from them
- Make each email a single, clear call to action
