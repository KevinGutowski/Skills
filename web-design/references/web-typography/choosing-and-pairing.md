## 1. Moment vs live-with (Santa Maria, ch. 4)

First sort the use, then choose:

- **Type for a moment** — "content that someone should only need a moment to read": interface copy, buttons, navigation, headlines, big marketing text. Like an airport sign — convey and release. For navigation/interface: faces that "hold up well at small sizes and aren't fussy" — JAF Facit stays legible small via "large, open counters and simple construction," where Futura's strict geometry blurs a/o and Zapfino is decoration. At display sizes almost anything works — "type acts more like an image" — but decorative faces get "a few words or less": "Like salt, country music, and in-laws, a little goes a long way" (on Bello).
- **Type to live with** — long-form text. "Every eccentricity is amplified when used page after page." Aim for even texture (blur your eyes; recurring sticking-out letterforms = wrong face). The Beatrice Warde crystal-goblet standard: "a reader shouldn't notice the type."

## 2. Three-trait evaluation (Santa Maria, ch. 4)

For extended reading, check:

1. **Sufficient x-height** — lowercase shouldn't be dwarfed by capitals (Minion, FF Meta Serif pass; Mrs. Eaves "may be too diminutive").
2. **Low or medium contrast** — Georgia fine, Bodoni distracts, Omnes "too uniform as a result of its geometry."
3. **Recognizable and distinct letterforms — the I/l/1 test**: "I shouldn't have to stop to discern if a capital I is a lowercase l or number 1" (Gill Sans fails). Matters most where context is thin: headlines, nav words, mixed numbers, code, serial numbers.

And: "don't get attached… If it doesn't serve your design, it doesn't serve the reader."

## 3. Word association (Santa Maria, ch. 4)

Instead of scrolling type catalogs asking "is this the right one?": write down the feelings the design should convey, brainstorm words, group them, then hunt typefaces expressing those traits. Worked case — Mighty (his consultancy): *stable, powerful, industrial, boxy, angular, geometric, machined, blue collar* → shortlist (Blackoak: "wide and chunky and looks like it could support a skyscraper") → final: bold condensed Proxima Nova in all caps. Blackoak lost for "Wild West baggage"; Eurostile "had the right bones but felt too open." The method's real value: "word associations give you permission to delay answering that nebulous question, 'Which typeface is right for my company?'"

## 4. Trait decomposition — the worked cases (Santa Maria, ch. 3)

Break a familiar face into attributes, then search on the attributes instead of the name.

**Helvetica** = very little stroke contrast; generous x-height; simple geometric forms; nearly closed apertures; right-angle terminals. Deduction: "clear and geometric, but not always very legible" — and culturally, "a generic default. People use it as a safe choice rather than face the fear of making a bad choice." Alternatives sharing the bones:
- **FF Dagny** — similar proportions/counters, "slightly more compact without the perpendicular terminals… more diminutive than Helvetica, and not so machined."
- **Pragmatica Slabserif** — Helvetica's attributes + serifs, "more academic."

**Georgia** (Matthew Carter, built for hostile screens) = moderate contrast; generous x-height, counters, spacing; "sharp and pointy angles, as if it were elbowing its way through a crowded room"; "beefy, almost slab-like serifs." Great for long text. Less-omnipresent kin:
- **Chaparral** — Georgia's legibility traits "with softer angles that evoke a more restrained elegance."
- **FF Tisa** — "a sure option for running text with more modern serifs and even less stroke contrast."
- **Droid Serif** — "a pointier, boxier cousin, almost as if it's sucking in a small paunch to impress someone."

"We don't need to match every trait… The important thing is to recognize the traits that make the typeface unique."

## 5. Appropriateness & history (Santa Maria, ch. 4)

"Not a matter of finding a singularly perfect typeface, but rather a matter of avoiding inappropriate ones… there are no right answers, just different degrees of appropriateness."

The worked contrast:
- **Helvetica on the NYC subway**: a 1956 Swiss typeface, in a system rebranded in the 1970s by an Italian designer, for New York — "the logical associations are scant, and I'd argue Helvetica is a bad choice," doubly so because its same-geometry letterforms hurt wayfinding.
- **Gotham at the National September 11 Memorial & Museum**: Frere-Jones's typeface "born and bred in New York City," drawn from the city's indigenous signage (Port Authority Bus Terminal) — "a far more resonant choice, one that marries a typeface's origins to a project's meaning."

How to research: foundry/designer statements (intent, inspiration, intended use); period-matching ("if you want to evoke a certain period, look for typefaces made during that time"); **Fonts In Use** for cultural associations and observed pairings.

## 6. Ready-mades, free fonts, narrowing the field (Santa Maria, ch. 4)

- **Avoid ready-mades** — "a typeface that has design baked in. These are the TV dinners of the design world." Holiday blood-drip and icicle fonts leave no room to work: "your typeface is the starting point, not the centerpiece."
- **Be careful with free fonts** — a roman takes a designer 1–2+ years (Balto: six). Common free-font failure modes: **incomplete character sets** (missing lowercase or punctuation), **less technical or aesthetic cohesion** ("the typeface feels uneven"), **a chance of being highly derivative of another font**. "Good fonts cost money because they take a lot of work to become good fonts."
- **Narrow the field**: keep a personal list of regulars; mine colophons of things you admire (A Book Apart: FF Yoga + Titling Gothic).

## 7. Skeleton / flesh / skin + the pairing matrix (Rutter, 'Learn how to describe typefaces')

Describe any typeface in three layers:
- **Skeleton** — the underlying form model: **dynamic** (calligraphic, oblique stress) → **rational** (regularised, vertical stress) → **geometric** (drawn, circle/square shapes, "contrast low to non-existent").
- **Flesh** — contrast level (contrasted ↔ linear) and serif treatment (seriffed / slab / sans).
- **Skin** — fine details: x-height first, then serif shapes, terminals, single/double-storey a, ear of the g — enough to pin a subgenre or the face itself.

Skeleton + flesh place every face on a **two-axis matrix**: dynamic→rational→geometric across, contrasted→linear down. The pairing rules:

> "All fonts that stand in one column will combine harmoniously, whereas mixing the horizontal neighbours is trickier. You can also successfully combine typefaces by going diagonally to give you a more contrasting combination. Put simply, stay in one form model or go for lots of difference; birds of a feather flock together, and opposites attract."

Method: **anchor first** (preferably the body face), then complements — "Choose typefaces that don't compete too much with each other, but aren't so similar as to be barely indistinguishable." Compare enlarged letters: x-heights, counters of e/a, bar of T, loop of g, terminals of f. Compatibility ≠ similarity (square + circle are both geometric). Serif/sans pairs work when they share a skeleton (Bressay + LFT Etica, both rational). Watch colour and apparent-size parity when mixing inline. Safest of all: superfamilies (Univers; Thesis with sans/semi-serif/serif/slab siblings; Questa), then same designer, then same foundry (TypeTogether documents its own pairings). Worked example from the book itself: Premiéra (body) + Skolar Sans (captions) harmonise as linear dynamic siblings; Ingeborg (headings, contrasted rational) works *because* it's the opposite — while still sharing the ear of the g, the flat bowl of the a, and a foundry.

## 8. The 10-genre taxonomy (Rutter, 'Type genres are more useful than classifications')

Classifications contradict each other (Antique/Grotesk/Gothic all mean different things by country); genres map style → impression → use:

| # | Genre | Form | Impression / use |
|---|-------|------|------------------|
| 1 | Humanist serif (Venetian, Garalde, Dutch-taste) | dynamic, moderate contrast, calligraphic | long/short text; friendly, rich, historical, natural |
| 2 | Transitional serif | rational, higher contrast, larger x-height | long text, newspapers; authority, charm, swagger |
| 3 | Rational serif (didone; Scotch subgenre for text) | rational, strongly modulated high contrast | headlines; elegance, fashion, expensive |
| 4 | Grotesque sans | rational, modulated low contrast, oval | headlines, short text; lively, industrial |
| 5 | Neo-grotesque sans | rational, low contrast, closed apertures | all text and signage; neutral, grids |
| 6 | Geometric sans | geometric linear, circle/square shapes | headings + longer text; modern, technical, mechanical |
| 7 | Humanist sans (Glyphic subgenre) | dynamic, open apertures, calligraphic | long text; fresh, warm, approachable / glyphic: official, ceremonial |
| 8 | Grotesque slab | rational, low contrast + heavy slabs, ball terminals | headlines; vintage, powerful, smile |
| 9 | Geometric slab | geometric linear, unbracketed slabs = stem width | headlines; industrial, friendly, cookbooks |
| 10 | Humanist slab | dynamic, low contrast + wedge/rectangular serifs | all text; professional, approachable |

## 9. Latin's worked traces (Better Web Typography, chs. 3 & 5)

**Body face for "Tech Geek"** (a tech-news site brief: modern, content-focused, heavy traffic):
1. Read real example content (TechCrunch articles, not lorem ipsum).
2. Write requirements down *before* browsing: "preferably serif… needs to feel modern which translates to vertical axis and larger x-height… large x-height, low to regular contrast… must have bold and italic styles; OpenType features like small caps and old-style figures would be a welcome addition; needs the English subset only; needs to render well on common screens."
3. Filter a catalog (Typekit, now Adobe Fonts) on those attributes → 12 → shortlist 6 (Expo Serif, Capitolium 2, Rooney, FF Meta Serif, Cordale, FF More) → 3.
4. **Review in real HTML/CSS in multiple browsers, never Photoshop/Sketch** ("Photoshop is notorious for its strange font rendering"; Firefox and Macs render heavier).
5. Eliminate with reasons: Rooney's soft roundness didn't match; FF More lacked small caps (he pairs old-style figures *with* small caps or neither). **Winner: FF Meta Serif** — matched the content, supports both features.

**Heading pairing** (ch. 5, the four-step process: find an anchor → find candidates → evaluate → eliminate/choose): candidates JAF Facit, Quatro, FF Enzo, Camingodos, Korolev against the FF Meta Serif anchor. Eliminations: Facit "doesn't feel right"; Quatro's w/y "stand out. In a bad way"; Enzo too humanist. Final two tested in role (red, then medium weight instead of bold): "Korolev literally comes to life when slightly lighter. It is now in complete contrast with the body text typeface." Camingodos fails precisely for half-heartedness — "it's too similar to the body text typeface to be truly contrasting and yet contrasting to some degree. **The thing with contrast in typography is that it needs to be either clear or nonexistent.**"

Also his prior question: "Do you really need another typeface or is one enough? Some of the best typographic work was done using a single typeface only."

## 10. Pairing rules summary

1. Anchor on the body face; decompose it (skeleton/flesh/skin or JSM's traits).
2. Same matrix column = harmony; diagonal = contrast; same row = danger.
3. Contrast must be "either clear or nonexistent" (Latin).
4. Shortcuts in order of safety: one family/superfamily → same designer → same foundry → matrix-guided stranger.
5. Always judge in context: real content, real browsers, target sizes and colours, multiple platforms.
