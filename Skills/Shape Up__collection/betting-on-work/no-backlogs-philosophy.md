# The No Backlogs Philosophy

## Why Backlogs Are a Problem

Backlogs are a big weight we don't need to carry. Dozens and eventually hundreds of tasks pile up that we all know we'll never have time for.

**The psychological burden:** The growing pile gives us a feeling like we're always behind even though we're not. Just because somebody thought some idea was important a quarter ago doesn't mean we need to keep looking at it again and again.

**The time cost:** Backlogs are big time wasters. The time spent constantly reviewing, grooming, and organizing old ideas prevents everyone from moving forward on the timely projects that really matter right now.

## What We Do Instead

Before each six-week cycle, we hold a **betting table** where stakeholders decide what to do in the next cycle. At the betting table, they look at pitches from the last six weeksâ€”or any pitches that somebody purposefully revived and lobbied for again.

Nothing else is on the table:
- No giant list of ideas to review
- No time spent grooming a backlog of old ideas
- Just a few well-shaped, risk-reduced options

With just a few options and a six-week cycle, these meetings are infrequent, short, and intensely productive.

## The Decision Flow

| Decision | What Happens |
|----------|--------------|
| **Bet on it** | It goes into the next cycle to build |
| **Don't bet** | We let it go. Nothing to track or hold on to. |

What if the pitch was great, but the timing wasn't right? Anyone who wants to advocate for it again simply tracks it independently and lobbies for it six weeks later.

## Decentralized Lists

We don't have to choose between a burdensome backlog and not remembering anything from the past.

Everyone can still track ideas independently without a central backlog:

| Who | What They Track | How |
|-----|----------------|-----|
| Support | Common requests and issues | Their own list |
| Product | Ideas to shape in future cycles | Their own notes |
| Programmers | Bugs they'd like to fix | Their own list |

None of these lists are direct inputs to the betting process. They're personal memory aids, not obligations.

## Cross-Pollinating Ideas

Regular but infrequent one-on-ones between departments help share ideas:
- Support tells Product about top issues they're seeing
- Product might track one of those as a potential project to shape
- In a future one-on-one, Support can lobby again for something that hasn't gotten attention

This approach:
- Spreads out the responsibility for prioritizing
- Makes prioritization manageable
- Keeps conversations fresh and timely

## Important Ideas Come Back

It's easy to overvalue ideas. The truth is, ideas are cheap. They come up all the time and accumulate into big piles.

**Really important ideas will come back to you.** When's the last time you forgot a really great, inspiring idea?

If it's a bug customers run into from time to time:
- It'll come back to your attention when a customer complains again
- If you hear it once and never again, maybe it wasn't really a problem
- If you keep hearing about it, you'll be motivated to shape a solution

## The Freedom of Letting Go

| With Backlogs | Without Backlogs |
|---------------|------------------|
| Always behind | Fresh start each cycle |
| Grooming meetings | Productive betting table |
| Guilt about old items | Focus on what matters now |
| Obligation to old ideas | Freedom to choose best options |

Letting go of the backlog isn't losing anything. It's gaining the freedom to focus on what's actually important right now.
